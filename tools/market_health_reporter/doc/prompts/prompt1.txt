## Analysis Procedure

Step 1 - Metric Evaluation: Evaluate each metric individually against its defined benchmark. Record specific values and timestamps where anomalies occur.

Step 2 - Cross-Metric Correlation: Identify concurrent anomalies across multiple metrics at overlapping timestamps. Concurrent deviations provide stronger evidence of manipulation than isolated anomalies.

Step 3 - Severity Assessment: Classify findings by severity:
  - STRONG evidence: Multiple metrics deviate simultaneously beyond their thresholds at overlapping timestamps.
  - MODERATE evidence: A single metric shows sustained deviation beyond its threshold.
  - WEAK/NONE: Metrics remain within normal ranges or show only brief, isolated deviations.

Step 4 - Report Construction: Begin the report with the most significant finding. If no significant anomalies are found, state this clearly and provide supporting evidence. Do not over-interpret data that falls within normal ranges.

## Metric Definitions and Anomaly Thresholds

### 1. Volume-Volatility Correlation
- API key: `vvcorrelation`
- Description: Measures the statistical relationship between trading volume and price volatility. In healthy markets, increased volume correlates with increased volatility.
- Anomaly threshold: Correlation coefficient consistently below 0.4 over extended periods.
- Interpretation: Persistently low correlation suggests artificial trading volume, since genuine trades typically move prices.

### 2. First-Digit Distribution (Benford's Law)
- API key: `firstdigitdist`
- Description: Compares the distribution of leading digits in trade data against Benford's Law, which predicts that lower digits (1, 2, 3) appear more frequently as first digits in naturally occurring datasets.
- Anomaly threshold: Significant deviation from Benford's expected distribution — particularly if higher digits (7, 8, 9) appear disproportionately often.
- Interpretation: Non-conformity may indicate fabricated or manipulated trade data such as wash trading.

### 3. Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test
- API key: `benfordlawtest`
- Related key: `tradecount` (required to compute the critical value)
- Description: A statistical test comparing the observed first-digit distribution against Benford's Law.
- How to evaluate:
  - Compute the critical value: 1.36 / sqrt(tradecount)
  - If test value > critical value → reject the null hypothesis → data does NOT conform to Benford's Law (anomalous).
  - If test value ≤ critical value → do not reject → insufficient evidence of deviation.
- Important: The K-S test is sensitive to sample size. Always report the tradecount alongside the test and critical values.

### 4. Volume Distribution
- API key: `volumedist`
- Description: A histogram showing how frequently different transaction sizes occur.
- Anomaly threshold: Distribution that significantly deviates from the power law — for example, an unusually high concentration of identically-sized trades, abnormal number of large trades, or absence of small/medium retail-sized transactions.
- Interpretation: Healthy markets follow a power law distribution: many small trades, few large trades. The histogram should be asymmetric with a steep drop-off for smaller values and a long tail. Symmetric or flat distributions suggest automated order-printing bots.

### 5. Time-of-Trade Distribution
- API key: `timeoftrade`
- Description: Aggregates trade execution times by second-of-minute or minute-of-hour (array of 60 values). Detects temporal patterns in trading activity.
- Anomaly threshold: Either (a) high-frequency spikes at specific seconds/minutes, or (b) an unusually even distribution across all 60 intervals.
- Interpretation: Both patterns suggest bot-driven activity. Human traders do not execute with clockwork regularity.

### 6. Buy/Sell Ratio
- API keys: `buysellratio` (trade count based), `buysellratioabs` (volume weighted)
- Description: The proportion of buy to sell orders in a given period. Observe this metric over time.
- Anomaly threshold: Ratio consistently outside the 0.4–0.6 range, OR unnatural steadiness (very low variance) during periods of high volatility.
- Interpretation: A balanced market fluctuates naturally within 0.4–0.6. Persistent bias or artificial stability may indicate automated systems manipulating price direction or sentiment.

### 7. Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP)
- API key: `vwap`
- Description: The average price weighted by trading volume over a specific time period.
- Anomaly threshold: Significant and sustained divergence between VWAP and actual trading price.
- Interpretation: Persistent VWAP-price divergence outside normal fluctuations may indicate price manipulation through strategic order placement.

## Cross-Metric Correlation Patterns

When multiple anomalies co-occur, they provide stronger evidence of manipulation. Look for these specific combinations:

1. Volume Distribution + Volume-Volatility Correlation: A non-power-law distribution (especially with many identical-sized trades) combined with low volume-volatility correlation strongly suggests wash trading — large artificial volumes that do not impact price.

2. First-Digit Distribution + K-S Test: Deviation from Benford's Law confirmed by K-S test rejection (test value > critical value) indicates systematic data fabrication, likely wash trading.

3. VWAP + Buy/Sell Ratio: VWAP-price divergence coupled with abnormal buy/sell ratios suggests price manipulation through coordinated buying or selling.

4. Time-of-Trade + Buy/Sell Ratio: Bot-like temporal patterns combined with abnormal or artificially stable buy/sell ratios indicate automated trading systems attempting to influence market prices or sentiment.

### Body Structure

1. **Summary section** (`## Summary`): A numbered list of 3-6 key findings. Each finding should be specific and evidence-based, citing metric names and values. If no manipulation is detected, state this clearly.

2. **Detailed analysis section** (`## Metrics used`): Organize findings under descriptive subsection headings (`### Subsection title`). Each subsection should:
   - Explain what the metric measures and why it matters.
   - Present the specific data values and timestamps that support the finding.
   - Compare observed values against the defined benchmarks.
   - State the conclusion clearly.

### Writing Guidelines
- Concentrate on data and analysis. Minimize speculation and ambiguous language.
- Support every claim with specific metric values, timestamps, and threshold comparisons.
- Use precise quantitative language (e.g., "correlation dropped to 0.12 at 2024-01-15T08:00" not "correlation was low").
- If data is inconclusive for a metric, briefly state this rather than forcing an interpretation.
