There are various indicators that are utilized to spot potential manipulative activities. These indicators, often rooted in statistical and economic theories, can be used individually or in combination to detect anomalies suggestive of market manipulation. Their importance lies in providing objective and quantifiable measures to assess market activities, which, when deviating from established norms, signal the need for closer scrutiny.

## Analysis procedure

1. Start with key metrics such as volume distribution, first-digit distribution, correlation between volume and volatility, buy-sell ratio and time-of-trade. It is crucial to observe these metrics over time. Look for periods where multiple metrics deviate simultaneously — concurrent anomalies are far more significant than isolated ones.
2. Identify anomalies using specific criteria, such as sudden spikes or significantly unusual trading volumes. The anomalous patterns and benchmarks are provided below. Analyze metrics over time to identify trends and patterns.
3. Assess the confidence level of each finding:
    - HIGH confidence: 3+ metrics show correlated anomalies in the same time window.
    - MEDIUM confidence: 2 metrics deviate simultaneously, or 1 metric deviates persistently over an extended period.
    - LOW confidence: A single metric shows an isolated deviation that could have legitimate explanations (e.g., a genuine whale trade causing a volume spike).
4. Construct a coherent and convincing report, beginning with the highest-confidence finding. Ensure the report accurately captures market abnormalities that occurred concurrently. Include specific timestamps and quantitative values as evidence.
5. If the data does not indicate any significant and clear anomalous patterns, avoid over-interpreting the datasets. If no signs of fraud or manipulation are evident, concisely report this absence and attach supporting evidence.

## Distinguishing wash trading from legitimate activity

Not every anomaly indicates manipulation. Consider these common benign explanations before concluding manipulation:
- A single large trade causing a volume spike may be a legitimate institutional order, not wash trading. Check whether the volume-volatility correlation also breaks down — real large trades typically move prices.
- Brief deviations in first-digit distribution can occur naturally in low-liquidity periods with few trades. Check `tradecount`: low sample sizes make Benford's Law unreliable.
- A buy/sell ratio outside the 0.4-0.6 range during a market-wide trend (e.g., broad selloff or rally) reflects genuine sentiment, not manipulation. Only flag ratio anomalies that diverge from what comparable markets show during the same period.

The hallmark of wash trading is sustained, multi-metric deviation that persists across time windows — not momentary spikes.

## Metric definitions

- Volume-Volatility Correlation.
    Description: A statistical measure that identifies the relationship between trading volume and market volatility. It is crucial to observe this over time.
    Name of the metric: This value is stored in the key `vvcorrelation`.
    Anomalous Pattern: A consistently low correlation (significantly below 0.4) between volume and volatility over extended periods. This suggests artificial trading volume, as real market trades typically correlate with price volatility.
    Benchmark: A correlation coefficient consistently below 0.4 can be considered suspicious.

- First-Digit Distribution.
    Description: Adherence of the first-digit distribution to Benford's Law can reveal inconsistencies in trading data. Non-conformity to the expected digit distribution could suggest manipulation, such as wash trading or massive sell-offs.
    Name of the metric: This value is stored in the key `firstdigitdist`.
    Anomalous Pattern: Significant deviation from Benford's expected distribution. The expected frequencies are: digit 1 at 30.1%, digit 2 at 17.6%, digit 3 at 12.5%, declining to digit 9 at 4.6%. If numbers begin disproportionately with higher digits (like 8 or 9) instead of following this distribution, the data warrants investigation.
    Benchmark: Normally, the empirical first-digit distribution converges with Benford's Law.

- Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test for the First-Digit Distribution.
    Description: The K-S test compares a sample with Benford's law, quantifying how closely the data conforms.
    Name of the metrics: This value is stored in the key `benfordlawtest`.
    Anomalous Pattern: A larger test value suggests a greater discrepancy between the dataset's distribution and Benford's Law.
    Benchmark: The K-S test is sensitive to sample size, stored in the key `tradecount`.
    If test value > critical value, reject the null hypothesis — the data does not conform to Benford's Law.
    If test value ≤ critical value, do not reject the null hypothesis.
    The critical value is calculated as 1.36 / sqrt(`tradecount`).
    Important: A single time window with test value exceeding the critical threshold may be noise. Flag this metric only when the K-S test value exceeds the critical value across multiple consecutive time windows.

- Volume distribution.
    Description: Shows how frequently different sizes of transactions occur within a time range.
    Name of the metric: This value is stored in the key `volumedist`.
    Anomalous Pattern: A distribution that significantly deviates from the power law, such as an unusually high number of large (whale) trades or a lack of small/medium trades.
    Benchmark: The volume distribution is expected to follow the power law — small to medium retail transactions should be frequent, while large "whale" orders are rare. The histogram should show a steep drop-off for smaller values and a long tail. A distribution that clusters around uniform trade sizes (symmetric or flat histogram) suggests bot-generated volume.

- Time-of-trade.
    Description: Analyzes trading activity distribution within each minute of an hour or each second of a minute, aggregated across all time periods into 60 buckets.
    Name of the metrics: This value is stored in the key `timeoftrade`.
    Anomalous Pattern: Two patterns suggest bot activity:
    1. High frequency of trades at fixed intervals (e.g., spikes at second 0, 15, 30, 45 suggesting a 15-second execution cycle).
    2. Nearly uniform distribution of trade counts across all seconds/minutes — organic trading is bursty, not evenly spread.
    To quantify: compute the coefficient of variation (CV = standard deviation / mean) of trade counts across the 60 buckets. CV below 0.5 suggests algorithmic uniformity. CV above 1.0 is consistent with organic, bursty trading activity.
    Benchmark: Organic trading shows irregular clustering during active periods and quiet gaps during off-hours. Perfectly even distributions are almost never natural.

- Buy/sell ratio.
    Description: The proportion of buy to sell market orders in a given time period. Observe this over time.
    Name of the metric: This value is stored in the key `buysellratio` and `buysellratioabs`.
    Anomalous Pattern: Two distinct anomaly types:
    1. Ratios that significantly deviate from the 0.4-0.6 range without a clear market-wide reason.
    2. Ratios that display unnatural steadiness during volatile periods — a buy/sell ratio confined to a narrow band (e.g., 0.48-0.52) when other metrics show high volatility suggests algorithmic control over order flow. This is especially suspicious for exchange-native tokens where the exchange has incentive to stabilize price.
    Benchmark: A balanced market should have a buy/sell ratio around 0.4-0.6.

- Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP).
    Description: The average price weighted by trading volume over a time period — more weight is given to price levels where significant volume traded.
    Name of the metric: This value is stored in the key `vwap`.
    Anomalous Pattern: A significant and consistent difference between the VWAP and the actual trading price. If reported volume is artificial (wash trading), the VWAP will be distorted because fake trades at specific price levels inflate their weight.
    Benchmark: While there is no fixed numerical benchmark, compare VWAP divergence during suspected wash trading periods against normal periods on the same market. A sudden widening of VWAP-price divergence that coincides with other anomalies strengthens the case.

## Cross-metric correlation patterns

The following simultaneous deviations, when they co-occur in the same time windows, significantly increase confidence in a manipulation finding:

1. Volume Distribution + Volume-Volatility Correlation: A volume distribution that deviates from the power law (many uniform-size trades), coupled with low volume-volatility correlation (below 0.4). This means large traded volume is not moving prices — the hallmark of wash trading where the same entity trades with itself.

2. First-Digit Distribution + K-S Test: Significant deviations from Benford's Law coupled with a K-S test value greater than the critical value across multiple time windows. This strongly indicates manufactured trade data. Wash trading generates repetitive trade sizes that violate natural digit distributions.

3. VWAP + Buy/Sell Ratio: Substantial VWAP-price divergence alongside abnormally stable buy/sell ratios. This suggests coordinated buying and selling to maintain a target price while inflating volume.

4. Time-of-Trade + Buy/Sell Ratio: Uniformly distributed trades (low CV in time-of-trade) combined with a buy/sell ratio outside the normal range suggests automated trading bots executing on fixed schedules to influence market sentiment.

5. Time-of-Trade + Volume Distribution: Uniform trade timing with uniform trade sizes is the strongest indicator of a single bot or bot cluster generating fake volume. Organic markets show variability in both dimensions.
