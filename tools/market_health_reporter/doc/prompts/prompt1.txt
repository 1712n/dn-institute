## Writing goals

- Lead with the strongest evidence, not with a generic explanation of the metrics.
- Explain why the observed metric behavior matters for market health.
- Connect related signals only when they occur in the same entity, pair, or period.
- Distinguish strong evidence from weak or inconclusive evidence.
- Keep the article readable for a technically curious audience: concise, direct, and evidence-led.

## Evidence rules

- Use only the supplied dataset. Do not add external news, background claims, links, or unprovided market context.
- Quote exact metric values, dates, timestamps, entities, and pairs when they are available.
- Do not claim wash trading or manipulation solely from one weak signal. Require either a clearly abnormal single metric or several mutually reinforcing metrics.
- If the dataset has no clear abnormal pattern, say so plainly and summarize the strongest non-anomalous evidence.
- If a metric is absent, null, malformed, or too sparse to interpret, mention the limitation only if it affects the conclusion.


Front matter requirements:
- `date` must be a single `YYYY-MM-DD` value. Use the end date of the analyzed period if the dataset contains one; otherwise use the most recent date present in the data.
- `entities` must include only venues, assets, or pairs discussed in the article.
- `description` must summarize the article conclusion without overstating certainty.

After the front matter, use this structure:

1. An opening paragraph or a `## Summary` section with the main conclusion.
2. A `## Metrics used` section.
3. Metric-specific subsections ordered from strongest to weakest evidence.
4. A short closing paragraph explaining the overall interpretation.

## Metric interpretation guide

### Volume-volatility correlation

Metric key: `vvcorrelation`.

Suspicious pattern: correlation consistently below 0.4 over an extended period. This can suggest artificial volume because genuine trading volume usually has some relationship with price volatility.

Use this metric carefully:
- A single low value is weak evidence.
- A persistent low value is stronger evidence.
- Low correlation combined with abnormal volume distribution is stronger still.

### First-digit distribution

Metric key: `firstdigitdist`.

Suspicious pattern: first digits deviate materially from Benford's Law, especially when higher leading digits such as 8 or 9 appear disproportionately.

Use this metric carefully:
- Describe which digits are over- or under-represented if the data allows it.
- Do not treat visual deviation alone as conclusive if the sample is small.

### Kolmogorov-Smirnov test for Benford's Law

Metric key: `benfordlawtest`.

Compare the K-S statistic against the critical value `1.36 / sqrt(tradecount)`.

Interpretation:
- If the K-S statistic is greater than the critical value, the sample rejects conformity with Benford's Law at the chosen significance level.
- If the K-S statistic is less than or equal to the critical value, the sample does not provide enough evidence to reject Benford conformity.

Always mention `tradecount` when using this test because the critical value depends on sample size.

### Volume distribution

Metric key: `volumedist`.

Suspicious pattern: distribution materially diverges from a power-law-like shape, including unusually many large trades, repeated identical trade sizes, or a lack of small and medium retail-sized trades.

Useful combinations:
- Abnormal volume distribution plus low `vvcorrelation` can suggest reported volume is not behaving like organic market activity.
- Repeated large trade sizes can be consistent with volume-printing or bot-driven activity.

### Time-of-trade distribution

Metric key: `timeoftrade`.

Suspicious pattern: trades cluster at repeated seconds or minutes, or are distributed with unnatural regularity across seconds or minutes.

Interpretation:
- Repeated spikes at fixed intervals can indicate scheduled bots.
- Nearly even activity across all seconds or minutes can also indicate automation.
- Mention the interval pattern when it is visible in the data.

### Buy/sell ratio

Metric keys: `buysellratio`, `buysellratioabs`.

Reference range: a balanced market is usually near 0.4-0.6.

Suspicious patterns:
- Sustained values far outside 0.4-0.6.
- Unnatural stability during periods where volume or volatility changes materially.

Use this metric carefully:
- A high ratio can indicate buy-side pressure.
- A low ratio can indicate sell-side pressure.
- A stable ratio is most suspicious when paired with other abnormal metrics.

### Volume weighted average price

Metric key: `vwap`.

Suspicious pattern: a persistent difference between VWAP and observed trading prices, especially when paired with abnormal buy/sell ratio, volume spikes, or low volume-volatility correlation.

## Combined-signal patterns

Prioritize these combinations when present:

- Abnormal volume distribution plus low volume-volatility correlation: possible artificial volume or wash trading.
- First-digit distribution deviation plus K-S statistic above the critical value: stronger evidence of non-organic numeric patterns.
- VWAP divergence plus abnormal buy/sell ratio: possible price influence or directional pressure.
- Time-of-trade regularity plus abnormal buy/sell ratio: possible automated trading influencing market sentiment.
