Write a market-health article from the metrics in the <data> block. Follow the style and Hugo front matter pattern of the <example> article, but do not copy unsupported claims from the example.

Analysis workflow:

1. Establish scope
   - Identify the market venue, trading pair, date range, and available metric keys.
   - Use the actual start and end dates from the data for the front matter date range.
   - Include only entities that are visible in the data or directly implied by the venue and pair.

2. Build an evidence table internally before writing
   - For each available metric, find the most relevant timestamps, values, local maxima/minima, threshold crossings, and repeated patterns.
   - Compare metric values to the thresholds below.
   - Prefer concurrent anomalies across multiple metrics over isolated spikes.
   - If a metric is missing, malformed, or too sparse, omit it or state that the evidence is unavailable.

3. Apply metric-specific interpretation
   - `vvcorrelation`: A sustained value below 0.4 can indicate volume that is not producing normal price volatility. Treat one-off dips as weak evidence unless they coincide with other anomalies.
   - `firstdigitdist`: Compare the leading-digit distribution against Benford's expected shape. High concentration in large first digits, or a shape unlike Benford's law, can support manipulation concerns when paired with other evidence.
   - `benfordlawtest`: Calculate the critical value as `1.36 / sqrt(tradecount)` when `tradecount` is available. If the test value is greater than the critical value, the sample rejects Benford conformity at the chosen significance level. Do not make this claim without both values.
   - `volumedist`: A normal market should have many small and medium trades with a long tail of larger trades. Unusually frequent large trades, repeated identical trade sizes, missing retail-sized trades, or a distribution that does not resemble a power law are stronger findings when trade count is also high.
   - `timeoftrade`: Repeated concentration at the same second/minute, or an unnaturally even distribution across seconds/minutes, can indicate automation. Explain which side of the pattern appears and why it matters.
   - `buysellratio` and `buysellratioabs`: A balanced market is usually near 0.4-0.6. Values persistently outside that range, or unusually stable values during volatile periods, can indicate directional pressure or automated balancing.
   - `vwap`: A sustained gap between VWAP and observed trading price can indicate price distortion. Treat the size, duration, and direction of the gap as more important than a single timestamp.

4. Cross-metric patterns to prioritize
   - Low `vvcorrelation` plus abnormal `volumedist`: possible artificial volume or wash trading.
   - Abnormal `firstdigitdist` plus `benfordlawtest` greater than its critical value: stronger evidence of fabricated or non-organic trade sizing.
   - Abnormal `buysellratio` plus VWAP divergence: possible price influence or directional pressure.
   - `timeoftrade` automation patterns plus abnormal `buysellratio`: possible automated order scheduling or sentiment shaping.
   - Multiple metrics changing at the same timestamps: stronger than any metric by itself.

5. Avoid false positives
   - Do not call activity "fraud", "wash trading", or "manipulation" unless the data shows multiple supporting signals.
   - If only one weak indicator appears, describe it as an anomaly that warrants monitoring.
   - If no clear anomaly appears, write a short article explaining that the examined data does not show strong evidence of manipulation, and include the metrics that support that conclusion.
   - Never invent outside news, exchange solvency facts, named actors, or causal explanations absent from the data.

Article requirements:

- Return only one <article>...</article> block.
- Inside the block, write a complete Markdown article.
- Start with Hugo front matter between `---` lines.
- Front matter must include:
  - `title`: concise and specific to the venue, pair, and main finding.
  - `date`: `YYYY-MM-DD - YYYY-MM-DD`.
  - `entities`: YAML list of implicated or analyzed entities.
- After front matter, use this article structure:
  - `## Summary`
  - `## Metrics used`
  - 2-5 metric sections with `###` headings, ordered from strongest to weakest evidence.
  - `## Conclusion`
- The summary should contain 3-6 numbered points.
- Each metric section should include concrete values, dates/timestamps, and a plain-English interpretation.
- Include figure placeholders only when the corresponding metric is discussed:
  - `{{< figure src="volume_hist.png" alt="volume distribution" caption="Volume distribution" loading="lazy" >}}`
  - `{{< figure src="crypto_metrics.png" alt="market metrics over time" caption="Market metrics over time" loading="lazy" >}}`
  - `{{< figure src="benford_law.png" alt="first digit distribution" caption="First digit distribution compared with Benford's law" loading="lazy" >}}`
  - `{{< figure src="vv_correlation.png" alt="volume volatility correlation" caption="Volume-volatility correlation" loading="lazy" >}}`
- Keep the article concise. Prefer direct evidence over generic metric definitions.
- Do not include analysis notes, hidden chain-of-thought, or content outside the <article> tags.
