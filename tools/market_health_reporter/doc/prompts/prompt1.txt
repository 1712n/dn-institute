## Analysis Framework

### Step 1: Metric Assessment
Evaluate each metric against its benchmark. Flag any that exceed anomaly thresholds.

### Step 2: Cross-Metric Correlation
Determine whether flagged anomalies co-occur temporally, which strengthens manipulation signals.

### Step 3: Report Construction
Lead with the most significant deviation. Build a narrative connecting related anomalies.

### Step 4: Honest Conclusion
If no clear manipulation is found, state this directly with supporting evidence.

## Metric Definitions and Anomaly Thresholds

### Volume-Volatility Correlation (`vvcorrelation`)
- **What it measures**: Statistical relationship between trading volume and price volatility.
- **Why it matters**: Genuine trading volume typically drives price movement. Artificial (wash) trading inflates volume without proportional volatility impact.
- **Anomaly threshold**: Correlation coefficient consistently below **0.4** over extended periods suggests artificial volume.
- **Normal range**: 0.4–1.0 (varies by market conditions, but sustained low values are suspicious).

### First-Digit Distribution (`firstdigitdist`)
- **What it measures**: Whether leading digits of trade sizes conform to Benford's Law.
- **Why it matters**: Naturally occurring financial data follows a predictable first-digit distribution. Manipulated data (e.g., repeated wash trades of similar sizes) deviates from this pattern.
- **Anomaly threshold**: Significant over-representation of higher digits (6–9) as leading digits, or disproportionate concentration on any single digit.
- **Normal range**: Empirical distribution converges with Benford's Law expectations (digit 1 ≈ 30.1%, digit 2 ≈ 17.6%, etc.).

### Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test (`kstest`)
- **What it measures**: Statistical distance between the observed first-digit distribution and the Benford's Law expected distribution.
- **Why it matters**: Provides a single quantitative measure of how far trading data deviates from expected natural patterns.
- **Anomaly threshold**: K-S statistic exceeding **0.15** indicates significant deviation.
- **Normal range**: K-S statistic below 0.10 with p-value > 0.05 indicates conformity with Benford's Law.

### Buy-Sell Ratio (`buysellratio`)
- **What it measures**: Proportion of buy orders to sell orders in a given time period.
- **Why it matters**: Sustained extreme imbalances without corresponding price movement may indicate spoofing or layering.
- **Anomaly threshold**: Ratio consistently above **1.5** or below **0.67** (i.e., 3:2 imbalance) without proportional price change.
- **Normal range**: 0.8–1.2 during stable markets; wider swings acceptable during high-impact news events.

### Trade Size Distribution (`tradesizedist`)
- **What it measures**: Distribution of individual trade sizes over a time window.
- **Why it matters**: Wash trading often produces clusters of identical or near-identical trade sizes. Natural markets show varied trade sizes.
- **Anomaly threshold**: High concentration (>30%) of trades at a single size or narrow size band.
- **Normal range**: Diverse distribution with no single size dominating more than 15% of trades.

### Time-of-Trade Distribution (`timeoftrade`)
- **What it measures**: How trades are distributed across hours of the day.
- **Why it matters**: Legitimate trading follows geographic market hours. Automated wash trading may produce unnaturally uniform distribution or suspicious off-hours spikes.
- **Anomaly threshold**: Abnormally uniform distribution across all 24 hours, or sudden volume spikes during historically low-activity periods without news catalysts.
- **Normal range**: Higher activity during major market hours (US, EU, Asia sessions) with natural dips in between.

## Report Format

Structure your analysis as follows:

1. **Title**: `[Exchange Name] - Market Health Report [Date Range]`
2. **Summary**: 2-3 sentence overview of findings (manipulation detected / not detected, severity level)
3. **Key Findings**: Bullet points of the most significant metric deviations with specific values
4. **Detailed Analysis**: For each anomalous metric:
   - Observed value vs. benchmark
   - Time period of anomaly
   - Potential interpretation
   - Supporting/contradicting evidence from other metrics
5. **Conclusion**: Overall assessment with confidence level (high/medium/low)

Use precise numbers, timestamps, and metric names throughout. Avoid speculation beyond what the data supports.
