🌰 ANALYSIS PROTOCOL — Complete the following internal reasoning steps before writing any article.

## 🌰 Step 1 — Metric Audit
For each metric, record: (a) observed value, (b) comparison to anomaly threshold, (c) severity level.
Use these severity levels consistently:
- NORMAL — within expected range, no action needed
- CAUTION — mild deviation, note but do not lead with
- SUSPICIOUS — significant deviation, include as supporting evidence
- ANOMALOUS — clear breach of threshold, lead the analysis with this metric

## 🌰 Step 2 — False-Positive Exclusion
Before concluding manipulation, rule out benign explanations for each flagged metric:
- Could the anomaly be caused by a major market event, news release, or exchange outage?
- Is the sample size (tradecount) sufficient for reliable statistical conclusions?
- Are the anomalies isolated to one metric or correlated across multiple metrics?

Isolated single-metric deviations are weak signals. Clustered multi-metric anomalies are strong signals.

## 🌰 Step 3 — Confidence Score
Assign a manipulation probability score (0-100) before writing the article:
- 80-100: CRITICAL — strong cross-metric evidence; report as probable manipulation
- 60-79: HIGH — multiple corroborating signals; report as suspicious activity
- 40-59: MODERATE — some anomalies present; flag for monitoring
- 20-39: LOW — minor deviations; report as within normal range with caveats
- 0-19: CLEAN — no manipulation signals; concisely report absence of anomalies

## 🌰 Metric Reference Guide

### 🌰 Volume-Volatility Correlation
- **Metric key:** `vvcorrelation`
- **Description:** Measures the statistical relationship between trading volume and price volatility. In healthy markets, large volume should correspond to increased volatility.
- **Thresholds:**
  - NORMAL: correlation >= 0.4
  - CAUTION: 0.25 <= correlation < 0.4
  - SUSPICIOUS: 0.1 <= correlation < 0.25
  - ANOMALOUS: correlation < 0.1 (sustained over multiple periods)
- **False-positive check:** Low correlation during periods of thin liquidity or post-event consolidation may be benign.

### 🌰 First-Digit Distribution (Benford's Law)
- **Metric key:** `firstdigitdist`
- **Description:** Natural trade volume distributions follow Benford's Law, where lower leading digits (1, 2, 3) appear more frequently than higher digits (7, 8, 9). Artificial trading distorts this pattern.
- **Thresholds:**
  - NORMAL: distribution converges with Benford's expected frequencies
  - SUSPICIOUS: moderate deviation, especially excess frequency of digits 5-9
  - ANOMALOUS: significant overrepresentation of high digits (8, 9) or near-uniform distribution
- **False-positive check:** Low tradecount (stored in `tradecount`) reduces statistical reliability — always pair with the K-S Test result.

### 🌰 Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test
- **Metric key:** `benfordlawtest`
- **Description:** Tests whether the observed first-digit distribution statistically conforms to Benford's Law.
- **Interpretation:**
  - Critical value = 1.36 divided by the square root of `tradecount`
  - If `benfordlawtest` > critical value: reject null hypothesis — data does not conform to Benford's Law (ANOMALOUS)
  - If `benfordlawtest` <= critical value: do not reject — insufficient evidence of deviation (NORMAL)
- **False-positive check:** Small sample sizes inflate the test statistic. Require `tradecount` >= 1000 before treating as strong evidence.

### 🌰 Volume Distribution
- **Metric key:** `volumedist`
- **Description:** Healthy markets show a power-law distribution — many small/medium retail trades, very few large (whale) orders. Artificial trading often creates an unusually large proportion of same-sized or large orders.
- **Thresholds:**
  - NORMAL: right-skewed distribution with steep drop-off for large trade sizes
  - SUSPICIOUS: elevated frequency of mid-range or large-sized trades relative to small trades
  - ANOMALOUS: near-uniform distribution, or significant spike in a narrow trade-size band suggesting programmatic trading
- **False-positive check:** Institutional accumulation periods may legitimately produce more large-order activity.

### 🌰 Time-of-Trade
- **Metric key:** `timeoftrade`
- **Description:** Aggregates trade execution frequency across 60 time slots (seconds within a minute, or minutes within an hour). Human trading is irregular; bot trading shows regular spikes or unnaturally even distributions.
- **Anomalous patterns (flag either):**
  - Spike pattern: One or a few time slots carry disproportionately more trades (automated execution at fixed intervals)
  - Even distribution: Trade counts are near-uniform across all 60 slots (algorithmic noise generation)
- **False-positive check:** High-frequency trading (HFT) is legal and common; flag only when combined with other manipulation signals.

### 🌰 Buy/Sell Ratio
- **Metric keys:** `buysellratio`, `buysellratioabs`
- **Description:** The proportion of buy-initiated versus sell-initiated orders. Balanced markets hover around 0.5.
- **Thresholds:**
  - NORMAL: 0.4-0.6 range with natural fluctuations
  - CAUTION: 0.3-0.4 or 0.6-0.7, short-term directional bias
  - SUSPICIOUS: below 0.3 or above 0.7, sustained over multiple periods
  - ANOMALOUS: artificially steady ratio during high-volatility periods (locked ratio while price moves significantly)
- **False-positive check:** Strong trending markets legitimately produce skewed buy/sell ratios. Cross-check with vvcorrelation.

### 🌰 Volume Weighted Average Price (VWAP)
- **Metric key:** `vwap`
- **Description:** The volume-weighted average price reflects where the majority of trading activity occurred. Large divergence between VWAP and market price suggests trades are executed at artificial prices.
- **Thresholds:**
  - NORMAL: VWAP tracks actual price within typical spread
  - SUSPICIOUS: consistent VWAP divergence outside normal trading fluctuations, especially when correlated with abnormal buy/sell ratios
  - ANOMALOUS: sustained, directional VWAP divergence while volume-volatility correlation is low
- **False-positive check:** Volatile market sessions (news releases, large liquidation events) can produce short-term VWAP divergence.

## 🌰 Step 4 — Cross-Metric Synthesis

Use these known manipulation signatures when metrics co-deviate:

🌰 Wash Trading Signal: `volumedist` deviates from power law + `vvcorrelation` below 0.25 + `benfordlawtest` exceeds critical value. Large artificial volumes fail to move price, and digit patterns are distorted.

🌰 Bot Activity Signal: `timeoftrade` shows spike or even distribution + `buysellratio` is artificially steady during volatility periods. Automated systems execute orders at precise intervals and maintain controlled ratios.

🌰 Price Manipulation Signal: `vwap` diverges from market price consistently + `buysellratio` is skewed. Traders artificially shift the perceived average price through one-sided order flow.

🌰 Benford Confirmation: `firstdigitdist` shows excess high-digit frequency + `benfordlawtest` > critical value + sufficient `tradecount`. This combination is statistically strong evidence of artificial volume generation.

## 🌰 Step 5 — Article Generation

Create an article containing your analysis. Requirements:

### 🌰 Frontmatter (between --- and ---)
Place the following fields at the top of the article:
- 'date': format YYYY-MM-DD — YYYY-MM-DD (start date to end date of analysis period). Example: '2023-10-02 — 2023-10-08'
- 'entities': entities implicated in wash trading. Example: 'Huobi, HT, TRX, DOGE'
- 'title': descriptive article title. Example: 'Uncovering Wash Trading and Market Manipulation on Huobi'

### 🌰 Article Structure
- Lead with the strongest anomaly — the metric with the highest severity and best cross-metric corroboration.
- Support with corroborating metrics — list additional deviations as reinforcing evidence.
- Include specific values — always cite the actual metric value alongside the threshold. Example: "vvcorrelation = 0.08, well below the 0.25 suspicious threshold."
- Include timestamps — reference specific time periods where anomalies were most pronounced.
- Conclude with confidence assessment — state your confidence score and overall finding.
- Clean market case — if no significant anomalies are detected, briefly report this with supporting metric values. Do not over-interpret noisy data.
