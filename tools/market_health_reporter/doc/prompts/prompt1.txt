PHASE 1: INDIVIDUAL METRIC EVALUATION
==========================================================

Evaluate each of the following metrics from the provided data. For each metric, extract the relevant values, compare them against the benchmark, and classify the finding as NORMAL, MODERATE CONCERN, or STRONG ANOMALY.

1. Volume-Volatility Correlation (key: `vvcorrelation`)
   - What it measures: The statistical relationship between trading volume and price volatility over time. In healthy markets, higher volume typically coincides with higher volatility because genuine trades move prices.
   - Benchmark: Correlation coefficient generally above 0.4.
   - NORMAL: Correlation consistently above 0.4 across the analysis period.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Correlation intermittently dropping below 0.4, or averaging between 0.2 and 0.4.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Correlation consistently below 0.2 over extended periods. This suggests artificial volume that does not impact price, a hallmark of wash trading.
   - Analysis steps: Calculate the average, minimum, and maximum correlation values across all data points. Identify any time windows where the correlation drops sharply. Note the duration and magnitude of any low-correlation periods.

2. First-Digit Distribution (key: `firstdigitdist`)
   - What it measures: Whether the leading digits of trading data conform to Benford's Law. Genuine financial data tends to follow this natural distribution, where lower digits (1, 2, 3) appear more frequently as leading digits than higher ones (7, 8, 9).
   - Expected distribution (Benford's Law): digit 1 ~30.1%, digit 2 ~17.6%, digit 3 ~12.5%, digit 4 ~9.7%, digit 5 ~7.9%, digit 6 ~6.7%, digit 7 ~5.8%, digit 8 ~5.1%, digit 9 ~4.6%.
   - NORMAL: Distribution closely tracks Benford's Law expectations.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Noticeable deviations for one or two digits (e.g., digit 1 frequency below 25% or above 35%).
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Systematic deviation, especially overrepresentation of higher digits (6-9) or underrepresentation of digit 1. Such patterns suggest manufactured or algorithmically generated trade data.
   - Analysis steps: Compare the observed frequency of each leading digit against Benford's expected values. Quantify the deviation for each digit. Identify which digits deviate most and by how much.

3. Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Test for Benford's Law (key: `benfordlawtest`)
   - What it measures: A formal statistical test comparing the observed first-digit distribution against Benford's Law. This provides a rigorous, quantitative assessment alongside the visual first-digit distribution analysis.
   - How to evaluate: You MUST compute the critical value before making a determination.
     * Critical value = 1.36 / sqrt(tradecount), where `tradecount` is stored in the data.
     * If test value > critical value: REJECT the null hypothesis. The data does NOT conform to Benford's Law at the chosen significance level, indicating potential manipulation.
     * If test value <= critical value: FAIL TO REJECT the null hypothesis. There is insufficient evidence that the data deviates from Benford's Law.
   - NORMAL: Test value <= critical value across most time periods.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Test value exceeds critical value in some but not all time periods.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Test value consistently and substantially exceeds critical value, especially when combined with visible deviations in the first-digit distribution.
   - Analysis steps: For each data point, extract `benfordlawtest` and `tradecount`. Compute the critical value as 1.36 / sqrt(tradecount). Compare the test value to the critical value. Report the percentage of time periods where the null hypothesis is rejected.

4. Volume Distribution (key: `volumedist`)
   - What it measures: The frequency distribution of transaction sizes. Healthy markets exhibit a power-law distribution where small and medium retail transactions dominate, with large "whale" trades being rare.
   - Benchmark: Power-law distribution — a histogram that shows high frequency of small transactions with a long tail of decreasing frequency for larger transaction sizes. The histogram should be asymmetric with a steep initial drop-off.
   - NORMAL: Distribution consistent with power-law shape.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Slight clustering at specific transaction sizes, or a somewhat flatter distribution than expected.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Significant deviation from power-law distribution, such as an unusually high number of large trades, clustering around specific round numbers, or absence of small/medium trades. These patterns can indicate coordinated wash trading.
   - Analysis steps: Examine the shape of the volume distribution. Look for unusual clustering, gaps, or peaks at specific transaction sizes. Check whether the distribution follows the expected power-law pattern.

5. Time-of-Trade (key: `timeoftrade`)
   - What it measures: The distribution of trade execution times within each minute (second-level granularity, 0-59). It aggregates when trades occur at the sub-minute level across the entire analysis period.
   - Benchmark: Human trading produces somewhat irregular, non-uniform distributions. No specific numeric benchmark exists, but patterns should reflect natural human variability.
   - NORMAL: Irregular distribution with natural-looking variation across seconds.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Mild clustering at specific seconds, or slightly more uniform than expected.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Either (a) extremely uniform distribution across all seconds (suggesting algorithmic time-spreading), or (b) sharp spikes at specific seconds (suggesting bot-driven execution at fixed intervals). Both patterns indicate automated trading systems.
   - Analysis steps: Examine the trade count distribution across seconds 0-59. Calculate the coefficient of variation. Look for periodic patterns (e.g., trades clustering every 10 seconds or every 15 seconds).

6. Buy/Sell Ratio (key: `buysellratio`, also check `buysellratioabs`)
   - What it measures: The proportion of buy versus sell market orders in each time period. This ratio reflects market sentiment and order flow dynamics.
   - Benchmark: Balanced markets typically show ratios in the 0.4-0.6 range.
   - NORMAL: Ratio fluctuates naturally within or near the 0.4-0.6 range, with some variation reflecting genuine market sentiment shifts.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: Ratio frequently outside 0.4-0.6, or unusually stable (not fluctuating) during periods of high volatility.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Ratio persistently extreme (above 0.7 or below 0.3), or unnaturally constant (near-zero variance) during volatile periods. Unnatural steadiness during volatility suggests algorithmic order flow designed to maintain artificial market conditions.
   - Analysis steps: Track the buy/sell ratio over time. Calculate the mean, standard deviation, and range. Identify periods where the ratio is extreme or unusually stable. Cross-reference with volume and volatility data to check whether ratio stability during volatile periods is suspicious.

7. Volume Weighted Average Price — VWAP (key: `vwap`)
   - What it measures: The average price weighted by trading volume. VWAP gives more weight to price levels where significant trading activity occurred, making it a key benchmark for whether trades are executing at fair prices.
   - Benchmark: VWAP should track close to the market price. No fixed numeric threshold, but persistent large deviations warrant attention.
   - NORMAL: VWAP closely tracks the trading price with minor, expected fluctuations.
   - MODERATE CONCERN: VWAP intermittently diverges from trading price by more than typical fluctuation ranges.
   - STRONG ANOMALY: Consistent and significant divergence between VWAP and actual trading price, suggesting that large volumes are being traded at off-market prices, potentially to manipulate the perceived average price.
   - Analysis steps: Compare VWAP values against price data over time. Identify periods of divergence and quantify the gap. Note whether divergences coincide with unusual volume or other anomalous metrics.

PHASE 2: CROSS-METRIC CORRELATION ANALYSIS
==========================================================

After evaluating each metric individually, analyze these specific cross-metric patterns that are strong indicators of manipulation when they co-occur:

A. Volume Distribution + Volume-Volatility Correlation
   If the volume distribution deviates from power-law (unusual number of large trades) AND volume-volatility correlation is low, this is a strong signal of artificial volume. Large genuine trades should move prices — if they don't, the volume may be fabricated.

B. First-Digit Distribution + K-S Test
   If first-digit distribution visibly deviates from Benford's Law AND the K-S test rejects the null hypothesis, this provides strong statistical evidence of data manipulation, potentially wash trading where large volumes of artificial trades are generated.

C. VWAP Divergence + Buy/Sell Ratio Anomalies
   If VWAP significantly diverges from market prices AND the buy/sell ratio is abnormal, this suggests coordinated price manipulation through strategic one-sided order flow.

D. Time-of-Trade Patterns + Buy/Sell Ratio Stability
   If time-of-trade shows bot-like patterns (uniform distribution or periodic spikes) AND the buy/sell ratio is unnaturally stable, this strongly suggests automated trading systems are operating, potentially for market manipulation purposes.

E. Volume Spikes + Multiple Metric Deviations
   If sudden volume spikes coincide with deviations in multiple other metrics (K-S test failure, unusual buy/sell ratio, low volume-volatility correlation), this cluster of simultaneous anomalies provides the strongest evidence of manipulative activity.
