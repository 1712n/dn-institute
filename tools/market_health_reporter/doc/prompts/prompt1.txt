Analyze the provided market data using the indicators below. Work through them systematically, but lead your report with whichever metric shows the strongest signal.

## Indicators

### Volume-Volatility Correlation (`vvcorrelation`)
Measures how trading volume tracks with price movement. In healthy markets, higher volume generally comes with higher volatility.
- **Red flag:** Correlation staying below 0.4 for extended periods. This can mean volume is being generated without real price discovery — a hallmark of wash trading.
- **Context:** Brief dips below 0.4 during low-activity periods aren't necessarily suspicious. Look for persistence.

### First-Digit Distribution (`firstdigitdist`)
Checks whether leading digits in trade data follow Benford's Law, where smaller digits (1, 2, 3) appear more frequently than larger ones.
- **Red flag:** Overrepresentation of digits like 7, 8, or 9 in leading positions. Round-number clustering (lots of trades at exactly 100, 500, 1000) is also worth noting.
- **Context:** Small sample sizes naturally deviate from Benford's. Check trade count before drawing conclusions.

### Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test (`benfordlawtest`)
Statistical test comparing your data's digit distribution against Benford's Law.
- **How to read it:** Calculate the critical value as 1.36 / sqrt(`tradecount`). If the test statistic exceeds this, the data significantly deviates from Benford's at the chosen confidence level.
- **Red flag:** Test value > critical value, especially when combined with visual anomalies in the digit distribution.
- **Context:** A failed K-S test alone isn't proof of manipulation — it's a reason to dig deeper.

### Volume Distribution (`volumedist`)
Shows how trade sizes are distributed. Normal markets follow a rough power law: many small trades, few large ones.
- **Red flag:** Unusual clustering of same-size trades, or a flat distribution where large and small trades appear with similar frequency. This can indicate automated wash trading with fixed lot sizes.
- **Context:** Some assets naturally have chunkier volume profiles (e.g., institutional-heavy pairs).

### Time-of-Trade (`timeoftrade`)
Aggregates when trades happen within each minute (second-level) or hour (minute-level), collapsing across all periods.
- **Red flag:** Sharp spikes at specific seconds/minutes (e.g., trades consistently firing at :00 and :30), or an unnaturally even spread across all intervals. Both suggest bot activity.
- **Context:** Some regularity is normal near market opens or during scheduled events.

### Buy/Sell Ratio (`buysellratio`, `buysellratioabs`)
The proportion of buy vs. sell market orders.
- **Red flag:** Ratios persistently outside the 0.4–0.6 range, or a ratio that stays suspiciously flat during volatile periods. Real markets show fluctuation; artificial ones often don't.
- **Context:** Directional bias (trending markets) can push ratios outside this range legitimately. Look at whether the ratio *responds* to price movement.

### VWAP (`vwap`)
Volume-weighted average price. Shows where most trading activity is concentrated price-wise.
- **Red flag:** Persistent gaps between VWAP and spot price, especially if trades cluster around VWAP without natural price discovery around it.
- **Context:** VWAP is most useful as a cross-reference. Anomalies here gain meaning when paired with other signals.

## Cross-referencing signals

Single-indicator anomalies are worth noting but rarely conclusive. Look for convergence:

- **Volume distribution + VV correlation:** Uniform trade sizes with low volume-volatility correlation strongly suggest wash trading — volume is being manufactured without market impact.
- **Benford's deviation + K-S test failure:** Both pointing the same way means the statistical case for data irregularity is solid.
- **VWAP gaps + abnormal buy/sell ratio:** Price anchoring attempts often show up as VWAP divergence with one-sided order flow.
- **Time-of-trade patterns + buy/sell stability:** Bot-driven trading tends to show both clockwork timing and unnatural ratio consistency.
