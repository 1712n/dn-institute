## Summary

1. **$4 billion stolen through a fake cryptocurrency**: OneCoin, founded in 2014 by Ruja Ignatova and Karl Sebastian Greenwood, defrauded approximately 3.5 million victims across 175+ countries by selling a fabricated cryptocurrency that had no blockchain — the entire system ran on a Microsoft SQL Server database where coin generation was scripted and prices were set internally.
2. **Founder disappeared and remains FBI Most Wanted**: Ruja Ignatova — "The Cryptoqueen" — boarded a Ryanair flight from Sofia to Athens on October 25, 2017 and has not been seen since, coinciding with the filing of a secret U.S. arrest warrant. She was added to the FBI Ten Most Wanted Fugitives list in June 2022, with a $5 million reward offered by the U.S. State Department in June 2024.
3. **Co-founder sentenced to 20 years**: Karl Sebastian Greenwood, OneCoin's co-founder and global master distributor who earned 5% of all monthly sales, was arrested in Thailand in July 2018, extradited to the U.S., and sentenced to 20 years in federal prison on September 12, 2023.
4. **No real blockchain existed**: A Norwegian blockchain developer publicly exposed that OneCoin used a SQL database script to generate coins rather than any distributed ledger technology — the "cryptocurrency" had no mining, no independent verification, and prices were manually set by the company from EUR 0.50 to EUR 29.95.
5. **Multi-level marketing across 175+ countries**: OneCoin was sold through an aggressive MLM network where investors purchased "educational packages" costing EUR 100 to EUR 118,000, received tokens to "mine" non-existent coins, and earned commissions by recruiting new investors into a pyramid structure spanning 175+ countries.

## Background

### Founders and Corporate Structure

OneCoin was founded in **late 2014** by **Ruja Ignatova** and **Karl Sebastian Greenwood**. The corporate structure was deliberately fragmented across multiple jurisdictions [1]:

- **OneCoin Ltd**: Registered in Dubai
- **OneLife Network Ltd**: Registered in Belize
- **Operational headquarters**: Sofia, Bulgaria
- **Subsidiary entities** in multiple countries

**Ruja Ignatova** held a doctorate in private international law from the University of Konstanz (2005) and a Master's degree in European law from Oxford University. She had previously worked as a consultant at **McKinsey & Company** in Sofia. Notably, she was convicted in Germany in **2012** for bankruptcy fraud and was involved with **BigCoin**, a multi-level marketing fraud, in 2013.

**Karl Sebastian Greenwood**, a UK and Swedish citizen, served as co-founder and "global master distributor," earning **5% of OneCoin's monthly global sales**.

OneCoin was marketed as a "Bitcoin killer" and claimed to have over **3.5 million members** by 2016.

### The Fake Blockchain

OneCoin claimed to operate a private blockchain with a mined cryptocurrency. In reality [2]:

- The system used a **Microsoft SQL Server database** where coin generation was scripted
- **No distributed ledger** existed — just a centralized database on company servers
- **No actual mining** occurred — coins were generated by a SQL script
- **No independent verification** was possible — the company controlled all balances, supply, and prices
- **Prices were fabricated** internally, rising from EUR 0.50 to EUR 29.95 in a predetermined schedule

A **Norwegian blockchain developer** publicly exposed that OneCoin was using a SQL database script to generate coins. OneCoin threatened to sue the whistleblower.

By approximately **March 2015**, Ignatova and Greenwood had begun allocating "fake coins" — coins that did not even exist in OneCoin's purported private blockchain.

### The MLM Structure

OneCoin was distributed through a multi-level marketing (pyramid) network [3]:

- **"Educational packages"**: Investors purchased packages ranging from EUR 100 to EUR 118,000, purportedly containing cryptocurrency trading educational materials (much of which was plagiarized from free sources including Wikipedia)
- **Tokens**: Each package included "tokens" for "mining" OneCoins — no actual mining occurred
- **Recruitment commissions**: Members earned commissions by recruiting new investors
- **xcoinx exchange**: The only exchange where OneCoins could be traded was xcoinx, secretly owned by OneCoin itself, with daily selling limits that trapped investors' money
- **Dealshaker**: A marketplace launched as an "Amazon killer" where holders could supposedly spend OneCoins

**China** arrested over **90 individuals** involved in promoting OneCoin and seized **$30.8 million** in assets.

## The Disappearance (October 25, 2017)

On **October 25, 2017**, Ruja Ignatova boarded a **Ryanair flight from Sofia, Bulgaria, to Athens, Greece**. She has not been seen publicly since. Her disappearance coincided with the filing of a **secret U.S. arrest warrant** [5].

After Ignatova's disappearance, her brother **Konstantin Ignatov** assumed leadership of OneCoin operations.

### Murder Theory

In **February 2023**, the investigative journalism project **BIRD**, citing Bulgarian police documents, reported that Ignatova may have been **murdered in 2018** aboard a yacht in the Ionian Sea on the orders of **Hristoforos Nikos Amanatidis ("Taki")**, a Bulgarian organized crime figure who initially sheltered her after her disappearance. Her body was allegedly dismembered and thrown overboard. However, her body has never been found, sightings continue to be reported, and the FBI maintains her on its Most Wanted list [6].

## Legal Consequences

### Ruja Ignatova (Founder — Fugitive)

- **Charges**: Wire fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, securities fraud, and conspiracy to commit money laundering (each carrying up to 20 years)
- **June 2022**: Added to **FBI Ten Most Wanted Fugitives** list — the only woman on the list at that time
- **June 2024**: U.S. State Department increased reward to **$5 million** for information leading to arrest
- **August 2024**: London High Court issued global freezing order on her assets in a class action by 400+ investors
- **Status**: Remains a fugitive

### Karl Sebastian Greenwood (Co-Founder)

- **July 2018**: Arrested at his home in **Koh Samui, Thailand**, in a joint FBI-Thai operation; Thai authorities seized 3.3 kg of gold bullion
- **October 2018**: Extradited to the United States
- **December 2022**: Pleaded guilty to wire fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, and conspiracy to launder money
- **September 12, 2023**: Sentenced to **20 years in federal prison** (with 5 years credit for time served) by Judge Edgardo Ramos (SDNY)
- **Forfeiture**: Ordered to forfeit approximately **$300 million** [7]

### Konstantin Ignatov (Ruja's Brother)

- **March 6, 2019**: Arrested at **Los Angeles International Airport** on wire fraud conspiracy charges
- **October 4, 2019**: Pleaded guilty to multiple charges (faced up to 90 years)
- Became a **cooperating witness** for prosecutors; testified against Mark Scott
- **March 5, 2024**: Sentenced to **time served** (34 months); released same day [8]

### Mark S. Scott (Lawyer / Money Launderer)

- Former partner at major U.S. law firm **Locke Lord LLP**
- Created fake private equity investment funds in the British Virgin Islands ("Fenero Funds") to launder over **$400 million** in OneCoin proceeds through accounts in the Cayman Islands and Ireland
- Made more than **$50 million** personally
- **November 2019**: Convicted on bank fraud and money laundering charges
- **2024**: Sentenced to **10 years in federal prison**
- **February 2025**: Federal appeals court upheld conviction and sentence
- Disbarred in New York [9]

### Irina Dilkinska (Head of Legal and Compliance)

- Arranged transfer of **$110 million** in OneCoin proceeds to a Cayman Islands entity
- **November 2023**: Pleaded guilty to wire fraud and money laundering conspiracies
- **April 3, 2024**: Sentenced to **4 years in prison**; ordered to forfeit **$111.4 million** [10]

The BBC investigative podcast **"The Missing Cryptoqueen"**, presented by Jamie Bartlett and premiering **September 17, 2019**, brought massive public attention to the OneCoin fraud. The series (18 episodes through 2024) investigated the mechanics of the scheme, the human toll on victims, the search for Ignatova, and the murder allegations [11].

## Market Manipulation Implications

The OneCoin fraud reveals fundamental failures in cryptocurrency market integrity:

1. **Fabricated infrastructure accepted at face value**: OneCoin operated for years selling a cryptocurrency with no blockchain, no mining, and no distributed ledger — demonstrating that the absence of independently verifiable on-chain infrastructure should be treated as the most basic disqualifying criterion for any cryptocurrency assessment
2. **MLM distribution as fraud amplifier**: The multi-level marketing structure created financial incentives for promoters to recruit regardless of the product's legitimacy, enabling the scheme to reach 3.5 million victims across 175+ countries — a scale that no direct marketing campaign could achieve
3. **Regulatory arbitrage across jurisdictions**: OneCoin's deliberate fragmentation across Dubai, Belize, Bulgaria, and multiple other jurisdictions enabled it to continue operating even as individual countries issued warnings and bans, demonstrating that cross-jurisdictional regulatory gaps enable fraud at scale
4. **Self-controlled exchange as liquidity trap**: The xcoinx exchange, secretly owned by OneCoin with daily selling limits, prevented investors from realizing that their holdings were worthless — demonstrating that exchange independence and open-market liquidity are prerequisites for any meaningful asset valuation

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

OneCoin's case demonstrates several indicators in the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **On-chain verification as foundational metric**: The most basic market health check — confirming that a cryptocurrency has a functioning, independently verifiable blockchain — would have immediately exposed OneCoin's SQL database fraud
- **Exchange independence**: Trading venues controlled by the token issuer (like xcoinx) cannot provide genuine price discovery — exchange ownership and control relationships should be assessed as part of any token health evaluation
- **Multi-jurisdictional compliance**: OneCoin's ability to operate across 175+ countries despite individual regulatory actions demonstrates that single-jurisdiction compliance checks are insufficient — cross-border coordination and comprehensive regulatory mapping are essential
- **MLM distribution pattern as risk signal**: The presence of multi-level marketing incentives in a cryptocurrency's distribution model represents a measurable structural indicator of potential fraud, distinguishable from legitimate referral programs through analysis of commission structures and recruitment dependency
