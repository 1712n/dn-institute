## Summary

1. **Over 90% wash traded volume**: The Blockchain Transparency Institute (BTI) found that over **90% of Bithumb's reported trading volume** was fabricated through wash trading, placing it among the worst offenders on CoinMarketCap's top-25 list.
2. **Airdrop-driven wash trading**: In August 2018, Bithumb's 120% trading fee airdrop incentivized traders to generate over **$250 million in daily fake volume**, with individual participants earning **$150,000+ per day** from the scheme.
3. **Serial security breaches**: Bithumb suffered three major hacks — a **2017 data breach** exposing 31,000 users (attributed to North Korean hackers), a **$32 million hack in June 2018**, and a suspected **$20 million insider theft in March 2019**.
4. **Governance and fraud**: Former chairman Lee Jung-hoon was indicted for **$100 million in fraud** during a failed acquisition, while de facto owner Kang Jong-hyun was arrested for **$48 million in embezzlement** and stock manipulation.

## Background

Bithumb, founded in 2014, became South Korea's largest cryptocurrency exchange by trading volume. At its peak, Bithumb accounted for approximately 75% of South Korea's cryptocurrency trading and was a globally significant exchange. However, its history has been marked by repeated security failures, governance scandals, and persistent evidence of artificial volume inflation.

## Volume Fabrication Evidence

### Blockchain Transparency Institute (2018)

BTI's reports classified Bithumb among exchanges with systematic fake volume. Their analysis, which cross-referenced reported trading data with order book depth and on-chain transaction patterns, determined that **over 90% of Bithumb's reported volume** was attributable to wash trading [1].

Chainalysis independently examined 12 exchanges suspected of faking volume in 2018, with Bithumb appearing on the list. Chainalysis noted that since trading on exchanges largely happens off-chain and is recorded in exchanges' order books, the volumes exchanges self-report are "easily faked or subject to wash trading" [2].

### Sylvain Ribes Slippage Analysis (2018)

Independent researcher Sylvain Ribes conducted a slippage-based analysis of exchange volumes by measuring the price impact of selling $50,000 worth of cryptocurrency on each exchange. His findings identified Bithumb among exchanges that were "so blatantly faking their volumes." The methodology was elegant in its simplicity: if an exchange claims high volume but a modest sell order causes outsized price movement, the reported liquidity does not exist [3].

### Bitwise Asset Management (March 2019)

Bitwise's SEC presentation identified only **10 exchanges with verifiable genuine volume** out of 81 analyzed. Bithumb was not among the legitimate 10. Bitwise estimated that **95% of reported Bitcoin volume** across unregulated exchanges was fake [4].

## The Airdrop Wash Trading Scheme (August 2018)

The most explicit wash trading episode at Bithumb was directly incentivized by the exchange's own promotion. In August 2018, Bithumb launched an airdrop program offering **120% payback of trading fees** with a daily limit of one billion Korean Won (~$900,000) on a first-come-first-served basis [5].

The economic dynamics were straightforward:

- Trading fees were **0.15% per trade** (taker rate)
- The airdrop returned **120% of fees paid** in cryptocurrency
- This created a guaranteed **0.03% profit per round-trip trade** (120% of 0.15% minus the 0.15% fee)
- Traders could capture the full daily airdrop allocation by generating massive wash volumes in minutes

The result: traders manufactured **over $250 million in daily fake volume** starting from August 25, 2018. Individual participants reportedly earned over **$150,000 per day** through this mechanized wash trading. The scheme transformed an exchange promotion into a direct subsidy for volume fabrication.

## Security Breaches

### June 2017: Data Breach (31,000 Users)

Hackers compromised an employee's personal computer, exposing data for **31,000+ Bithumb users** including names, phone numbers, email addresses, and transaction histories. Bithumb was later found to have stored customer data on employee computers without encryption [6].

A U.S. Secret Service-commissioned report attributed the attack to **North Korean hackers**, who allegedly demanded a **$16 million ransom**. Courts found Bithumb "guilty of negligence" for failing to allocate adequate security resources.

### June 2018: $32 Million Hack

Approximately **$32 million** in cryptocurrency was stolen when hackers compromised Bithumb's hot wallet. This was one of the largest exchange hacks of 2018 [7].

### March 2019: $20 Million Insider Theft

Bithumb reported a third security breach, with approximately **$20 million in EOS and Ripple tokens** stolen. Bithumb itself blamed insiders for the theft, with funds quickly moved to other exchanges. The insider attribution raises questions about the exchange's internal controls and personnel vetting [8].

The cumulative impact was severe: Bithumb reported a **net loss of KRW 205.5 billion (US$180 million)** in 2018, a dramatic reversal from KRW 427.2 billion in profit in 2017.

## Governance Failures and Fraud

### Lee Jung-hoon: $100 Million Fraud Indictment

In 2018, Bithumb chairman Lee Jung-hoon negotiated with Kim Byung-gun, chairman of BK Medical Group, for a takeover of Bithumb Holdings. Lee allegedly collected **$100 million** as a "contract fee" and promised to list Kim's BXA token on Bithumb. The token was never listed, and the acquisition fell through [9].

Lee was indicted in July 2021. Prosecutors sought an **eight-year prison sentence**. In January 2023, Lee was acquitted on the grounds that the prosecution could not prove the alleged promises were made.

### Kang Jong-hyun: Embezzlement and Stock Manipulation

Kang Jong-hyun, recognized as the de facto owner of Bithumb, was arrested in **February 2023** for:

- **Manipulating the stock price** of Bithumb-affiliated companies
- **Embezzling 62.8 billion won (~$48 million)** in corporate funds
- Providing **3 billion won (~$2 million)** in cash plus luxury goods to former CEO Lee Sang-jun as bribes for cryptocurrency listings

Lee Sang-jun was subsequently sentenced to **two years in prison** for accepting the bribes. Kang received a sentence of **one year and six months** for breach of trust [10].

### Tax Evasion

In January 2018, South Korean authorities **raided Bithumb** for alleged tax evasion. While the exchange was found not guilty of the primary charges, it was still ordered to pay nearly **$28 million in back taxes** [11].

## Regulatory Context

South Korea's approach to cryptocurrency regulation has evolved significantly since Bithumb's early years. However, during the critical 2017–2019 period when wash trading was most documented, enforcement was limited and the domestic market operated with minimal oversight.

The combination of incentive-driven wash trading (airdrop scheme), independent evidence from BTI, Chainalysis, Bitwise, and Ribes, and serial governance failures creates a comprehensive picture of an exchange where market manipulation was not an aberration but a systemic pattern — alternately driven by external actors exploiting exchange promotions and by internal leadership pursuing personal enrichment at the expense of market integrity.
