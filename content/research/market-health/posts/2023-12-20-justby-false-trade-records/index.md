## Summary

This case study analyzes Justby International Auctions and Cunwen Zhu as a market-health warning about romance-fraud trading schemes that provide false trade records while routing customer funds away from actual trading. On June 22, 2023, the CFTC filed a civil enforcement action against Zhu and Justby, alleging more than $1.3 million in misappropriated customer funds intended for digital asset commodity and forex trading.

On December 20, 2023, the CFTC announced the Central District of California had issued a default judgment against Zhu and Justby. The order found them liable for fraudulently soliciting funds in connection with digital asset commodities and/or forex trading and misappropriating customer funds. It required $1,352,843 in restitution and a $4,000,000 civil monetary penalty, and it banned Zhu and Justby from CFTC-regulated markets and CFTC registration.

The market-health problem was a false-record and money-mule loop. Customers allegedly believed their funds would be used to trade digital assets and forex on legitimate platforms. Instead, the CFTC said no actual trading occurred on customers' behalf, trade records were false, and Zhu and Justby obtained customer funds and transferred the majority to bank accounts, digital wallets, and digital asset trading platforms controlled by other scheme participants.

## Trading Narrative

Justby can be evaluated with a trade-record-to-fund-flow reconciliation. A customer who transfers funds for digital asset and forex trading should be able to connect the deposit to a trading account, order history, fills, open positions, realized P&L, fees, custody balances, and withdrawals. A trade record provided by the scheme is not independent evidence.

The CFTC described Zhu and Justby as part of an elaborate, coordinated romance-fraud scheme. Potential customers were allegedly cultivated through friendly or romantic relationships and then solicited into a fraudulent financial opportunity. The CFTC said at least 29 customers transferred more than $1.3 million to Justby for supposed digital asset and forex trading.

The trading story broke at execution. The CFTC releases said no actual trading took place on behalf of customers and that the supposed trading platforms were controlled by other members of the fraudulent scheme. False trade records allegedly maintained the pretense that customers were engaged in actual trading. In market-health terms, the customer evidence package was internally generated by the same scheme that controlled funds.

The money-mule role matters because it explains how platform credibility and fund movement can separate. The December 2023 release said Zhu and Justby acted as money mules by obtaining customer funds and facilitating transfers to other scheme players. That role turns a local company or individual into part of the market signal: the recipient may look like a business counterparty while funds are actually being rerouted.

## False Market Signals

### False trade records

The CFTC said customers received false trade records to maintain the pretense of actual trading. A trade record should be tested against broker statements, wallet transactions, exchange order IDs, fills, timestamps, fees, and withdrawals.

### Supposed legitimate platforms

Customers allegedly sent funds to trade digital assets and forex on supposedly legitimate platforms. Platform legitimacy should be verified by legal entity, registration status, domain control, custody path, and independent market records.

### Relationship-based solicitation

The scheme allegedly used friendly or romantic relationships before solicitation. Relationship trust can make the trading opportunity feel low-risk, but it provides no evidence of custody, execution, or market returns.

### Money-mule fund routing

Zhu and Justby allegedly obtained customer funds and moved the majority to accounts and wallets controlled by other scheme participants. That routing pattern should suspend reliance on platform statements because funds are leaving the represented trading path.

### Controlled trading platforms

The CFTC said the trading platforms were controlled by other members of the fraudulent scheme. Platform control by scheme participants means account balances and records may be operator-created rather than market-created.

### Restitution uncertainty

The CFTC cautioned that repayment orders may not recover losses if wrongdoers lack sufficient assets. By the time false trade records are disproved, the liquidity may already be gone.

## Detection Checklist

1. Reconcile every trade record to independent broker or exchange records, order IDs, timestamps, wallet flows, and fees.
2. Treat relationship-based investment invitations as unverified until the platform, entity, and custody path are independently confirmed.
3. Verify whether the fund recipient is the actual trading platform or an intermediary moving money for others.
4. Trace outgoing transfers from recipient wallets and bank accounts before accepting displayed trades.
5. Check whether the platform producing trade records is controlled by the promoter or other scheme participants.
6. Preserve chats, trade records, wallet addresses, and transaction hashes before requesting withdrawals.
7. Treat restitution orders as evidence of harm, not as proof that liquidity can be recovered.
8. Preserve legal posture: this article relies on CFTC allegations, court orders, and public enforcement releases.

## Market-Health Lessons

Justby shows that false trade records can be enough to sustain a trading narrative when customers are already socially primed to trust the opportunity. The control is to verify the record source. If the scheme controls both the platform and the trade statement, the record is a claim rather than market evidence.

The case also shows why money-mule routing is a market-health signal. A customer may believe funds entered a trading account, while the actual path moves through bank accounts, digital wallets, and platforms controlled by other scheme participants. Fund-flow tracing should therefore come before performance analysis.

Finally, Justby demonstrates that final judgments are not a substitute for early verification. The December 2023 order quantified restitution and penalties, but the CFTC still cautioned that repayment may not happen if assets are gone. Prevention depends on detecting the fake-record pattern before funding increases.
