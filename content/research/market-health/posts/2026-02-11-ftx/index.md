## Summary

1. **$8 billion customer fund gap**: When FTX collapsed in November 2022, an $8 billion shortfall was discovered between what the exchange owed customers and what it could pay out, making it the largest fraud in cryptocurrency history at the time.
2. **$65 billion backdoor for Alameda**: FTX co-founder Gary Wang built an "allow_negative" flag in FTX's code that permitted Alameda Research — a trading firm also controlled by Sam Bankman-Fried — to maintain a negative balance of up to $65 billion, effectively giving it unlimited access to customer deposits.
3. **Fake insurance fund**: Wang testified that the insurance fund balance publicly displayed on FTX's website was generated by a formula that multiplied daily trading volume by approximately 7,500 and divided by one billion — the number had no relationship to actual reserves.
4. **Alameda's unfair trading advantages**: The CFTC found that Alameda received faster API execution times (by several milliseconds), an exemption from FTX's auto-liquidation system, and an effectively unlimited line of credit funded by customer deposits.
5. **25-year prison sentence**: Sam Bankman-Fried was convicted on all seven fraud charges in November 2023 and sentenced to 25 years in federal prison in March 2024 — the most severe sentence for financial fraud in the cryptocurrency industry.

## Background

FTX was founded in 2019 by Sam Bankman-Fried and Gary Wang. Bankman-Fried had previously founded Alameda Research, a quantitative cryptocurrency trading firm, in 2017. FTX grew rapidly to become one of the world's largest cryptocurrency exchanges, reaching a peak valuation of $32 billion in January 2022. The exchange was headquartered in the Bahamas and offered spot trading, futures, options, and leveraged tokens across hundreds of trading pairs.

Alameda Research, nominally a separate entity, shared the same founder and operated as FTX's primary market maker. While FTX publicly claimed that Alameda received no preferential treatment, internal code and court testimony later revealed the opposite. The two entities were deeply intertwined, with FTX customer deposits flowing freely to Alameda through a hidden backdoor in FTX's systems.

## The "fiat@" Backdoor

### Hidden Code Enabling Fund Diversion

At the core of the fraud was a special account within FTX's database labeled "fiat@ftx.com" — sometimes referred to simply as the "fiat@" account. Gary Wang testified that, at Bankman-Fried's direction, he created an "allow_negative" flag in FTX's code that was applied exclusively to Alameda's account [1]:

- The flag permitted Alameda to withdraw funds even when its account balance was insufficient, effectively creating an **unlimited line of credit** backed by customer deposits
- No other FTX user had this capability — all other accounts were subject to automatic liquidation if balances fell below required margins
- The backdoor allowed Alameda to maintain a negative balance of up to **$65 billion**
- FTX employees from LedgerX (a U.S. subsidiary) discovered the backdoor in spring 2022 while reviewing code for regulatory compliance, but Nishad Singh reportedly removed only some of the code while the core loophole remained
- The employee who reported the issue, Adam Schoening, was reportedly fired by August 2022

### Scale of Fund Diversion

By June 2022, Alameda had borrowed **$9.9 billion** from FTX customer accounts [2]. The borrowing accelerated after Alameda suffered massive losses during the Terra/Luna collapse in May-June 2022:

- Alameda conducted the largest amount of UST-LUNA swaps among Anchor depositors and suffered significant losses
- To repay crypto lenders demanding their money back — including a $400 million loan recall from Genesis on June 14, 2022 — Alameda used FTX customer deposits
- FTX's internal books showed Alameda owed **$8 billion** to FTX by the time of the collapse

## Fake Insurance Fund

FTX publicly advertised an insurance fund to assure users their positions were protected against socialized losses. Gary Wang's testimony during the criminal trial revealed this fund was entirely fabricated [3]:

- FTX's website displayed an insurance fund balance that was **generated by a formula**: daily trading volume × approximately 7,500 ÷ 1,000,000,000
- FTX publicly claimed the fund held "five and a half million USD and five million FTT"
- When asked whether this number reflected the actual amount in the insurance fund, Wang testified: **"No"**
- There was **no FTT** in the insurance fund, and the actual USD amount was lower than displayed
- The Python code generating the fake number was presented as evidence during the trial

## Alameda's Unfair Trading Advantages

### CFTC Findings

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission's complaint against FTX and Alameda documented multiple forms of preferential treatment [4]:

- **Faster execution**: While other API users had their orders routed through FTX's standard system, Alameda could bypass certain portions and gain faster API access, receiving execution **several milliseconds** ahead of other users
- **Exemption from auto-liquidation**: FTX's distinctive risk management system automatically liquidated positions when margin requirements were breached — Alameda was exempt from this process
- **Unlimited line of credit**: The "allow_negative" flag gave Alameda the ability to withdraw billions of dollars in customer assets without any collateral requirement
- None of these advantages were disclosed to other FTX users or the public

### FTT Token Price Manipulation

Sam Bankman-Fried directed the support of FTT (FTX's native token) prices, which served as a key collateral asset on Alameda's balance sheet [5]:

- A November 2, 2022 CoinDesk report revealed that **$5.8 billion** out of **$14.6 billion** of Alameda's assets were in FTT tokens
- This circular dependency — where FTX's own token constituted the majority of its affiliated trading firm's collateral — meant both entities were vulnerable to any decline in FTT's price
- When Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao announced on November 6, 2022 that Binance would liquidate its FTT holdings, the resulting sell pressure triggered a death spiral that exposed the entire fraud

## Collapse Timeline

- **November 2, 2022**: CoinDesk publishes Alameda's balance sheet showing heavy FTT concentration
- **November 6, 2022**: Binance CEO CZ announces plans to liquidate ~$580 million in FTT
- **November 7, 2022**: FTX experiences approximately $6 billion in withdrawal requests within 72 hours
- **November 8, 2022**: FTX halts withdrawals; Binance signs non-binding acquisition LOI
- **November 9, 2022**: Binance withdraws from the deal after due diligence
- **November 11, 2022**: FTX, FTX US, Alameda Research, and approximately 130 affiliated companies file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy; Bankman-Fried resigns as CEO
- **November 11, 2022**: John Ray III appointed as CEO; he later states: "Never in my career have I seen such a complete failure of corporate controls and such a complete absence of trustworthy financial information"
- **December 12, 2022**: Bankman-Fried arrested in the Bahamas at U.S. request

## Legal Consequences

### Sam Bankman-Fried

- Convicted on all **seven charges** on November 2, 2023, including wire fraud, conspiracy to commit wire fraud, securities fraud, commodities fraud, and money laundering [6]
- Sentenced to **25 years in federal prison** on March 28, 2024
- Ordered to forfeit **$11 billion**

### Cooperating Witnesses

- **Caroline Ellison** (former Alameda CEO): Sentenced to **2 years in prison** in September 2024 after cooperating with prosecutors and testifying against Bankman-Fried
- **Gary Wang** (FTX co-founder): Sentenced to **time served and 3 years supervised release** in November 2024; ordered to forfeit $11 billion [7]
- **Nishad Singh** (FTX Director of Engineering): Sentenced to **time served and 3 years supervised release** in October 2024 [7]

### Bankruptcy Recovery

John Ray III's restructuring team recovered significant assets. By 2024, the FTX estate announced plans to distribute approximately **$16 billion** to creditors, with most customers expected to receive 100% or more of their claims based on petition-date values.

## Market Manipulation Implications

FTX's case represents the most comprehensive example of exchange-level market manipulation in cryptocurrency history:

1. **Fictitious insurance fund**: The fabricated insurance fund numbers gave users false confidence that their positions were protected, while no actual reserves existed
2. **Structural market manipulation**: Alameda's faster execution and unlimited credit line allowed it to front-run other traders and take positions without capital requirements — advantages invisible to the market
3. **Collateral circularity**: Using FTT as both exchange token and primary collateral for Alameda created a reflexive loop where Alameda's solvency depended on maintaining the price of an asset it could artificially support
4. **Volume and liquidity distortion**: As FTX's primary market maker, Alameda's trading activity — funded by customer deposits rather than its own capital — distorted the exchange's volume and liquidity metrics

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

FTX's fraud demonstrates several risk indicators relevant to the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Circular collateral dependency**: When an exchange's native token constitutes the majority of its affiliated entities' balance sheets, a single sell-off can trigger cascading insolvency
- **Insurance fund opacity**: FTX's completely fabricated insurance fund demonstrates the need for independently verifiable, on-chain proof of reserves for exchange insurance mechanisms
- **Preferential market access**: Hidden advantages for affiliated trading desks represent a form of structural manipulation that standard volume analysis cannot detect
- **Proof of reserves limitations**: FTX repeatedly claimed customer funds were safe and segregated, demonstrating that attestations without independent verification are insufficient
