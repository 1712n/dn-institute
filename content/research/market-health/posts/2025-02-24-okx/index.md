\## Overview



OKX, operated by Aux Cayes FinTech Co. Ltd and headquartered in the Seychelles, is one of the

largest cryptocurrency exchanges in the world by reported trading volume. On February 24, 2025,

the U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) announced that Aux Cayes FinTech had pleaded guilty to

operating an unlicensed money-transmitting business in violation of U.S. Anti-Money Laundering

(AML) laws. The exchange agreed to pay more than $504 million in penalties, comprising an $84.4

million criminal fine and a $420.3 million forfeiture of fees earned from U.S. customers.



The case represents one of the largest enforcement actions against a cryptocurrency exchange and

exposes a multi-year pattern of compliance failures, volume manipulation incentives, and

deliberate evasion of regulatory oversight.



---



\## Background: Volume as a Business Strategy



\### Inflated Reported Volume (2018–2023)



OKX (formerly OKEx) has been repeatedly flagged for wash trading and artificial volume inflation

since at least 2018. An independent analysis published in March 2018 estimated that up to 93% of

OKX's reported trading volume at the time was fabricated, placing it as the top offender among

major exchanges.



The Blockchain Transparency Institute (BTI) published multiple reports between 2018 and 2020

flagging OKX for wash trading. In its September 2019 report, BTI estimated that a significant

portion of OKX's spot volume was artificially generated. OKX publicly contested these findings,

claiming its trading surveillance team operated 24/7 to detect self-trading, and that suspicious

accounts were immediately suspended.



Despite these denials, OKX continued to rank at or near the top of volume-sorted exchange

rankings on CoinMarketCap and similar aggregators throughout this period — rankings that directly

influenced user acquisition, listing fees charged to token projects, and the exchange's

competitive positioning.



\### The Incentive Structure for Fake Volume



Exchange rankings based on reported volume create a direct financial incentive for manipulation:



| Factor | Impact of Inflated Volume |

|---|---|

| Token listing fees | Higher-ranked exchanges can charge more per listing |

| User acquisition | Traders are drawn to exchanges perceived as liquid |

| Institutional credibility | High volume signals legitimacy to institutional investors |

| Market maker agreements | Larger volume attracts more market makers, enabling fee rebates |



Independent research from the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) estimated that on

unregulated exchanges, reported volumes were inflated on average by over 70% across the sample

studied. OKX, operating outside U.S. regulatory jurisdiction for most of this period, fit the

profile of exchanges with elevated incentives and minimal consequences for volume fabrication.

\## The DOJ Investigation: Key Findings



\### Scope of Violations (2018 to Early 2024)



The DOJ investigation covered a period of approximately six years, from 2018 through early 2024.

Key findings disclosed in court documents and the DOJ press release include:



\- \*\*U.S. customer transactions exceeded $1 trillion\*\* in total volume processed through OKX's

&nbsp; global platform, despite an official policy banning U.S. users since 2017.

\- \*\*More than $5 billion in suspicious transactions\*\* and illicit proceeds were processed through

&nbsp; OKX, based on a review of third-party transaction data.

\- OKX staff actively assisted U.S. customers in circumventing KYC requirements. According to the

&nbsp; FBI, OKX employees advised individuals to provide false identification information.

\- Until approximately May 2023, OKX did not use commercially available transaction monitoring

&nbsp; software to detect suspicious activity, money laundering, or sanctions exposure.



\### The Penalty Breakdown



| Component | Amount |

|---|---|

| Criminal fine | $84.4 million |

| Forfeiture of U.S. customer fees | $420.3 million |

| \*\*Total penalties\*\* | \*\*$504.7 million\*\* |



As a condition of the plea, OKX was required to retain an external compliance consultant through

February 2027 to oversee its AML and KYC programs.



Acting U.S. Attorney Matthew Podolsky stated: "For over seven years, OKX knowingly breached

anti-money laundering laws and avoided implementing required policies to prevent criminals from

abusing our financial system."

\## AML Failures as Market Manipulation Enablers



\### No Transaction Monitoring Until 2023



The absence of automated transaction monitoring until mid-2023 is significant from a market

health perspective. Without monitoring:



1\. \*\*Wash traders\*\* could operate accounts that simultaneously placed buy and sell orders without

&nbsp;  triggering compliance alerts.

2\. \*\*Criminal proceeds\*\* could be laundered through trading activity, with the resulting trade

&nbsp;  volume appearing as genuine market activity to outside observers.

3\. \*\*Sanctioned entities\*\* could trade freely, inflating volumes in specific assets without

&nbsp;  detection.



This created a structural environment in which reported metrics — volume, order book depth,

trade frequency — were unreliable indicators of actual market demand.



\### Huione Exposure (Post-Settlement)



Even after the February 2025 guilty plea, an investigation by the International Consortium of

Investigative Journalists (ICIJ) published in November 2025 found that OKX customer accounts

received at least $226 million in USDT from Huione — a Cambodia-based financial group

subsequently blacklisted by the U.S. Treasury — after OKX had already agreed to the DOJ

compliance settlement. OKX stated it had subjected one of the three Huione addresses identified

by ICIJ to enhanced monitoring before the Treasury alert in May 2025.

\## Market Health Metrics: OKX Anomalies



\### Benford's Law Analysis



Academic research applying Benford's Law to exchange trade data — a statistical method used to

detect fabricated numerical datasets — identified OKX as among the exchanges with distributions

inconsistent with organically generated trade flows in the 2018–2020 period. Naturally occurring

financial transactions follow predictable leading-digit distributions; deviations from this

pattern are a standard forensic indicator of data fabrication.



\### Buy/Sell Ratio Distortions



Genuine liquid markets tend to exhibit relatively balanced buy and sell ratios across short time

windows. Wash trading, where a single actor or group of colluding accounts places matched orders,

produces artificially balanced ratios that differ from the patterns generated by independent

market participants reacting to price signals. OKX's spot markets showed patterns consistent with

this behavior in multiple third-party analyses conducted between 2018 and 2021.



\### Volume-to-Traffic Ratio



Web traffic analysis tools (SimilarWeb, Alexa) were used by BTI and independent researchers to

estimate the ratio of reported trading volume to actual site traffic. OKX consistently ranked

among the exchanges with the most unfavorable ratios — high reported volume relative to the

number of actual users visiting the platform — a metric widely used as a proxy for artificial

volume.

\## Impact on Market Participants



The consequences of OKX's compliance failures extend beyond the exchange itself:



\- \*\*Token projects\*\* that paid listing fees based on OKX's inflated volume rankings paid a

&nbsp; premium for exposure to a market that was, in part, artificial.

\- \*\*Retail traders\*\* making decisions based on volume signals were acting on unreliable data,

&nbsp; potentially entering or exiting positions based on fabricated liquidity.

\- \*\*Market makers\*\* providing quotes on OKX were operating in an environment where a portion of

&nbsp; counterparty flow was synthetic, distorting their pricing models.

\- \*\*Competing exchanges\*\* that complied with AML requirements were placed at a competitive

&nbsp; disadvantage relative to OKX, which generated hundreds of millions in fee revenue by

&nbsp; circumventing compliance costs.
