## Summary

1. **Transaction mining pioneer**: FCoin introduced "trans-fee mining" in June 2018, returning 100% of trading fees as FT tokens — creating the structural template for wash trading incentives later adopted by Bit-Z, CoinBene, and others.
2. **Volume exceeded all other exchanges**: At its peak in late June 2018, FCoin's reported daily volume briefly surpassed the combined volume of all other major exchanges, reaching over $5.6 billion daily — an anomaly driven entirely by its mining model.
3. **Estimated 85%+ fake volume**: The Blockchain Transparency Institute flagged FCoin among exchanges with the highest proportions of fabricated volume, with independent analyses estimating that the vast majority of its reported activity was self-trading driven by mining incentives.
4. **$130M collapse**: In February 2020, founder Zhang Jian announced FCoin's closure, revealing a shortfall of 7,000–13,000 BTC (approximately $70–$130 million at the time), attributing it to internal errors and the unsustainable economics of the mining model.

## Background

FCoin was founded in May 2018 by Zhang Jian, a former CTO/VP of Huobi, one of China's largest cryptocurrency exchanges. The exchange launched with an aggressive "trans-fee mining" model that immediately attracted attention — and volume. Based in Beijing, FCoin marketed itself as a community-driven exchange that would "return value to users" through its mining mechanism.

Within weeks of launch, FCoin's reported trading volume exceeded that of established exchanges including Binance, OKEx, and Huobi. The exchange's meteoric rise and equally dramatic collapse became one of the most studied case studies in cryptocurrency market manipulation.

## The Transaction Mining Model

FCoin's "trans-fee mining" model operated as follows [1]:

- **100% fee rebate as tokens**: All trading fees paid in BTC/ETH were returned to users as FT (FCoin Token) at rates based on the previous day's average price. The total FT supply was capped at 10 billion tokens, with 51% allocated to the public through transaction mining
- **Revenue sharing**: FT holders received 80% of the exchange's daily trading fee revenue as dividends, paid in BTC
- **Deflationary mechanism**: FCoin periodically bought back and burned FT tokens using a portion of revenue

This created a self-reinforcing cycle:

1. Users paid trading fees in BTC/ETH
2. Fees were returned as FT tokens (often worth more than the fees paid)
3. FT tokens paid dividends from the exchange's total fee pool
4. Higher trading volume meant higher dividends, incentivizing more (wash) trading
5. The apparent volume attracted new users and listing fees

When the value of received FT tokens exceeded the trading fees paid, every trade became a net-positive activity regardless of whether it represented genuine market interest. This eliminated the primary economic deterrent against self-trading.

## Volume Fabrication Evidence

### Peak Volume Anomaly (June 2018)

FCoin's reported daily trading volume reached extraordinary levels within weeks of launch [2]:

- By late June 2018, FCoin reported over **$5.6 billion in daily trading volume**
- This briefly exceeded the **combined volume** of the next six largest exchanges
- The volume spike had no corresponding increase in unique users, web traffic, or social media engagement
- Volume correlated directly with FT token price movements — when FT price dropped, volume dropped proportionally

### Blockchain Transparency Institute

BTI's exchange volume reports consistently identified FCoin among the worst offenders for wash trading. Their cross-referencing of reported trading data with order book depth, web traffic, and on-chain transaction patterns indicated that the vast majority of FCoin's reported volume was generated by automated wash trading bots exploiting the mining incentive [3].

### Ethereum Network Impact

FCoin's wash trading activity was so voluminous that it had measurable effects on the Ethereum network itself. During peak mining periods in June–July 2018, the high frequency of ERC-20 token transactions associated with FT token distributions contributed to significant Ethereum network congestion, driving up gas prices for all users [5].

### Industry Recognition

The manipulation was widely recognized within the industry:

- Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao publicly questioned the model, asking "How is this different from an ICO?" and warning that without transaction fee revenue, the exchange would need to profit through token price manipulation
- Multiple exchanges that copied FCoin's model (including CoinBene, Bit-Z, and BitMax) were similarly flagged for wash trading
- CoinMarketCap eventually adjusted its methodology partly in response to mining-driven volume inflation

## The Collapse

### Timeline

- **May 2018**: FCoin launches with trans-fee mining
- **June 13, 2018**: FT token reaches all-time high of $1.25; volume peaks at $5.6B+ daily
- **July–August 2018**: FT token price crashes over 95% to $0.06 as mining rewards dilute value
- **2019**: FCoin struggles with declining volume and multiple service disruptions
- **February 17, 2020**: Zhang Jian publishes open letter announcing FCoin's closure [4]

### Zhang Jian's Admission

In his February 2020 open letter, Zhang Jian revealed:

- FCoin had a shortfall of **7,000–13,000 BTC** (approximately $70–$130 million)
- He claimed it was "neither hacked nor an exit scam" but acknowledged it was "a little too complicated to be explained in a single sentence"
- System errors had credited users with excessive transaction-based mining rewards over an extended period since mid-2018
- The exchange lacked a complete back-end auditing system until mid-2019, allowing the deficit to compound undetected
- Zhang admitted to making "decision errors" by attempting to artificially boost FT token demand through buybacks as the price declined throughout 2019

The admission confirmed what many had suspected: the mining model's economics were fundamentally unsustainable, and the exchange had been operating with a growing deficit as mining rewards consumed fee revenue faster than genuine trading activity could replace it.

### Aftermath

Following the closure announcement:

- Zhang Jian reportedly fled China to Japan to avoid repercussions from angry investors
- On-chain analysis by blockchain forensics firms traced approximately **25,350 BTC** passing through FCoin's cold wallets, with portions of funds moving to addresses associated with Huobi and OKEx
- Affected users organized collective legal action, but recovery efforts were largely unsuccessful given the cross-jurisdictional challenges and Zhang Jian's relocation
- The incident became a landmark case study in Chinese cryptocurrency enforcement discussions

## Market Manipulation Implications

FCoin's case is particularly significant because it created the **template** for mining-driven volume fabrication:

1. **Structural incentive design**: The 100% fee rebate model made wash trading mathematically profitable, requiring no market-making skill or genuine trading interest
2. **Copycat proliferation**: At least a dozen exchanges adopted variants of FCoin's model in 2018–2019, including Bit-Z, CoinBene, BitMax, Coineal, and others
3. **Aggregator gaming**: The inflated volumes gamed CoinMarketCap rankings, creating a competitive pressure where exchanges felt compelled to adopt similar models to maintain rankings
4. **Listing fee extraction**: High apparent volumes attracted token projects willing to pay listing fees, creating a secondary revenue stream built entirely on fabricated metrics
5. **Retail harm**: Users who deposited funds based on perceived exchange legitimacy (driven by high CoinMarketCap rankings) lost access to their assets when FCoin collapsed

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

FCoin's case demonstrates several indicators documented in the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Volume-incentive correlation**: Trading volume directly tracked token mining profitability rather than market conditions
- **Volume-traffic divergence**: Reported volume vastly exceeded what user metrics could support
- **Unsustainable economics**: The mining model consumed more value than it generated, creating an inevitable collapse trajectory
- **Cross-validation failure**: No independent analysis verified FCoin's volume as genuine organic trading
- **Copycat pattern**: The model's proliferation to other exchanges demonstrates how a single manipulation technique can become systemic
