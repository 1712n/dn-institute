## Summary

1. **$660 million unsecured loan to a single hedge fund**: Voyager Digital lent 15,250 Bitcoin and $350 million in USDC to Three Arrows Capital (3AC) on an entirely unsecured basis — representing approximately 40% of customer assets entrusted to the platform — and received no repayment when 3AC defaulted on June 27, 2022.
2. **False FDIC insurance claims**: From 2018 through July 2022, Voyager falsely told customers their deposits were "FDIC insured" and "as safe with us as at a bank," prompting joint cease-and-desist orders from the FDIC and Federal Reserve, an FTC settlement imposing a $1.65 billion judgment, and a permanent ban on offering deposit products.
3. **Two failed acquisition deals**: FTX won the bankruptcy auction for Voyager's assets at $1.42 billion in September 2022, but FTX's own collapse in November 2022 voided the deal; Binance.US then agreed to a $1.02 billion acquisition in December 2022, but terminated it in April 2023 citing the "hostile regulatory climate."
4. **Self-liquidation with 50–70% customer recovery**: After both acquisition deals collapsed, Voyager self-liquidated beginning May 2023, distributing approximately 35.72% of claims in-kind initially and issuing additional cash distributions totaling $587 million, with customers expected to recover between 50% and 70% of their deposited value.
5. **Civil-only enforcement — no criminal charges**: Unlike Celsius (Mashinsky, 12 years) and FTX (Bankman-Fried, 25 years), CEO Stephen Ehrlich faced only civil actions from the CFTC ($750,000 disgorgement, 3-year trading ban) and FTC ($2.8 million payment, permanent crypto ban), with total penalties of just $3.55 million against $1.7 billion owed to customers.

## Background

### Stephen Ehrlich and Voyager's Business Model

Voyager Digital was founded in **2018** by **Stephen Ehrlich** and co-founders, launching its platform in 2019. The company was incorporated in Canada as Voyager Digital Ltd. (Toronto-based) [1].

Ehrlich was a **25-year veteran of capital markets**: he started at TIR Securities (an institutional brokerage sold to E\*TRADE Financial), served as CEO of E\*TRADE Professional Trading LLC, led eight mergers and acquisitions, and became CEO of Lightspeed Financial following a management buyout in July 2006.

**Business model**: Voyager operated as a cryptocurrency brokerage and lending platform. Customers deposited crypto and cash, and Voyager promised returns as high as **12% APY** on certain deposits. The company pooled customer assets and lent them to institutional third parties — including hedge funds — to generate yield.

**Scale**: By early 2022, Voyager claimed **3.5 million verified users** with approximately **1,190,000 funded accounts** (as of March 31, 2022). At its peak, total assets reached approximately **$5.9 billion**, with Q1 2022 revenue of $102.7 million. At the time of bankruptcy, Voyager held roughly **$1.3 billion in crypto** and more than **$350 million in cash** at Metropolitan Commercial Bank.

### False FDIC Insurance Claims

From at least **2018** until bankruptcy in July 2022, Voyager stated on its website, mobile app, and social media that customer deposits were **"FDIC insured"** and **"as safe with us as at a bank."** Ehrlich personally made these representations [2].

**The reality**: Voyager was not a bank and was not insured by the FDIC. Voyager maintained a deposit account at **Metropolitan Commercial Bank** (supervised by the Federal Reserve). Customer cash held at that bank was FDIC-insured only against the failure of Metropolitan Commercial Bank itself — not against Voyager's failure. Cryptocurrency deposits were **not insured at all**.

On **July 28, 2022**, the FDIC and Federal Reserve Board issued a **joint cease-and-desist letter** demanding Voyager stop making "false or misleading representations" about FDIC deposit insurance status, citing violations of Section 18(a)(4) of the Federal Deposit Insurance Act [2]. Voyager removed the claims only after receiving this letter.

## The $660 Million Three Arrows Capital Exposure

### The Unsecured Loan

On **June 22, 2022**, Voyager publicly disclosed that it had **$660 million** in exposure to Three Arrows Capital (3AC), a Singapore-based cryptocurrency hedge fund managing approximately $10 billion at peak [3]:

- **15,250 Bitcoin** (~$310 million at the time)
- **$350 million in USDC**

The CFTC later confirmed that Voyager transferred over $650 million to 3AC **"on an unsecured basis"** — meaning there was no collateral protecting the loan.

### Default

- **June 24, 2022**: Voyager requested repayment of **25 million USDC** — 3AC did not pay
- **June 27, 2022**: Voyager demanded repayment of the **entire balance** — 3AC did not pay
- **June 27, 2022**: Voyager issued a **formal notice of default**

Three Arrows Capital had suffered massive losses from the **Terra/Luna ecosystem collapse** in May 2022, failed to meet margin calls, and filed for liquidation in the British Virgin Islands on June 27, 2022.

### Alameda Research Entanglement

On the same day it disclosed the 3AC exposure, Voyager announced it had secured a **$500 million credit facility from Alameda Research** (Sam Bankman-Fried's trading firm): $200 million in cash/USDC plus approximately $300 million in Bitcoin [4].

In a deeply ironic twist, bankruptcy filings later revealed that **Alameda Research had itself borrowed $377 million from Voyager** — meaning Alameda went from borrower to emergency lender within weeks.

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| June 22, 2022 | Voyager discloses $660M 3AC exposure; secures $500M Alameda credit facility |
| June 23, 2022 | Customer withdrawal limits reduced from $25,000 to $10,000 per day |
| June 24, 2022 | Deadline for 3AC to repay 25M USDC — payment not received |
| June 27, 2022 | Formal default notice issued to 3AC; full repayment deadline passes |
| July 1, 2022 | **All trading, deposits, withdrawals, and loyalty rewards suspended** |
| July 5, 2022 | Chapter 11 bankruptcy filed in U.S. Bankruptcy Court (SDNY) |

CEO Ehrlich stated: *"Prolonged volatility in the crypto markets, and the default of Three Arrows Capital, require us to take this decisive action"* [4].

## Failed Acquisitions

### FTX Deal ($1.42 Billion — Voided)

- **September 27, 2022**: FTX won the bankruptcy auction for Voyager's assets at approximately **$1.42 billion** ($1.31 billion for digital assets + $111 million in additional consideration) [5]
- **October 21, 2022**: Bankruptcy court approved the deal
- **November 11, 2022**: FTX itself collapsed and filed for bankruptcy
- **November 15, 2022**: The FTX-Voyager deal was formally **voided**

### Binance.US Deal ($1.02 Billion — Terminated)

- **December 19, 2022**: Binance.US agreed to acquire Voyager's assets at approximately **$1.02 billion** [6]
- **February 2023**: The SEC objected, alleging the deal would involve the sale of unregistered securities
- **April 1, 2023**: The bankruptcy judge ruled the government's case had "substantial merits"
- **April 25, 2023**: Binance.US **terminated the deal**, citing the *"hostile and uncertain regulatory climate in the United States"*

### Self-Liquidation

- **May 5, 2023**: Voyager announced it would **self-liquidate** and wind down operations
- **May 17, 2023**: Bankruptcy Court approved the Chapter 11 liquidation plan

## Customer Recovery

Recovery occurred in multiple phases [7]:

| Phase | Date | Details |
|-------|------|---------|
| First distribution (in-kind crypto) | June 2023 | ~35.72% of outstanding claim amounts; customers transferred to external wallets |
| Second distribution (USD checks) | July 31, 2024 | $587 million in checks sent; 86% deposited (~$573 million) |
| FTX settlement recovery | April 29, 2024 | $445–450 million recovered from FTX debtors |
| 3AC recovery | Ongoing | $20.43 million (Voyager's pro rata share of initial 3AC distribution from $675M claim) |
| D&O insurance | Ongoing | $14.35 million allocated from Directors & Officers insurance mediation |

**Estimated total recovery**: Customers who received both the in-kind distribution and cash checks are estimated to have recovered approximately **50–70%** of their claims. Full 100% recovery is considered unlikely.

## Enforcement Actions

### CFTC (October 12, 2023)

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission charged Ehrlich with **fraud and registration failures** for operating an unregistered commodity pool — specifically, falsely touting Voyager as a "safe haven" while secretly transferring billions in customer assets as unsecured loans to high-risk third parties [8].

**Resolution (September 15, 2025)**: Consent order requiring **$750,000 in disgorgement** to Voyager customers, a **3-year registration and trading ban**, and a permanent injunction against violating anti-fraud provisions of the Commodity Exchange Act. Ehrlich neither admitted nor denied the claims.

### FTC (October 12, 2023)

The Federal Trade Commission reached a settlement with Voyager Digital's corporate entities and filed a separate complaint against Ehrlich personally for falsely claiming customer accounts were FDIC insured [9].

**Corporate settlement**: **$1.65 billion judgment** (suspended to allow return of remaining assets through bankruptcy); permanent ban on offering deposit/exchange/investment products.

**Ehrlich settlement (June 27, 2025)**: Ehrlich and his wife **Francine Ehrlich** (to whom he had transferred millions) agreed to pay **$2.8 million** and accepted a **permanent ban** on marketing or selling retail cryptocurrency products.

### No Criminal Charges

Unlike the leaders of other collapsed crypto platforms — **Alex Mashinsky** (Celsius, 12 years), **Sam Bankman-Fried** (FTX, 25 years), and **Do Kwon** (Terra/Luna, 15 years) — Stephen Ehrlich **faced no criminal indictment from the Department of Justice**. His total financial penalties of $3.55 million ($2.8M FTC + $750K CFTC) represent just **0.2%** of the $1.7 billion owed to customers.

## Market Manipulation Implications

The Voyager Digital collapse reveals critical failures in customer asset protection:

1. **Unsecured concentration risk**: Lending $660 million — approximately 40% of customer assets — to a single hedge fund on an entirely unsecured basis demonstrates the absence of even basic risk management controls that would be mandatory in traditional finance
2. **False safety representations as deposit attraction**: Voyager's false FDIC insurance claims functioned as a marketing tool to attract retail deposits under a manufactured sense of security — customers who believed their funds were federally insured had no reason to assess Voyager's counterparty risk
3. **Contagion amplification through interconnected lending**: The Voyager → 3AC → Alameda → FTX chain — where Voyager's lender (Alameda) was simultaneously its borrower ($377M), its acquirer ($1.42B deal), and itself insolvent — demonstrates that crypto lending platforms created a web of circular exposure that amplified rather than distributed risk
4. **Regulatory gap exploitation**: Voyager operated for years making false FDIC insurance claims without enforcement action until after the collapse, demonstrating that regulatory oversight of crypto platforms lagged far behind the scale of customer exposure

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

Voyager's case demonstrates several indicators in the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Counterparty concentration as risk signal**: A single counterparty (3AC) representing 40% of platform assets, with no collateral, represents an extreme concentration risk that should be detectable through counterparty exposure analysis — if platforms disclosed such data
- **Yield sustainability as leading indicator**: Returns of up to 12% APY on crypto deposits signal that the platform is taking significant lending risk to fund yields, and the gap between offered yields and sustainable market rates provides a measurable warning signal
- **Regulatory status verification**: Voyager's false FDIC insurance claims demonstrate that self-reported regulatory status and safety claims cannot be accepted at face value — independent verification of insurance, licensing, and reserve claims is essential for market health assessment
- **Acquisition failure as solvency signal**: The collapse of two consecutive acquisition deals (FTX at $1.42B, Binance.US at $1.02B) within six months illustrates how external transaction failures can serve as real-time indicators of ecosystem-wide financial stress
