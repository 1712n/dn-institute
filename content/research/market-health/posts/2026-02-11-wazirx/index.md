## Summary

1. **$235 million hack**: On July 18, 2024, North Korea's Lazarus Group exploited WazirX's multi-signature wallet, stealing approximately $235 million in cryptocurrency — 45% of the exchange's total reserves — making it the second-largest crypto hack of 2024.
2. **Binance ownership dispute**: Binance announced acquiring WazirX in November 2019, but in August 2022 claimed the deal was never completed and that it holds no equity in WazirX's operating entity, leaving ownership responsibility unclear during India's largest crypto crisis.
3. **4.3 million affected creditors**: The hack locked 6.6 million users out of their funds for over a year, with a Singapore court-approved restructuring plan offering creditors approximately 55% recovery of lost funds.
4. **India Enforcement Directorate investigation**: India's ED investigated WazirX for alleged money laundering and froze assets worth $8.1 million (INR 64.67 crore), adding regulatory pressure to an exchange already reeling from the hack.
5. **Custody blame game**: WazirX blamed wallet provider Liminal Custody for the breach, while an independent Grant Thornton audit found no evidence that Liminal's systems were compromised, pointing instead to weaknesses in WazirX's infrastructure.

## Background

WazirX was founded in 2018 by **Nischal Shetty**, **Siddharth Menon**, and **Sameer Mhatre** as India's largest cryptocurrency exchange. The platform grew to serve over 6.6 million users, becoming the dominant crypto trading venue in India.

**Corporate structure:**
- **Zettai Pte Ltd** (Singapore): Parent entity, incorporated January 2019. Nischal Shetty holds 74.27%, Sameer Mhatre 18.56%, Siddharth Menon 7.17%
- **Zanmai Labs Pvt Ltd** (India): Subsidiary handling INR operations, with 99% held by Shetty and Zettai

Zettai was established in Singapore after the Reserve Bank of India's 2018 cryptocurrency ban, separating crypto operations (Singapore) from fiat operations (India).

## The Binance Acquisition Dispute

### Announced Acquisition (November 2019)

On November 21, 2019, Binance announced it had "acquired" WazirX, stating the deal would enable Indian users to buy cryptocurrency with INR through Binance's fiat gateway [1]:

- The acquisition amount was **never publicly disclosed**
- WazirX was integrated into Binance's ecosystem, with WazirX users able to transfer funds between the two platforms
- Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao promoted the deal as a strategic entry into India's market

### Binance Disowns WazirX (August 2022)

In August 2022, Binance issued a surprising clarification [2]:

- Binance stated the 2019 "acquisition" was limited to an agreement to purchase **certain assets and intellectual property** of WazirX
- Binance claimed it **"did not purchase any equity and does not own any equity in Zanmai Labs"**, the entity operating WazirX
- WazirX founder Nischal Shetty publicly disputed this, stating "Binance lied about ownership"
- The ownership dispute remained unresolved when the hack occurred in July 2024, leaving users uncertain about which entity bore responsibility for their funds

## The $235 Million Hack

### Attack Details (July 18, 2024)

On July 18, 2024, hackers compromised WazirX's multi-signature wallet managed through Liminal Custody's infrastructure [3]:

- **Total stolen**: approximately $235 million in cryptocurrency
- **Asset breakdown**: $102 million in SHIB tokens, $52.6 million in ETH, $11 million in MATIC, $7.6 million in PEPE, plus other tokens
- The stolen amount represented **45% of WazirX's total reserves**
- The attack exploited a vulnerability in the multi-sig wallet's transaction signing process
- Blockchain analysis firm **Elliptic attributed the hack to North Korea's Lazarus Group**

### Money Laundering

- The hacker converted most stolen assets into **43,800 ETH** (valued at $149 million)
- Within two months, the attacker used **Tornado Cash** to launder the majority of stolen funds
- By September 2024, the hacker was "almost done laundering" the stolen assets

### WazirX vs. Liminal Custody Dispute

A bitter blame game erupted between WazirX and its wallet provider [4]:

- **WazirX's position**: A forensic analysis by Mandiant Solutions found no compromise of WazirX's IT systems; WazirX claimed the issue originated from Liminal's infrastructure
- **Liminal's position**: Liminal stated its infrastructure was not breached and that only WazirX could access or move funds
- **Independent audit**: A **Grant Thornton** audit found **no evidence that Liminal's systems had been compromised**, pointing instead to weaknesses within WazirX's infrastructure
- WazirX terminated its contract with Liminal on August 14, 2024, yet continued using Liminal's infrastructure for over $175 million in remaining assets for 75+ days after the hack

## India Enforcement Directorate Investigation

India's Enforcement Directorate (ED) had been investigating WazirX before the hack [5]:

- The ED investigated WazirX for alleged violations of India's Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA)
- Authorities **froze WazirX assets worth $8.1 million** (INR 64.67 crore)
- The investigation examined whether WazirX facilitated money laundering through its platform
- The ED investigation compounded the exchange's credibility crisis following the hack

## Restructuring and Recovery

### Singapore Court Moratorium

Following the hack, Zettai Pte Ltd sought protection through Singapore's courts [6]:

- Zettai filed for a moratorium (creditor protection) in Singapore's High Court
- The first restructuring proposal was **rejected by the court in June 2025** due to procedural flaws and inadequate creditor protections
- An amended scheme was put to creditor vote, with **95.7% approval** from 149,559 creditors holding $206.9 million in claims

### Recovery Plan

The approved restructuring plan offered creditors partial recovery [7]:

- **Immediate distribution**: 52% of claims in liquid assets
- **Recovery Tokens**: 48% of claims as tradeable tokens, to be repurchased using net profits from exchange operations
- Overall, affected users may recover up to **55% of their lost funds** in USDT-equivalent value
- Remaining distributions would occur over **three years** from illiquid assets and operational profits
- Distribution managed through Zanmai India under India's FIU jurisdiction

### Operations Restart (October 2025)

- WazirX resumed trading on **October 24, 2025** — over a year after the hack
- Initial relaunch included **zero-fee trading** for 30 days
- Phased reintroduction of INR trading pairs, starting with USDT
- 6.6 million users regained access to the platform

## Market Manipulation Implications

The WazirX case illustrates systemic risks in exchange operations:

1. **Ownership opacity as liability**: The Binance-WazirX ownership dispute meant that when crisis struck, no entity clearly accepted responsibility for customer funds — demonstrating how ambiguous corporate structures can leave users unprotected
2. **Custodial risk concentration**: Storing 45% of reserves in a single multi-sig wallet created a single point of failure that one exploit could drain
3. **State-sponsored threats**: The Lazarus Group attribution highlights that exchanges face nation-state-level adversaries, requiring security standards beyond typical commercial protection
4. **Regulatory fragmentation**: Operating across Singapore, India, and the Bahamas (via Binance) created jurisdictional complexity that slowed both the regulatory response and creditor recovery

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

WazirX's case demonstrates several indicators in the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Reserve concentration risk**: The 45% reserve loss from a single wallet highlights the need for reserve distribution analysis as a market health metric
- **Corporate structure transparency**: The unresolved Binance ownership dispute demonstrates that exchange ownership clarity is a prerequisite for meaningful market health assessment
- **Post-incident recovery metrics**: The 55% recovery rate and year-long operational shutdown provide benchmarks for evaluating exchange resilience
- **Custody infrastructure as market health indicator**: The WazirX-Liminal dispute shows that third-party custody arrangements introduce opaque risk layers that trading metrics alone cannot capture
