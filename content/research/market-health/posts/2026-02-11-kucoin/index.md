## Summary

1. **$297.4 million DOJ penalty**: In January 2025, Peken Global Limited (the Seychelles-based entity operating KuCoin) pled guilty to operating an unlicensed money transmitting business. The penalty comprised $184.5 million in criminal forfeiture and $112.9 million in fines, with founders Chun Gan and Ke Tang each forfeiting approximately $2.7 million.
2. **Volume-boosting extortion**: A February 2019 investigation by The Block revealed that KuCoin approached low-volume token projects with offers of $90,000–$180,000 "volume-boosting" packages — effectively pay-for-wash-trading schemes — with delisting as the consequence for refusal.
3. **$5 billion in suspicious transactions**: DOJ findings showed KuCoin processed approximately $5 billion in suspicious transactions and sent $4 billion, including proceeds from darknet markets, ransomware, malware, and fraud schemes.
4. **No KYC until July 2023**: Despite operating since 2017, KuCoin did not require customer identification until July 2023, with staff publicly stating on social media that KYC was not mandatory.
5. **$275 million hack**: In September 2020, KuCoin suffered a $275 million cryptocurrency theft subsequently attributed to North Korea's Lazarus Group, exposing critical security infrastructure failures.

## Background

KuCoin was founded in September 2017 by Chun Gan (also known as Michael Gan) and Ke Tang in Hong Kong. The exchange grew rapidly, reaching over 30 million registered users by September 2023 and surpassing 41 million users by 2025. KuCoin positioned itself as "The People's Exchange," offering spot trading, futures, margin trading, and lending services across over 700 cryptocurrency trading pairs.

The exchange was incorporated in the Seychelles through Peken Global Limited, despite having significant operations and users worldwide, including an estimated millions of U.S. customers. KuCoin raised a $20 million pre-Series A funding round in 2018 and launched its native token KCS (KuCoin Shares), which offered holders trading fee discounts and a share of exchange revenue.

## Volume-Boosting and Wash Trading Allegations

### The Block Investigation (February 2019)

In February 2019, The Block published an investigation revealing that KuCoin had approached token projects with "volume-boosting" offers that amounted to extortion-backed wash trading [1]:

- KuCoin identified approximately **16 tokens** in the bottom 18% of daily trading activity and threatened them with delisting
- Projects were offered "advanced marketing packages" that would boost their trading volume — at a price
- **Encrypgen**: CEO was pitched a package priced at **$90,000**; after refusing to pay, Encrypgen's token was delisted
- **Jibrel Network**: COO Talal Tabbaa received a **$180,000** proposal; he stated: "You have the ability to improve your volume or you'll be delisted... they recommended market-making firms... It was basically to do wash trading"
- **Unikrn** and **Publica** were also among the projects approached with similar offers
- The recommended "market-making firms" were reportedly affiliated with KuCoin itself

### KuCoin's Response

KuCoin denied offering volume-boosting services, claiming the emails could have come from fraudulent accounts and stating the exchange would address any policy violations internally [1]. However, cryptocurrency analyst Sylvain Ribes noted that "Chinese exchanges call market making what is, in reality, wash trading, which sees an entity trade against itself, artificially pumping volume" [2].

## AML and KYC Failures

### DOJ Indictment (March 2024)

On March 26, 2024, the U.S. Department of Justice unsealed an indictment against KuCoin and its founders Chun Gan and Ke Tang, charging them with conspiring to operate an unlicensed money transmitting business and violating the Bank Secrecy Act [3]:

- From its launch in **2017 through at least July 2023**, KuCoin did not require customers to provide identification
- KuCoin never registered with FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) as a money services business
- The exchange filed **no suspicious activity reports** despite processing billions in transactions
- KuCoin staff publicly stated on social media platforms that KYC verification was **not mandatory**
- The exchange processed approximately **$5 billion** in suspicious transactions received and **$4 billion** sent
- Proceeds handled included funds from **darknet markets, ransomware operations, malware distribution, and fraud schemes**

### Guilty Plea and $297.4 Million Settlement (January 2025)

On January 27, 2025, Peken Global Limited pled guilty to one count of operating an unlicensed money transmitting business [4]:

- **Criminal forfeiture**: $184.5 million
- **Criminal fine**: $112.9 million
- **Founders' forfeitures**: ~$2.7 million each (Gan and Tang)
- **Total**: $297.4 million
- KuCoin was required to **exit the U.S. market for a minimum of two years**
- Founders Gan and Tang received a **two-year deferred prosecution agreement** (both were at large at the time of the indictment)
- They were also **barred from management roles** at the exchange

### CFTC Civil Enforcement (March 2024)

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission filed a simultaneous civil enforcement complaint against KuCoin on March 26, 2024, alleging violations of commodity trading laws by offering leveraged trading products to U.S. customers without registration [5].

## $275 Million Security Breach

### The Hack (September 2020)

On September 25, 2020, KuCoin disclosed that hackers had breached its hot wallets, resulting in the theft of approximately **$275 million** in various cryptocurrencies [6]:

- The attack targeted KuCoin's hot wallets containing Bitcoin, Ethereum, ERC-20 tokens, and other assets
- KuCoin CEO Johnny Lyu stated the exchange had identified suspects and involved law enforcement
- Blockchain analysis firm Chainalysis attributed the attack to **North Korea's Lazarus Group**, a state-sponsored hacking operation
- KuCoin claimed to have recovered approximately **84%** of stolen funds through cooperation with other exchanges and blockchain projects that froze or rolled back transactions
- The incident exposed the risks of centralized hot wallet management and insufficient security infrastructure

### Implications

The Lazarus Group attribution was significant because it connected KuCoin's security failure to a broader pattern of North Korean state-sponsored cryptocurrency theft. The hack demonstrated that despite KuCoin's rapid user growth, its security infrastructure had not kept pace.

## India Regulatory Action (2024)

In addition to U.S. enforcement:

- In December 2023, India's Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) issued compliance notices to KuCoin for non-compliance with the Prevention of Money Laundering Act
- In January 2024, India **blocked access** to KuCoin's platform
- KuCoin subsequently registered with India's FIU and paid a **$41,000 penalty**
- Access was restored by mid-2024 after KuCoin implemented mandatory KYC

## Market Manipulation Implications

KuCoin's case illustrates several forms of exchange-level market manipulation:

1. **Pay-to-play volume inflation**: The volume-boosting packages created a system where listed tokens' apparent trading activity was a function of how much their projects paid, rather than genuine market interest
2. **Delisting as coercion**: Using the threat of delisting to extract payments from token projects inverted the exchange's role from neutral marketplace to active participant in volume fabrication
3. **AML vacuum as feature**: By deliberately avoiding KYC for six years, KuCoin attracted users — including illicit actors — who valued anonymity, artificially inflating both user counts and trading activity
4. **Scale of distortion**: With over 30 million users and consistent top-10 exchange rankings, KuCoin's practices had significant impact on market-wide volume statistics and token project valuations

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

KuCoin's documented practices demonstrate several indicators in the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Incentivized volume fabrication**: The explicit sale of "volume-boosting" packages represents the most direct evidence possible that an exchange was facilitating wash trading as a revenue source
- **AML compliance as market health signal**: KuCoin's six-year avoidance of KYC requirements correlates with its processing of $5 billion in suspicious transactions, suggesting AML posture can serve as an indicator of broader market integrity issues
- **Regulatory enforcement validation**: The convergence of DOJ, CFTC, and Indian FIU actions against KuCoin demonstrates that multiple independent regulators identified the same compliance failures
- **Security infrastructure correlation**: The $275 million Lazarus Group hack suggests that exchanges with weak compliance frameworks may also have weak security infrastructure — both representing risks to market integrity
