## Summary

1. **Tether Issuance and Bitcoin Price Manipulation:** A peer-reviewed study published in *The Journal of Finance* found that unbacked USDT issuances were timed following market downturns and resulted in significant Bitcoin price increases, with the activity traceable to a single entity operating on Bitfinex.
2. **Regulatory Settlements:** Bitfinex and Tether paid a combined **$61 million** in fines — $18.5 million to the New York Attorney General (Feb 2021) and $42.5 million to the CFTC (Oct 2021) — for misrepresenting Tether's dollar reserves and commingling customer funds.
3. **Crypto Capital Corp and the $850 Million Loss:** Bitfinex secretly covered an $850 million loss of customer and corporate funds through payment processor Crypto Capital Corp by drawing on Tether's reserves, without disclosure to customers or USDT holders.
4. **Reserve Composition Misrepresentation:** Tether claimed for years that each USDT was backed 1:1 by US dollars in a bank account. Regulatory investigations revealed that reserves included commercial paper, secured loans, corporate bonds, and other non-cash instruments, with full dollar backing existing for only 27.6% of the time during the 2016–2018 period.
5. **2016 Exchange Hack:** Bitfinex suffered a security breach in August 2016 resulting in the theft of 119,754 BTC (then worth $72 million, now valued at over $11 billion), with the hacker sentenced to 5 years in federal prison in November 2024.

## Background

Bitfinex and Tether are both operated by **iFinex Inc.**, a company registered in the British Virgin Islands. Bitfinex, launched in 2012, grew to become one of the largest cryptocurrency exchanges by volume. Tether (USDT), launched in 2014, became the world's most widely used stablecoin, with a market capitalization exceeding $100 billion. The shared ownership and management between the exchange and the stablecoin issuer created structural conflicts of interest that regulators later identified as central to multiple instances of market manipulation and fraud.

## Metrics used

### Tether issuance patterns — Academic evidence of price manipulation

The landmark study by Griffin and Shams, "[Is Bitcoin Really Untethered?](https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.12903)" (*Journal of Finance*, Vol. 75, No. 4, August 2020, pp. 1913–1964), analyzed blockchain data to investigate whether Tether issuances influenced Bitcoin prices during the 2017 bull run. Key findings:

- **Timed purchases following downturns:** Using algorithms to analyze on-chain data, the authors found that purchases with newly minted Tether were timed following market downturns, consistently resulting in sizable increases in Bitcoin prices.
- **Single entity attribution:** The flow of USDT-to-BTC purchases was attributable to **a single entity** operating on Bitfinex. This entity's activity clustered below round price levels — a pattern consistent with algorithmic price support rather than organic trading.
- **Asymmetric autocorrelation:** Bitcoin returns exhibited asymmetric autocorrelation patterns consistent with the supply-based hypothesis — that unbacked digital money was being used to inflate cryptocurrency prices, rather than legitimate cash demand driving the market.
- **Insufficient reserves at month-ends:** The study found evidence suggesting Tether had insufficient reserves before month-end periods, consistent with a pattern of minting unbacked tokens and then arranging reserves only for periodic attestations.

A subsequent academic paper (Ante et al., 2022, *Digital Finance* 4:217–239) provided further supporting evidence, finding that cryptocurrency returns were unusually high in the 24 hours before and after stablecoin issuances, and that Bitcoin prices increased following public Whale Alert announcements of Tether minting events.

### The Crypto Capital Corp affair — $850 million cover-up

In 2018, Bitfinex lost access to approximately **$850 million** in customer and corporate funds held by Crypto Capital Corp, a Panamanian payment processor. Rather than disclosing this loss, Bitfinex and Tether engaged in a series of transactions to conceal the shortfall:

1. In November 2018, Tether transferred **$625 million** from its reserves to Bitfinex to cover the exchange's losses.
2. In March 2019, Bitfinex drew an additional **$900 million** line of credit from Tether's reserves.
3. These transfers meant that Tether's reserves — supposedly backing USDT at 1:1 — were being used as a slush fund for the affiliated exchange's operational losses.

The New York Attorney General's investigation revealed that at the time of these transfers, Tether did not have sufficient dollar reserves to back all outstanding USDT. The NYAG concluded that Bitfinex and Tether "deceived clients and the market by overstating reserves, hiding approximately $850 million in losses around the globe."

### Reserve misrepresentation — The 1:1 backing myth

Tether maintained on its website for years that "every Tether is always backed 1:1 by traditional currency held in our reserves." Regulatory investigations and legal proceedings revealed a different reality:

- **CFTC findings (October 2021):** The CFTC determined that from June 1, 2016 to February 25, 2019, Tether misrepresented that USDT was fully backed by fiat currency held in Tether's reserves. In fact, Tether **held sufficient fiat reserves to back outstanding USDT for only approximately 27.6% of the days** during this 26-month sample period.
- **Actual reserve composition:** When Tether finally disclosed its reserve breakdown in May 2021, it revealed that only **2.9% of reserves were held in cash**. The remainder comprised commercial paper (49.6%), fiduciary deposits (18.4%), secured loans (12.6%), corporate bonds, funds, and precious metals (9.9%), reverse repo notes (3.6%), and Treasury bills (3.0%).
- **No independent audit:** As of 2026, Tether has never published a full independent audit. It provides periodic "attestation reports" from accounting firms, which examine reserves at a single point in time rather than providing the comprehensive historical analysis of a true audit.

## Regulatory actions and legal outcomes

### New York Attorney General — $18.5 million settlement

On February 23, 2021, the New York Attorney General (NYAG) announced a settlement with iFinex Inc. (Bitfinex and Tether) following a 22-month investigation. The settlement required:

- Payment of **$18.5 million** in penalties.
- Bitfinex and Tether to **cease all trading activity with New York residents**.
- Quarterly transparency reports on Tether's reserve composition for two years.

NYAG Letitia James stated: "Bitfinex and Tether recklessly and unlawfully covered up massive financial losses to keep their scheme going and protect their bottom lines."

### CFTC — $42.5 million fine

On October 15, 2021, the CFTC announced two separate orders:

- **Tether:** Ordered to pay a **$41 million** civil monetary penalty for making untrue or misleading statements regarding USDT's dollar backing.
- **Bitfinex:** Ordered to pay a **$1.5 million** civil monetary penalty for illegal, off-exchange retail commodity transactions in digital assets.

The CFTC's acting Director of Enforcement stated that Tether's claims of full dollar backing were "a lie."

### CFTC subpoena (2017)

On December 5, 2017, the CFTC issued subpoenas to both Bitfinex and Tether concerning the backing of minted USDT — the first regulatory action targeting the stablecoin's reserve claims.

### Class action litigation

Multiple class action lawsuits were filed against Tether and Bitfinex in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York:

- **Main class action (filed 2019):** Judge Katherine Polk Failla dismissed RICO claims but allowed market manipulation and antitrust claims to proceed. A third amended complaint was filed in July 2024; litigation remains ongoing.
- **Anderson & Dolifka class action:** Fully dismissed by Chief Judge Laura Taylor Swain in August 2023 for lack of plausible allegations of injury.

### 2016 Bitfinex hack — $72 million (now $11+ billion) stolen

In August 2016, hacker **Ilya Lichtenstein** exploited a vulnerability in Bitfinex's multi-signature withdrawal system, bypassing BitGo's co-signing approval process, and stole **119,754 BTC** (worth $72 million at the time). In February 2022, the DOJ seized $3.6 billion in stolen Bitcoin from encrypted files. Lichtenstein pleaded guilty in August 2023 and was sentenced on November 14, 2024 to **5 years in federal prison**. His wife, Heather Morgan ("Razzlekhan"), received an **18-month sentence** for conspiracy to launder the stolen funds. Both were released early under the First Step Act in 2025.
