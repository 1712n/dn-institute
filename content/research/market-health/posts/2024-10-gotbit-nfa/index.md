## Summary

Gotbit, a Russia-linked cryptocurrency firm, operated one of the most brazen market manipulation businesses in crypto history. Between 2022 and 2024, the company marketed itself as a market maker while offering wash trading and price manipulation as core services. Internal pitch decks promised clients they could "push the price up to 10x to create FOMO" during token launches. The firm's CEO, Aleksei Andriunin (age 26), was arrested in Portugal in October 2024 as part of the FBI's Operation Token Mirrors sting and faces extradition to the United States on charges of wire fraud, conspiracy, and market manipulation.

## Business Model

Gotbit positioned itself at the intersection of hedge fund operations and meme coin market making. The firm targeted new token projects seeking to establish market presence after launch, offering a package of services that went far beyond legitimate market making.

Legitimate market makers provide liquidity by placing buy and sell orders around the current price, tightening the spread and reducing volatility. Gotbit's offering was fundamentally different. The firm's pitch materials explicitly described price manipulation capabilities, including coordinating buying pressure during launch periods to create the appearance of organic demand, maintaining artificial volume levels to improve rankings on token aggregator sites, and timing sell-offs to maximize profit extraction after retail investors entered positions.

The "10x FOMO" language in Gotbit's pitch deck is particularly notable because it removes any ambiguity about the firm's intent. This was not market making with aggressive parameters. This was a manipulation service marketed as market making.

## Operational Scale

Gotbit's manipulation extended across multiple tokens simultaneously. The firm was linked to artificial volume generation on more than 60 distinct cryptocurrency assets as part of the broader Operation Token Mirrors investigation. Among the most prominent was the Saitama token, which reached a market capitalization of $7.5 billion during the period of alleged manipulation.

The firm earned over $3 million in two years from manipulation services, according to prosecutors. While this figure represents the firm's revenue rather than the total market impact, the artificial volume generated by Gotbit's algorithms was described by prosecutors as reaching "billions of dollars" in fake trades.

## Technical Methods

Gotbit deployed automated trading systems that generated self-trading volume across centralized exchanges. The algorithms traded tokens between accounts controlled by the firm, creating the appearance of market activity where no genuine exchange of value occurred. Key technical characteristics included coordinated execution across multiple exchange accounts to avoid simple detection, volume patterns calibrated to appear organic rather than algorithmic, and timing of artificial volume to coincide with marketing events and social media campaigns for maximum impact on retail investor perception.

## Detection Indicators

Gotbit's activity exhibited patterns that market surveillance systems should flag. Volume on affected tokens showed suspicious correlation with marketing campaigns rather than genuine market events. The distribution of trade sizes deviated from natural power-law distributions, with overrepresentation of mid-size trades consistent with algorithmic execution. Counterparty analysis would have revealed that a disproportionate share of trading volume on affected tokens circulated between a small cluster of related accounts.

## Legal Proceedings

CEO Aleksei Andriunin was arrested in Portugal in October 2024, awaiting extradition to the United States. Employee Fedor Kedrov was also charged. The SEC filed parallel civil charges seeking permanent injunctions and disgorgement of profits.

The Gotbit case represents the first time a crypto market-making firm's internal marketing materials were used as evidence of manipulation intent, establishing that explicit promises of price manipulation in client-facing documents can form the basis for criminal prosecution.
