## Summary

1. **In November 2022, FTX, Alameda Research, and more than one hundred related entities collapsed into bankruptcy** after a rapid confidence crisis exposed a multi-billion-dollar shortfall. FTX had been one of the world's largest centralized cryptocurrency exchanges, so the failure became a defining exchange-solvency event.
2. **The core failure was customer-asset misuse and related-party entanglement between FTX and Alameda Research**. Court filings, regulator complaints, bankruptcy reports, and criminal trial evidence showed that Alameda had access to FTX customer funds and special account privileges that ordinary users did not understand or approve.
3. **The run began after market participants questioned Alameda's balance sheet and FTT exposure**. Binance's public decision to sell FTT intensified pressure, FTX customers attempted mass withdrawals, and the exchange could not satisfy redemptions.
4. **The bankruptcy estate and new management later described severe failures in controls, records, governance, wallet management, and financial reporting**. FTX did not have the internal systems expected of a platform holding billions in customer assets.
5. **The incident created durable market-health lessons for centralized exchanges**: proof-of-reserves without liabilities is insufficient, affiliated trading firms create conflict risk, platform tokens can be reflexive collateral, withdrawal queues are critical stress signals, and customer balances are not safe if segregation and governance fail.

## Background

### FTX Exchange

FTX was a centralized cryptocurrency exchange founded in 2019 by Sam Bankman-Fried and Gary Wang. It grew rapidly by offering derivatives, spot markets, leveraged products, institutional features, and a polished user interface. Before collapse, FTX was widely perceived as one of the most sophisticated global crypto exchanges.

FTX also had a native token, FTT, which gave fee discounts and other platform benefits. Like other exchange tokens, FTT's value depended heavily on confidence in the issuing platform.

### Alameda Research

Alameda Research was a quantitative crypto trading firm founded before FTX. It was closely associated with FTX through common ownership, executives, infrastructure, and business relationships. Alameda traded across crypto venues, borrowed from lenders, invested in projects, and acted as a major market participant.

The relationship between FTX and Alameda created several structural risks:

| Risk | Why it mattered |
|------|-----------------|
| Related-party exposure | Alameda could have incentives or privileges not visible to customers |
| Customer-fund access | If Alameda could use customer assets, FTX customer balances were not fully segregated |
| FTT collateral | FTT's market value depended on FTX confidence, creating reflexive collateral risk |
| Governance concentration | A small group of insiders controlled critical systems and decisions |
| Opaque liabilities | Customers and counterparties could not independently see the full balance sheet |

## What Failed

### Customer Asset Segregation

Centralized exchanges should segregate customer assets from company operating assets and affiliated trading-firm assets. FTX failed this market-health requirement. Evidence in court and bankruptcy proceedings showed that customer assets were used by Alameda and were not fully available for customer withdrawals when the run began.

The core control failure can be summarized as:

1. Customers deposited fiat or crypto to trade on FTX.
2. Account balances appeared in FTX's internal ledger.
3. Alameda had access to funds or credit related to those balances through special privileges and account structures.
4. FTX and Alameda used funds for trading, investments, loans, expenses, or other obligations.
5. When customers tried to withdraw at scale, the assets backing displayed balances were not present in sufficient liquid form.

This is the central distinction between an exchange balance and self-custodied crypto. A database entry is only as good as the controls, assets, and legal obligations behind it.

### Alameda's Special Privileges

Reports and trial evidence described special treatment for Alameda on FTX. These privileges included features such as unusually large negative-balance capacity and exemption-like behavior in liquidation systems. Ordinary customers were not operating under the same risk rules.

Market-health implications:

- affiliated market makers can become hidden creditors or borrowers
- liquidation exemptions can shift losses to the exchange or customers
- negative balances can become unsecured loans
- related-party privileges defeat normal risk-engine assumptions
- platform solvency can depend on one affiliated trader's balance sheet

### FTT Reflexivity

FTT created a reflexive confidence loop. Alameda reportedly held large amounts of FTT and used or represented token value in ways that mattered to its balance sheet. But FTT's market value depended heavily on confidence in FTX itself. When confidence fell, FTT price fell, weakening Alameda and FTX perceptions further.

| Platform-token issue | FTX/FTT lesson |
|----------------------|----------------|
| Thin external liquidity | Large token positions cannot always be sold near marked prices |
| Issuer dependence | Token value falls when confidence in issuer falls |
| Collateral risk | A platform token is weak collateral for the platform's own obligations |
| Balance-sheet opacity | Marked token value can overstate realizable liquidity |
| Run acceleration | Falling token price can publicly signal distress |

## The Run and Bankruptcy

### Withdrawal Surge

After concerns about Alameda and FTT became public, FTX customers attempted to withdraw billions of dollars. A solvent exchange with segregated liquid reserves should be able to process customer withdrawals, subject to normal operational constraints. FTX could not.

Withdrawal runs create observable signals:

- delayed withdrawals
- queue growth
- social-media reports of stuck transactions
- exchange-token price decline
- widening spreads or market depth deterioration
- emergency fundraising or acquisition rumors
- public assurances from executives

FTX exhibited many of these signals in a compressed period.

### Bankruptcy Filing

FTX filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on November 11, 2022. New CEO John J. Ray III, who had experience with complex bankruptcies including Enron, later described severe failures in corporate controls and reliable financial information.

The bankruptcy process involved:

- locating wallets and assets
- securing remaining crypto
- reconciling customer claims
- investigating transfers and insider conduct
- selling or recovering investments
- negotiating with creditors
- litigating claims against insiders and counterparties
- preparing distributions

The bankruptcy estate later recovered substantial assets, helped by crypto price appreciation and investment recoveries. But later recovery does not change the market-health failure: customers lost access to assets during the crisis and became creditors in a legal process.

## Legal and Regulatory Fallout

### Criminal Trial

Sam Bankman-Fried was tried in U.S. federal court and convicted in November 2023 on fraud and conspiracy counts. In March 2024, he was sentenced to 25 years in prison.

Several former insiders, including Caroline Ellison, Gary Wang, and Nishad Singh, pleaded guilty and testified. Their testimony described customer-fund misuse, Alameda privileges, misleading public statements, and internal awareness of the shortfall.

### Regulatory Actions

Regulators including the SEC and CFTC brought actions involving FTX, Alameda, Bankman-Fried, and other executives. Allegations centered on fraud, customer-asset misuse, misleading investors, and improper operation of the exchange and affiliated trading firm.

For market-health analysis, the key point is that legal accountability came after the risk had already materialized. Surveillance systems need to detect structural red flags before court findings:

- related-party trading firm privileges
- platform-token collateral concentration
- opaque reserves and liabilities
- weak governance and finance controls
- inconsistent executive assurances
- withdrawal friction under stress

## Market Impact

FTX's collapse had broad effects:

1. **Exchange confidence shock**: Customers across crypto withdrew assets from centralized exchanges and demanded proof-of-reserves.
2. **CeFi contagion**: BlockFi and other exposed firms entered or deepened insolvency processes.
3. **Market liquidity reduction**: Alameda's disappearance removed a major trading firm and liquidity provider.
4. **Regulatory acceleration**: Policymakers used FTX as a central example in exchange, custody, stablecoin, and market-structure debates.
5. **Proof-of-reserves debate**: Exchanges rushed to publish wallet attestations, but critics emphasized that liabilities and off-balance-sheet obligations were equally important.

### Proof of Reserves Is Incomplete Without Liabilities

After FTX, many exchanges published proof-of-reserves snapshots. This was useful but incomplete. A market-health framework needs:

| Component | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| Wallet proof | Shows assets controlled at a point in time |
| Liability proof | Shows customer balances and obligations |
| Segregation proof | Shows customer assets are not pledged or lent improperly |
| Related-party disclosure | Reveals exposure to affiliates |
| Governance controls | Limits insider privilege and unilateral transfers |
| Audit and reconciliation | Tests that internal ledgers match external wallets |

FTX showed that a platform can appear large, liquid, and reputable while internal controls are severely deficient.

## Vulnerability Pattern: Exchange-Affiliate Commingling

FTX fits a specific exchange-risk pattern:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Exchange growth | Customers deposit assets and trade actively |
| Affiliate expansion | Related trading firm grows alongside exchange |
| Hidden credit | Affiliate receives privileges or access unavailable to ordinary users |
| Platform-token collateral | Affiliate balance sheet depends on exchange-issued token |
| Market skepticism | Public reporting reveals concentration or opaque links |
| Run | Customers withdraw, token falls, and liquidity evaporates |
| Insolvency | Shortfall becomes visible and bankruptcy follows |

This pattern is especially dangerous because it combines custody risk, credit risk, market risk, governance risk, and information asymmetry.

## Surveillance Signals

A market-health system should monitor:

1. **Exchange-token concentration**: Large affiliated holdings of platform-issued tokens.
2. **Related-party market-making**: Trading firms with special exchange access or common control.
3. **Withdrawal latency**: Delays, partial processing, or inconsistent withdrawal success across users.
4. **Public balance-sheet leaks**: Reports showing illiquid token-heavy assets or unclear liabilities.
5. **Emergency acquisition talks**: Sudden rescue negotiations during withdrawal stress.
6. **Proof-of-reserves gaps**: Asset snapshots without liability coverage or related-party disclosures.
7. **Executive statements vs wallet flows**: Public solvency assurances inconsistent with observed on-chain movement or withdrawal reports.
8. **Stablecoin and fiat-ramp pressure**: Unusual deposit/withdrawal imbalances, bank delays, or fiat settlement issues.

## Lessons for Market Health

1. **Customer balances require asset segregation**. Exchange databases are not proof that assets exist or are reserved.
2. **Affiliated trading firms are high-risk counterparties**. Common ownership and special privileges can turn an exchange into a hidden lender.
3. **Platform tokens are weak collateral**. Their value often depends on the same confidence they are supposed to support.
4. **Proof-of-reserves must include liabilities**. Asset-only proofs can miss borrowings, pledges, customer claims, and affiliate exposure.
5. **Withdrawal friction is an emergency signal**. A solvent exchange should not need to stop ordinary customer withdrawals during a market-price decline.
6. **Governance controls matter as much as code**. Centralized exchange failures often come from permissions, accounting, and internal override powers rather than smart-contract bugs.
7. **Later recovery is not the same as solvency at failure**. Bankruptcy distributions can improve, but customers still suffered a loss of control, liquidity, and trust.
