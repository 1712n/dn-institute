## Summary

Round-lot clustering is a simple way to separate organic retail flow from mechanical volume generation. Real retail flow often leaves visible traces around psychologically convenient order sizes: 10, 50, 100, 500, or 1,000 units. Bot-generated wash volume, by contrast, often creates repeated trade sizes, low variation, and weak or absent round-size preference.

This article adds a reproducible miniature dataset and a concrete detection workflow for the Market Health wiki. The example is intentionally exchange-neutral and uses synthetic observations so the method can be reviewed without accusing a specific venue from incomplete public data.

Key findings from the included sample dataset:

1. The reference venue has a round-lot share of **24.6%**, consistent with visible retail-like clustering.
2. The anomalous venue has a round-lot share of only **2.1%** while **51.2%** of trades concentrate near a small set of repeated non-round sizes.
3. The anomalous venue's mean/median spread is compressed compared with the reference venue, which is consistent with repetitive order-printing rather than heterogeneous traders.

## Why round lots matter

Retail traders often choose memorable sizes because they enter orders manually, think in fiat-equivalent bands, or reuse exchange UI presets. This does not prove a venue is clean, but a complete absence of round-size clustering in an otherwise active market is suspicious. It can indicate that most prints are generated by automated strategies whose purpose is to create reported activity rather than execute genuine inventory transfer.

The signal is strongest when combined with other Market Health metrics already described in this wiki:

- transaction-size distribution,
- buy/sell volume imbalance,
- Benford-style first-digit checks,
- time-of-trade regularity,
- and cross-venue comparison.

## Detection workflow

For each venue and market pair:

1. Collect executed trades for a comparable time window.
2. Normalize trade sizes to base-asset units and, if useful, quote-asset value.
3. Count trades that fall close to common round lots: 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 500, 1,000, etc.
4. Count repeated non-round sizes that appear too often to be plausible independent retail decisions.
5. Compare the round-lot share against peer venues for the same asset and time window.
6. Treat the result as a risk flag, not a standalone verdict.

A practical tolerance window is required because exchange precision varies. For small assets, a tolerance such as ±0.02% around each round lot may be appropriate. For highly volatile quote-asset notional values, it may be better to cluster in quote value rather than raw token amount.

For the reproducible sample dataset below, the calculations use these exact rules:

- **Round-lot set:** every multiple of 10 units from 10 through 1,000, computed in the raw `trade_size` base-asset unit from `sample-trade-size-observations.csv`.
- **Round-lot closeness:** absolute base-unit distance from the nearest round-lot value, with `abs(trade_size - nearest_round_lot) <= 0.05`. This is an absolute tolerance, not a percentage tolerance.
- **Repeated non-round bucket:** `round(trade_size)` is used as the bucket key after excluding round-lot trades.
- **Repeated non-round cutoff:** a non-round bucket is counted as repeated if it appears in at least 10 observations for the same venue. The repeated non-round share is `repeated_non_round_count / venue_trade_count`.
- **Mean, median, and skewness:** computed from raw `trade_size`; skewness is population skewness, `E[((x - mean) / stddev)^3]`.

These simple thresholds are not universal defaults. They are deliberately explicit here so the table can be recomputed from the attached CSV; production analysis should tune the tolerance and repetition cutoff to asset precision, venue minimum order sizes, and the chosen observation window.

## Example dataset

The supporting file [`sample-trade-size-observations.csv`](sample-trade-size-observations.csv) contains 480 example observations split between two venues:

| Venue | Round-lot share | Repeated non-round share | Mean size | Median size | Skewness |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| VenueA_reference | 24.6% | 0.8% | 46.23 | 30.00 | 5.44 |
| VenueB_anomalous | 2.1% | 51.2% | 51.47 | 42.04 | 0.96 |

The anomalous venue is not suspicious because it has fewer round lots alone. It is suspicious because weak round-lot behavior appears together with a high concentration of repeated non-round sizes. That combination is consistent with a bot printing volume in mechanically selected order sizes.

## Interpretation

Round-lot clustering should be read as a comparative metric. Different assets attract different users, minimum order sizes, and UI defaults. A low round-lot share can be normal for professional-only venues or for markets where orders are usually generated algorithmically for legitimate reasons. The red flag emerges when one venue materially differs from peers in the same market and period while also showing other wash-trading symptoms.

Useful confirmation checks include:

- whether the same repeated trade sizes appear at regular intervals,
- whether buy and sell prints alternate without meaningful price discovery,
- whether order sizes remain stable across volatility regimes,
- whether reported volume increases without matching order-book depth,
- and whether the abnormal pattern disappears when public attention increases.

## Limitations

This metric cannot identify beneficial ownership. It also cannot distinguish market making from wash trading by itself. A market maker may use repeated sizes for inventory management, and some retail-heavy venues may have weak round-lot patterns because of app-specific order presets. The metric is therefore best used as a triage signal that prioritizes deeper order-book and trade-sequence review.
