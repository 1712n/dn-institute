## Summary

1. **$4.7 billion customer deficit**: When Celsius Network halted withdrawals on June 12, 2022, approximately 1.7 million customers had $4.7 billion in inaccessible assets on the platform, making it one of the largest crypto lending collapses in history.
2. **Insolvent since inception**: A 689-page independent examiner report filed on January 31, 2023 concluded that Celsius "on a stand-alone basis has been insolvent since inception" and used new customer deposits to pay rewards to existing customers in a "very Ponzi-like" manner.
3. **$558 million in CEL token manipulation**: Celsius spent at least $558 million purchasing its proprietary CEL token on the open market using customer deposits to artificially inflate its price by over 14,000%, while CEO Alex Mashinsky secretly sold $48 million of his own CEL holdings.
4. **Coordinated federal enforcement**: On July 13, 2023, the DOJ, SEC, CFTC, and FTC simultaneously brought charges against Celsius and Mashinsky -- the DOJ arrested Mashinsky on criminal fraud charges, while three civil agencies filed parallel complaints.
5. **12-year prison sentence**: Mashinsky pleaded guilty to commodities fraud and securities fraud on December 3, 2024, and was sentenced to 12 years in federal prison on May 8, 2025, with $48.4 million in forfeiture ordered.

## Background

### Founding and Business Model

Celsius Network was incorporated in London in 2017 by **Alex Mashinsky**, **S. Daniel Leon**, and **Hanoch "Nuke" Goldstein**. The platform launched as a cryptocurrency lending and borrowing service accessible via a mobile app, positioning itself as a decentralized alternative to traditional banking. Mashinsky, who served as CEO, frequently used the slogan "unbank yourself" and marketed Celsius as "the safest place for your crypto" [1].

Celsius's core product was its **Earn program**, which promised customers annual interest rates of up to **18%** on cryptocurrency deposits. The company claimed to generate these yields by lending customer deposits to institutional borrowers and deploying them in decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols. Celsius also offered a **Borrow program** allowing customers to take loans against their crypto collateral at rates as low as 4.95% APR, and a **Custody program** for simple asset storage [2].

The company claimed to return up to 80% of its revenue to depositors through interest payments. By the fall of 2021, Celsius had grown to become one of the largest crypto platforms in the world, claiming approximately **$25 billion in assets under management** at its peak.

### Funding and Growth

Celsius raised significant venture capital during its growth phase. By mid-2021, the company had raised over $750 million in funding, including a $400 million Series B round in November 2021 at a $3 billion valuation. The platform reported **1.7 million registered users** by June 2022.

## The Fraud: How Celsius Actually Operated

### Never Profitable

The independent examiner appointed by the bankruptcy court, **Shoba Pillay** (a former federal prosecutor from Jenner & Block), filed her final report on January 31, 2023. The 689-page report concluded that "the business model Celsius advertised and sold to its customers was not the business that Celsius actually operated" [3].

Key findings included:

- Celsius was **never profitable** and was "insolvent since inception" on a standalone basis
- The company persistently could not generate sufficient revenue from its crypto deployments to pay the high interest rates it promised customers
- Celsius used **new customer deposits to pay interest (rewards) to existing customers**, which the report described as "very Ponzi-like"
- In April 2022, a Celsius employee internally described the company's practice of using customer stablecoins and growing short in customer assets to buy CEL as "very ponzi like" [3]

### CEL Token Manipulation Scheme

At the center of the fraud was Celsius's proprietary token, **CEL**. The examiner report and DOJ charges revealed a systematic scheme to artificially inflate CEL's price [3][4]:

- Celsius spent at least **$558 million** purchasing CEL tokens on the open market
- These purchases were funded with **customer deposits** -- specifically customer-deposited Bitcoin (BTC) and Ether (ETH) -- not from company profits as publicly claimed
- Mashinsky and the company publicly represented that CEL buybacks were funded by Celsius's lending profits, which was false
- The manipulation drove CEL's price up by more than **14,000%** starting in mid-2020
- At its peak, CEL traded above $8; it is now virtually worthless

### Insider Enrichment

While customers' assets were being used to prop up CEL's price, executives were selling their own holdings at the inflated prices [3][5]:

- **Alex Mashinsky** sold approximately **$48 million** worth of CEL tokens while publicly claiming he was not selling
- **S. Daniel Leon** (co-founder) sold at least **$9.74 million** in CEL tokens between 2018 and 2022
- Combined, Mashinsky and Leon sold at least **$68.7 million and $9.74 million** respectively
- Mashinsky publicly stated on social media and in AMAs ("Ask Me Anything" sessions) that he was not selling CEL, when he was actively doing so -- sometimes selling directly to Celsius itself

### Mashinsky's Personal Withdrawals

In the weeks before Celsius halted customer withdrawals, Mashinsky took steps to protect his own assets [5][6]:

- In mid-to-late May 2022, Mashinsky withdrew approximately **$10 million** in cryptocurrency from Celsius
- Approximately **$8 million** was in non-CEL crypto assets
- He made these withdrawals while simultaneously reassuring customers publicly that Celsius was in a "strong financial position" with "sufficient liquidity"
- Other co-founders, including Leon and Goldstein, also withdrew significant sums before the freeze

### Risky and Undisclosed Investments

Celsius deployed customer funds into high-risk investments that were never disclosed to depositors [7]:

- **Lido/stETH exposure**: Celsius had at least **$450 million** in staked ETH (stETH) through Lido Finance. When stETH began trading at a discount to ETH, Celsius held only about $0.94 for every $1 of ETH owed to customers. Only 27% of Celsius's ETH was liquid; the rest was locked in stETH or staked in ETH 2.0
- **Terra/Luna losses**: Celsius had invested in the Anchor protocol on Terra, which offered up to 20% returns on UST deposits. The May 2022 Terra/Luna collapse caused Celsius to lose approximately **$500 million**
- **BadgerDAO hack**: Celsius lost approximately **$50 million** in customer crypto in the December 2021 BadgerDAO exploit
- **Unsecured lending**: The FTC found that Celsius routinely made unsecured loans totaling **$1.2 billion** as of April 2022, contrary to Mashinsky's public claims that all Celsius loans were over-collateralized

## Collapse Timeline

- **May 7, 2022**: Terra/Luna ecosystem begins collapsing; Celsius pulls over $535 million from Anchor protocol
- **May-June 2022**: Mashinsky withdraws approximately $10 million from Celsius while publicly reassuring customers
- **June 7, 2022**: Celsius CEO tells customers on Twitter Spaces that the platform has no liquidity issues
- **June 12, 2022**: Celsius **halts all withdrawals, swaps, and transfers**, citing "extreme market conditions" -- at this point, approximately 1.7 million customers have funds trapped on the platform
- **June 30, 2022**: Celsius announces hiring of restructuring advisors
- **July 7, 2022**: Alex Mashinsky resigns as CEO
- **July 13, 2022**: Celsius Network LLC and affiliated entities file for **Chapter 11 bankruptcy** in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of New York
- **July 14, 2022**: Bankruptcy filings disclose a **$1.19 billion balance sheet deficit** ($4.3 billion in assets vs. $5.5 billion in liabilities), with customer deposits comprising $4.72 billion of liabilities
- **January 4, 2023**: Bankruptcy Judge Martin Glenn rules that crypto deposited into Celsius's Earn program **belongs to Celsius, not to the depositors** -- under the Terms of Use, customers had transferred title of their assets to Celsius
- **January 31, 2023**: Examiner Shoba Pillay files 689-page final report concluding Celsius was insolvent from inception and operated in a Ponzi-like manner

## Legal Consequences

### DOJ Criminal Charges (July 13, 2023)

On July 13, 2023 -- exactly one year after the bankruptcy filing -- the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York announced criminal charges against Mashinsky and former Chief Revenue Officer **Roni Cohen-Pavon** [4]:

**Alexander Mashinsky** was charged with:
- Securities fraud
- Commodities fraud
- Wire fraud
- Conspiracy to commit securities manipulation and fraud

**Roni Cohen-Pavon** was charged with:
- Conspiracy to commit price manipulation
- Securities fraud
- Manipulation of security prices
- Wire fraud

Cohen-Pavon faced a combined maximum term of **65 years** in prison.

### Mashinsky's Guilty Plea (December 3, 2024)

On December 3, 2024, Alexander Mashinsky pleaded guilty before U.S. District Judge John Koeltl to [5]:

- **One count of commodities fraud** (maximum 10 years)
- **One count of securities fraud** (maximum 20 years)

As part of his plea, Mashinsky admitted that:
- He was "the leader of the criminal activity at Celsius"
- His crimes resulted in losses **in excess of $550 million**
- He personally benefited more than **$48 million**
- He agreed to forfeit **$48,393,446**

### Mashinsky's Sentencing (May 8, 2025)

On May 8, 2025, U.S. District Judge John Koeltl sentenced Alexander Mashinsky to [6]:

- **12 years in federal prison**
- **3 years of supervised release**
- **$50,000 fine**
- **Forfeiture of $48,393,446**

The DOJ had requested a **20-year sentence**, calling it "just punishment" for victimizing thousands and causing billions in losses. Mashinsky's defense had sought a lighter sentence of approximately 5 years. The 12-year sentence ranks among the longest in criminal cases arising from the 2022 cryptocurrency market collapse [6].

### Cohen-Pavon's Guilty Plea

Roni Cohen-Pavon, 36, pleaded guilty in **September 2023** to all four charges against him and agreed to cooperate with prosecutors, including testifying against Mashinsky. His sentencing was delayed following Mashinsky's December 2024 guilty plea, with a follow-up hearing scheduled for April 18, 2025. Under his plea agreement, prosecutors would request leniency if he fully cooperated [8].

### SEC Enforcement Action (July 13, 2023)

The Securities and Exchange Commission filed a complaint against Celsius Network Limited and Mashinsky, charging them with [9]:

- Violating the **registration provisions** of the Securities Act of 1933 -- the Earn program constituted an unregistered securities offering
- Violating the **anti-fraud provisions** of both the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934
- The SEC alleged Celsius "lied to investors by presenting itself as a safe investment opportunity" while operating "a failing business model" and taking "significant risks with investors' crypto assets"

### CFTC Enforcement Action (July 13, 2023)

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission filed its complaint -- its **first action against a digital asset lending platform** -- charging [10]:

- Celsius and Mashinsky engaged in a **scheme to defraud hundreds of thousands of customers** from 2018 through June 2022
- Celsius operated as an **unregistered commodity pool operator (CPO)**
- Mashinsky operated as an **unregistered associated person (AP)** of a CPO
- Celsius misrepresented that it only issued collateralized loans, when it routinely made unsecured loans
- The CFTC and Celsius agreed to resolve the complaint with a permanent injunction

### FTC Settlement and Charges (July 13, 2023)

The Federal Trade Commission reached a settlement with Celsius Network and filed separate charges against the three co-founders [11]:

- Celsius was **permanently banned** from offering, marketing, or promoting any product or service involving consumer deposits, exchanges, or investments
- A judgment of **$4.7 billion** was imposed against Celsius, though it was suspended to allow remaining assets to be returned to consumers through the bankruptcy process
- The FTC charged **Alexander Mashinsky**, **S. Daniel Leon**, and **Hanoch "Nuke" Goldstein** with tricking consumers into transferring crypto onto the platform by falsely promising deposits would be safe and always available
- The FTC alleged Celsius took title to and misappropriated deposits totaling more than **$4 billion**, using them to fund operations, pay rewards, borrow from other institutions, and make high-risk investments

## The $4.7 Billion Deficit: Where the Money Went

The total customer deficit of approximately $4.7 billion resulted from multiple sources of loss and misuse:

| Category | Estimated Loss |
|---|---|
| CEL token market manipulation purchases (customer funds) | $558 million |
| Terra/Luna collapse (Anchor protocol) | ~$500 million |
| stETH illiquidity and devaluation | $450 million+ |
| Unsecured loans outstanding | $1.2 billion (as of April 2022) |
| BadgerDAO hack | ~$50 million |
| Executive enrichment (Mashinsky, Leon, others) | $68+ million |
| Operational losses, poor trading, and other DeFi losses | Hundreds of millions |
| Paying customer rewards with new deposits | Cumulative structural deficit |

The gap between what Celsius owed customers ($5.5 billion) and what it held in assets ($4.3 billion) at the time of bankruptcy filing was **$1.19 billion**. However, the total customer deposits trapped on the platform were approximately **$4.7 billion**, and significant portions of remaining assets were in illiquid positions that could not be immediately converted to meet obligations.

## Bankruptcy Resolution and Creditor Recovery

### Plan Confirmation

- The Modified Chapter 11 Plan of Reorganization was approved by approximately **98% of account holders**
- The plan was confirmed by the Bankruptcy Court on **November 9, 2023**
- Celsius **emerged from bankruptcy on January 31, 2024**, and commenced distributions

### What Creditors Received

- Initial distribution: over **$3 billion** in cryptocurrency and fiat currency
- As of mid-2025, Celsius has distributed more than **$2.53 billion** to approximately **251,000 of 372,000** eligible creditors (68%) across 165 countries
- Creditors are expected to receive between **67% and 85%** of their holdings depending on claim type
- Distributions were made through **Coinbase, PayPal, Venmo, and Hyperwallet**
- Some creditors also received equity shares in **Ionic Digital, Inc.**, a new Bitcoin mining company formed from Celsius's mining operations and owned by creditors
- Celsius wound down its mobile and web applications by February 28, 2024

### Key Bankruptcy Ruling

On January 4, 2023, Bankruptcy Judge Martin Glenn issued a critical ruling that crypto deposited into Celsius's **Earn program belonged to Celsius, not to the depositors**. Under Celsius's Terms of Use, customers had transferred title and ownership of their deposited assets to Celsius, meaning Earn depositors were treated as unsecured creditors in the bankruptcy -- a devastating outcome for many users who believed their assets remained theirs [12].

## Market Manipulation Implications

The Celsius collapse demonstrates several forms of market manipulation and fraud in the cryptocurrency lending sector:

1. **Token price manipulation as business model**: Celsius's use of $558 million in customer deposits to inflate CEL's price -- while insiders sold at inflated prices -- represents one of the largest documented token manipulation schemes. The 14,000%+ price increase was entirely artificial, sustained by customer funds rather than organic demand.
2. **Yield as lure for Ponzi dynamics**: Celsius's promise of up to 18% annual returns attracted retail depositors whose funds were then used to pay earlier depositors' rewards, creating a structural deficit that grew with every new customer.
3. **Misrepresentation of collateralization**: Mashinsky publicly claimed all loans were over-collateralized, while Celsius routinely made unsecured loans totaling $1.2 billion. This misrepresentation masked the true risk profile of customer assets.
4. **Insider front-running of collapse**: Multiple executives withdrew millions from the platform weeks before freezing customer funds, a form of information asymmetry exploitation that left retail users holding the bag.

## Relevance to Market Health Metrics

Celsius's case demonstrates several indicators relevant to the DN Institute [Market Health Metrics](https://dn.institute/research/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) framework:

- **Token price manipulation detection**: The $558 million in artificial CEL buybacks using customer funds created abnormal on-chain purchasing patterns and volume anomalies that, in retrospect, were detectable through trade distribution and volume analysis
- **Unsustainable yield as risk signal**: Returns of 10-18% APY in a low-yield macro environment, paid on assets like Bitcoin and Ethereum with no native yield mechanism, should be treated as a structural red flag -- the yields themselves indicated the platform was either taking undisclosed risks or operating unsustainably
- **Proof of reserves limitations**: Celsius marketed itself as "safer than a bank" while being insolvent from inception, demonstrating that customer-facing claims about safety are meaningless without independent, continuous verification of reserves and liabilities
- **Concentration and illiquidity risk**: Only 27% of Celsius's ETH holdings were liquid at the time of collapse, with the remainder locked in stETH or ETH 2.0 staking -- a dangerous mismatch between liquid withdrawal obligations and illiquid asset positions
