## Summary

1. **$215 Million in Customer Funds Lost:** QuadrigaCX, once Canada's largest cryptocurrency exchange, collapsed in January 2019 after CEO Gerald Cotten allegedly died while traveling in India, taking with him the sole access to cold wallets holding approximately $215 million (CAD $250 million) in customer cryptocurrency.
2. **Commingling and Misuse of Funds:** An independent investigation by Ernst & Young (the court-appointed monitor) revealed that Cotten had been using customer deposits to fund personal expenses, trade on competing exchanges under aliases, and cover operational losses for years before the collapse.
3. **Fictitious Balances:** Cotten created fake accounts on QuadrigaCX's platform under aliases (including "Chris Markay" and "Aretino") and credited them with fictitious currency, which he then used to trade against real customer deposits — effectively trading with funds that did not exist.
4. **No Cold Wallet Reserves:** Despite claiming to hold customer funds in secure cold storage, the identified cold wallets were found to be empty. The last transfers from these wallets occurred months before the collapse, and the funds had been moved to personal accounts and external exchanges.
5. **CCAA Proceedings and OSC Investigation:** QuadrigaCX filed for creditor protection under Canada's Companies' Creditors Arrangement Act (CCAA) in February 2019. The Ontario Securities Commission concluded that QuadrigaCX operated as a fraud and that Cotten was running a Ponzi-like scheme.

## Background

QuadrigaCX was founded in 2013 by Gerald Cotten and Michael Patryn (born Omar Dhanani, who had prior fraud convictions in the United States). The exchange grew to become Canada's largest cryptocurrency trading platform, processing over CAD $1 billion in transactions. Cotten served as the sole operator of the exchange's backend systems, including the cold wallets that stored the majority of customer funds — a single point of failure with no oversight or key backup.

On December 9, 2018, Cotten died in Jaipur, India, reportedly from complications of Crohn's disease. His death was reported to QuadrigaCX staff in late December, and the exchange halted withdrawals on January 28, 2019. Cotten's widow, Jennifer Robertson, stated that she was unable to locate his laptop passwords or cold wallet keys.

## Metrics used

### Fictitious trading accounts and phantom liquidity

The Ernst & Young investigation uncovered a systematic fraud operation:

- Cotten created at least **four internal accounts** under aliases, with a combined fictitious balance of approximately **CAD $200 million** in crypto and fiat.
- The "Chris Markay" account alone had a fictitious balance exceeding CAD $100 million.
- These accounts were used to place buy and sell orders against real customer accounts, creating the illusion of market liquidity while extracting actual customer funds.
- When customers deposited fiat or crypto, the funds were moved to Cotten's personal wallets or used to trade on Poloniex, Bitfinex, Kraken, and other exchanges under personal accounts.

### Empty cold wallets

One of the most damning findings was the state of QuadrigaCX's cold storage:

- Six cold wallet addresses were identified as belonging to QuadrigaCX.
- Five of the six wallets had been **emptied by April 2018** — eight months before Cotten's death.
- The sixth wallet contained only **0.5 BTC** at the time of the collapse.
- Total known cryptocurrency shortfall: approximately **26,488 BTC**, **11,378 BCH**, **11,149 BSV**, **35,230 BTG**, **199,888 LTC**, and **429,966 ETH** were owed to customers but not held in any identified wallet.

### Ponzi-like fund flows

The investigation revealed that QuadrigaCX operated in a manner consistent with a Ponzi scheme:

- New customer deposits were used to fund withdrawal requests from existing customers.
- Cotten transferred customer funds to personal accounts on competing exchanges, where he incurred significant trading losses estimated at **CAD $115 million**.
- When trading losses exceeded available funds, withdrawals were delayed or denied, creating a growing deficit.

## Regulatory actions and legal outcomes

### CCAA proceedings — February 5, 2019

QuadrigaCX filed for creditor protection under the Companies' Creditors Arrangement Act (CCAA) in the Nova Scotia Supreme Court. Ernst & Young was appointed as the independent monitor to investigate and recover assets.

### OSC Staff Notice — June 2020

The Ontario Securities Commission published its findings, concluding that:

- QuadrigaCX was operating as an **unregistered securities dealer**.
- Cotten operated a **fraud** — the exchange was effectively a Ponzi scheme where new customer deposits funded earlier withdrawals.
- The exchange lacked basic internal controls, segregation of funds, and independent oversight.

### Exhumation and death verification

Due to widespread public skepticism about Cotten's death, the FBI requested and obtained the exhumation of Cotten's body in December 2019. The results confirmed his identity and cause of death, ending speculation that he had faked his death to escape with the funds.

### Asset recovery

- Jennifer Robertson voluntarily returned approximately **CAD $12 million** in assets (real estate, vehicles, cash) to the estate.
- The court-appointed monitor recovered additional assets, but the total recovery represented a small fraction of the CAD $250 million owed to approximately **76,000 affected users**.
- Creditors received approximately **13 cents on the dollar** through the CCAA proceedings.

### Michael Patryn (Omar Dhanani)

Co-founder Michael Patryn had prior convictions for identity fraud in the United States (under his birth name Omar Dhanani). He distanced himself from QuadrigaCX before the collapse and denied involvement in the misuse of funds. His background raised additional concerns about the exchange's integrity from its founding.
