> **Scope.** The flagged conduct is by pseudonymous, automated wallets. Nothing here indicates that Backed Finance, Kraken, the Solana Foundation, Raydium, or Orca operate these wallets, are aware of them, or benefit from them; xStock pools are permissionless, and a venue or issuer does not control who trades in them. This is a flag on an on-chain trading **pattern**, not a finding that any person acted with the intent to mislead that wash trading requires in law.

## Summary

1. **A wallet-level detector over the liquid xStock pools on Solana flags five.** Scoring the 16 pools with enough swaps to measure (the 14 xStock pools liquid enough to score, plus two organic-token controls), the flag is the share of dollar volume transacted by *balanced heavy round-trippers*: wallets that buy and sell the same pool at least five times each and end within 10% of flat. The five flagged pools score **0.36 to 0.80**; the a-priori organic controls (the liquid tokens WIF and JUP) score zero, and **every** other pool, including the same xStocks in their other pools, scores **at most 0.06**. There is a clean gap, not a continuum.
2. **The signature is a wallet that buys and sells in matched size for no net position.** Pool-level "circular volume" does not separate wash from organic (over a short window most active wallets in any pool both buy and sell, so the liquid controls WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular too, as high as some flagged pools). The wallet-level definition separates them completely: the organic controls contain **zero** such wallets, the flagged pools are dominated by a handful. On QQQX the busiest wallet bought **$10,657** and sold **$10,695** across 36 buys and 36 sells.
3. **Both legs are in the same pool, so it is not arbitrage.** Arbitrage buys in one venue and sells in another to capture a dislocation; these wallets round-trip *in and out of a single pool*, where there is no second price to trade against, and their buy and sell dollars match to a fraction of a percent, so no spread is captured. Round-tripping one pool at matched size is volume for its own sake, not a position or a profit.
4. **The bots run on a robot's clock.** The QQQX wallet's 72 swaps **alternate buy, sell, buy, sell with no exception** at a roughly fixed cadence; its cumulative position sawtooths between zero and about $300 and never leaves flat. Three TSLAX wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back, one finishing as the next begins. The organic controls measured here show no wallet with this regularity.
5. **Within each pool the wallets are a co-deployed fleet, not independent traders.** In TSLAX/Orca six fresh wallets form a **creation chain**: each one's first on-chain act was paid for by the previous wallet, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT, the seeds falling in near-constant ~4.5 USDT steps. Three SPYX wallets run **identical parameters** (7 buys, 7 sells, ~$12,220 each); two wallets trade SPYX in both its Raydium and Orca pools. (The serial peel chain is shown for TSLAX/Orca specifically; elsewhere the wallets share upstream funders, which a depth-capped trace cannot resolve to a single operator.)
6. **It is the pool, not the token.** QQQX is flagged in one Raydium pool and clean in another; TSLAX is flagged on Orca and clean on Raydium. The token contract and the equity behind it do not change between the two pools, only the venue does, so the behaviour is a property of where it trades.
7. **The wallets predate the pools, and the fleet is mixed.** Some named wallets have been on-chain since **October 2025**, before the flagged pools existed, with no xStock activity until recently, so they are aged wallets turned to washing; others, like the TSLAX peel chain, were created the **very day of the snapshot**. The funding wallets reach back to **December 2024**. The wallet ages are mixed - aged and same-day alike - and the funding predates the pools, so this is not a set of accounts spun up for a single burst.
8. **Scale, stated carefully.** In the measured windows the bots round-tripped **$467,000** of buy-and-sell that nets to nothing (a hard, directly-observed floor). A 24-hour figure is an extrapolation, and the two natural methods disagree by about threefold (**$32M to $102M per day** across the five pools, and by an order of magnitude pool by pool); the floor and the per-pool shares are the claims to rely on. Following these same wallets through their full on-chain history lifts the directly-observed matched total to **$5.6M** across 2,836 swaps.
9. **Read it as a flag on behaviour, not a verdict.** Solana addresses are pseudonymous; this characterises a pattern of automated, self-cancelling trading and its on-chain funding structure, not the identity or intent of any person. Every wallet and transaction below is checkable on a public explorer, and the active wallet set rotates over time.

## The market: xStocks, on-chain on Solana

[xStocks](https://xstocks.com) are tokenized US equities and ETFs issued by **Backed Finance AG** under a Liechtenstein prospectus, each backed one-to-one by a custodied share and minted as an **SPL token on Solana** ([Solana Foundation case study](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks)). They launched on **30 June 2025** through a partnership with Kraken and the Solana Foundation, and trade against USDC, USDT and SOL on **Raydium**, **Orca** and **Meteora**, with Jupiter routing aggregated quotes. They are the same instruments whose centralized-exchange markets a companion study found to carry a [pervasive wash-trading pattern on Gate](https://github.com/mkzung/gate-xstocks-wash-analysis). The question here is whether the same *kind* of pattern appears in the tokens' native, fully transparent venue, where every trade is a public transaction and every trader a traceable wallet. It does, and on-chain the analysis can do something no centralized tape allows: name the wallets, reconstruct how they were funded, and hand the reader the transaction hashes.

The macro shape is a hint. Several xStock pools turn over their entire liquidity tens to a thousand times a day (QQQX: $33.7M of volume on a $20.7k pool, a turnover of 1,624x) where an organically traded token like WIF turns over a fraction of its liquidity. This article resolves that hint pool by pool, then wallet by wallet.

## A screen of the liquid xStock pools

The analysis scores every xStock pool the snapshot covers that has at least 150 swaps (the liquid ones; the universe is built from a Dexscreener search over the main xStock tickers), alongside organic controls run through the identical pipeline. The pool-level score is the **share of dollar volume transacted by wash-bot wallets**, where a wash bot is defined purely at the wallet level:

> a wallet that buys **and** sells the same pool at least five times each (`min(buys, sells) >= 5`), and whose total buy and sell dollars land within 10% of each other (`min/max >= 0.90`).

The definition is deliberately set at the wallet level rather than the pool level. A pool-level "how circular is the volume" measure does **not** separate wash from organic: over a short window most active wallets in any liquid pool both buy and sell, so the liquid controls WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular respectively, as high as the flagged pools. The wallet-level definition separates them completely, because it isolates a specific actor a real market does not contain: a wallet that market-takes both directions in matched size, repeatedly, ending flat.

**Calibration is not circular.** The flag is set above the two **a-priori organic controls**, the liquid non-xStock tokens **WIF** and **JUP**, chosen independently of the result; both score zero. The threshold is **0.20**. It is *not* defined by turnover (volume/liquidity), which is itself a wash signal, so the clean xStock pools are a **result**, not a definitional control: the same QQQX and TSLAX that flag in one pool fall below 0.06 in their other pools, run through the same detector.

Five pools clear the 0.20 flag; every other pool, including the same xStocks in their other pools, scores at most 0.06." >}}

The five flagged pools are **TSLAX/Orca** (0.80, 5 bots), **QQQX/Raydium** (0.78, 2 bots), **SPYX/Raydium** (0.43, 5 bots), and **two SPYX/Orca pools** (0.41 and 0.36, 3 bots each), which are also the five highest-volume xStock pools in the universe.

Two honesty notes the reader should weigh:

- **The threshold matters, and the result is robust to it where it counts.** Re-running the screen across `min_rt` in {3, 5, 8, 10} and balance in {0.80, 0.90, 0.95}, the five-vs-rest separation holds at balance 0.90 for every `min_rt`. It is genuinely load-bearing on the balance cutoff: at 0.80 the separation collapses (organic arbitrageurs that lean two-sided light up). 0.90 is defensible because the wallets are bimodal, not continuous: among heavy round-trippers, 18 of 33 in the flagged pools sit at balance >= 0.90, against just 1 of 22 in the non-flagged pools, itself a borderline 0.91. One flagged pool (the smaller SPYX/Orca) is threshold-sensitive; the other four hold to balance 0.95.
- **The snapshot is a window, not a day, and the share is window-sensitive.** GeckoTerminal returns the most recent ~300 swaps per pool, which spans **13 to 79 minutes** in the busy flagged pools and many hours in the quiet ones. Wash *share* therefore moves with window length: slice a quiet pool down to the shortest flagged window (~14 minutes) and a single wallet round-tripping inside that slice can lift its in-window share above the flag - four of the non-flagged pools do exactly that. So the share is an in-window intensity, not a daily rate, and one short window is not by itself proof. What separates the flagged pools is a *fleet sustained across the whole snapshot*: over their full ~300 swaps every flagged pool carries two to five balanced bots, while no non-flagged pool carries more than one. And those fleets are corroborated where a lucky-slice wallet is not - they recur six hours later (`persistence.py`), trace to shared funding, and cycle millions over their lifetimes. The per-pool share should not be read as a flat daily rate, and the dollar extrapolation in "The scale" is treated accordingly.

{{< figure src="balance.png" alt="USD balance of heavy round-trippers: flagged-pool wallets cluster near 1.0, other-pool wallets spread below 0.9" caption="USD balance of every heavy round-tripper (at least 5 buys and 5 sells). In the flagged pools (orange) they pile up at near-perfect balance; in every other pool (green) they are directional and spread below the cut. The 0.90 threshold falls in the gap between the two modes." loading="lazy" >}}

The separation is categorical, not a tuned cutoff. Across the **22** heavy round-trippers in the non-flagged pools, just **one** reaches the wash-bot balance, and it is a lone borderline 0.91; the **18 of 33** in the flagged pools form a distinct mode pressed against 1.0 (median balance 0.94 versus 0.66 across the non-flagged pools). A one-sided Fisher's exact test (the hypergeometric tail, as `verify.py` computes) on that split puts the odds of it arising from one population at about **1 in 12,000** (p ~ 8e-5). Those counts are per-pool wallet appearances - the flagged side spans the fourteen distinct named wallets, several active in more than one pool - so the split does not hinge on treating appearances as independent; nor does it hinge on the 0.90 cut, since the two balance distributions barely overlap at all (the medians above). The flagged wallets are not the tail of the organic distribution, they are a different population. Turnover, an independent signal the detector does not use, corroborates the **thin** pools strongly (QQQX 1,624x on $20.7k of liquidity) but only weakly the **deep** ones (SPYX/Raydium is a $1.67M pool at 56x); there the wallet structure, not turnover, carries the flag.

**The pattern recurs, and the wallets do not.** Re-sampled about six hours after the primary snapshot, **not one** of the wash bots reappears in any pool: the fleet has rotated wholesale. Three of the five pools are again actively washed in the new window (TSLAX 75%, QQQX 69%, one SPYX/Orca pool 41%); the other two sit below the flag at that moment. The activity comes in bursts of matched round-tripping by a rotating set of wallets, so a single window catches some pools mid-burst and others between, which is why any named wallet is point-in-time. The recurrence and the complete wallet turnover are the constants. Both samples are committed; `persistence.py` reproduces the comparison.

## The signature: matched buys and sells

Plot every wallet by the dollars it bought against the dollars it sold. A wallet that buys and sells in matched size sits on the diagonal; a directional trader sits off it.

{{< figure src="signature.png" alt="scatter of USD bought versus USD sold per wallet, wash bots on the diagonal in the flagged pools, organic wallets scattered" caption="USD bought versus USD sold per wallet. Left: the flagged xStock pools, where the wash bots (orange) sit on the buy=sell diagonal at $3k to $33k. Right: an organic control (WIF/JUP), where the busiest wallets are directional or one-sided, off the diagonal." loading="lazy" >}}

In the flagged pools the heavy wallets line up on the diagonal at five figures; in the organic control the busiest wallets sit off it, many pure buyers or pure sellers on the axes. On **QQQX/Raydium** the busiest wallet, [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF), bought **$10,657** and sold **$10,695**; on **TSLAX/Orca** four wallets each bought and sold near **$10,100**, their own buy and sell within a fraction of a percent. A wallet that buys and sells the same amount, in the same pool, ends essentially flat and captures no spread.

This is the place to rule out the benign readings, one at a time:

- **Not arbitrage.** Arbitrage needs two prices to trade between; both legs here execute in the same pool. And the bots capture no spread - across all their trades their sells return about **99.3%** of what their buys cost (a sub-1% net loss to fees), not a profit.
- **Not aggregator routing misattributed to an intermediary.** Each flagged address is a plain keypair owned by the System Program (not a program-derived router account), and each buy and each sell is its own separate transaction signed and paid by that wallet, not a route-leg of one user's swap.
- **Not market-making, and not one lone two-sided wallet.** A market maker rests orders to earn the spread rather than market-taking both sides back to flat; but the claim here does not rest on any single balanced wallet, which an active venue can produce. It rests on *coordinated fleets*: in TSLAX/Orca a peel chain of wallets each funded into existence by the last; in SPYX three wallets running identical buy and sell counts to the dollar; and in every flagged pool wallets that alternate buy/sell with near-perfect regularity (shown below). A genuine market maker or lone two-sided trader is not deployed as an identical-parameter triplet, nor funded into existence by the previous wallet in a peel chain.
- **Not a lookalike token.** Each flagged pool's base mint matches Backed's official xStock published by the [Solana Foundation](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks), and the pools trade at the shares' real prices (TSLAX near $401, SPYX near $750) - what is manufactured is volume, not a price.

What remains is a wallet whose only product is volume: small, similar-sized swaps (around 0.4 QQQX, about $300 each) bought and sold in equal number, so the token position returns to flat and the dollar position nearly so.

## The mechanism: a wallet that never takes a position

{{< figure src="cadence.png" alt="QQQX wallet alternates buy and sell perfectly across 72 swaps while its net position sawtooths and never leaves flat" caption="Wallet C6FyA84D on QQQX/Raydium. Top: 72 swaps in perfect buy/sell alternation. Bottom: cumulative net position, which sawtooths between zero and about $300 and never leaves flat. The wallet's trades net to no position; their economic effect is to add reported volume without the wallet holding the asset." loading="lazy" >}}

The wallet [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF) makes 72 swaps that **alternate buy, sell, buy, sell with no exception**, a median 17 seconds apart; its running position rises one clip on each buy and falls one on the next sell, a sawtooth that never leaves flat. Across the flagged pools each pool's busiest bot alternates 82% to 100% of the time (the QQQX leader perfectly), and in TSLAX/Orca three wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back, one finishing as the next starts. Near-perfect alternation at a fixed cadence is a programmatic signature: a loop running `buy; sell; buy; sell`. The organic controls measured here contain no wallet that behaves this way.

## The coordination: a fleet built from one funding chain

Following each flagged wallet back to its first-ever transaction, through the free Solana RPC, shows how the fleet was built.

{{< figure src="funding.png" alt="the TSLAX wash wallets form a chain, each created and seeded by the previous one with a decreasing USDT amount" caption="The TSLAX/Orca fleet as a creation chain. Each wallet's first transaction was paid for by the previous wallet, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT. The seeds fall in near-constant ~4.5 USDT steps (522.22, 517.67, 513.34, 508.83, 504.48), the signature of automated sequential deployment." loading="lazy" >}}

In the **TSLAX/Orca** pool the five wash bots, plus the fresh wallet that seeded the first of them, form a single six-wallet chain: [`85zuUQ5w`](https://solscan.io/account/85zuUQ5wHER33mpqxezdaUNjnWfL6fhh5Z7wNnaURTjS) seeded [`8gw6JyEW`](https://solscan.io/account/8gw6JyEW1PGLzehpNeNU4jzb2iVtjet1o7YHQPxM1wmH) with **522.22 USDT**, which seeded [`FMMs8SGx`](https://solscan.io/account/FMMs8SGxukt4DRy1wKGyjCcovqj2xci16doZDPqc33eC) with **517.67**, which seeded [`AtzmNv2w`](https://solscan.io/account/AtzmNv2ww5iQRT5XytY43SKvTTeFPL3LdrFPeCiJDFAv) with **513.34**, which seeded [`vwbEYDGU`](https://solscan.io/account/vwbEYDGUamnFvhr9fq6RQnXK8Pn76okVw1kwgFPpt9Z) with **508.83**, which seeded [`2eTkWQyt`](https://solscan.io/account/2eTkWQyt9iGuXP5XUayXRdZuvLoHzDf48ZDjY8EWQUiF) with **504.48**. Each wallet's first transaction was *signed and paid for* by its predecessor, which created its USDT token account and funded it in the same transaction. Fresh wallets created in sequence, each seeded a near-constant step below the last, is the structure of an automated deployment, not of independent people choosing to trade the same pool. Two further patterns confirm coordination within the fleet:

- **Identical parameters.** In **SPYX/Raydium** three distinct wallets ([`78jkE1hP`](https://solscan.io/account/78jkE1hPATuzEnAY6ENpfuLf9DZYtXS5iqnmYAeFpFNY), [`3T1YdUjo`](https://solscan.io/account/3T1YdUjo2edVC1oJ6MK7HvKic2JKGs53GC4HuEV6U7rv), [`B2ES7Uzw`](https://solscan.io/account/B2ES7UzwvJTw2A7dX2WpvqQ7xxB58AkSbSp4WbE2eCqL)) each ran **7 buys and 7 sells of about $12,220**, to the dollar; in **TSLAX/Orca** three wallets each ran **21 buys and 21 sells of about $10,100** and a fourth ran 20 and 20.
- **Shared operation across pools.** Two wallets, [`6G11Z8xR`](https://solscan.io/account/6G11Z8xR1auD4mbgkh9minP9fQycKWdQcAJ7uxH8Smoz) and [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky), trade SPYX in **both** its Raydium and Orca pools.

The funding trace was capped in depth, so this analysis **does not** claim a single named operator across all five pools, nor an exchange of origin. The strict serial peel chain is demonstrated for TSLAX/Orca specifically; several other bots - including the largest, [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky) - trace not to a serial chain but to shared upstream funding wallets, which a depth-capped trace cannot distinguish from a common exchange or bridge. What the evidence does show, transaction by transaction, is that each flagged pool is run by a coordinated fleet - fresh or shared-funded wallets, several of them running identical parameters to the dollar, all on a robotic cadence - not by independent traders. This wallet-level coordination is the core of the finding and stands on its own, independently of any threshold or control-pool comparison: a noisy control in some other window would not change what these specific wallets are.

## It is the pool, not the token

The same tokens trade cleanly in their other pools. **QQQX** is flagged in its Raydium pool [`EibwWLHy`](https://solscan.io/account/EibwWLHyuFeqgU6fvi6g5FtDLF9kQsQL5HQXvcJNkDN8) (78% wash share) and is **clean** in a second Raydium pool of the identical token mint (0 bots, score 0); **TSLAX** is flagged on Orca and **clean** on Raydium (1 marginal wallet, score 0.06, below the flag). The token, its custodied share, and its contract are the same in both; what differs is the pool. The activity concentrates where it does, plausibly in the pools whose volume rankings or incentive programs would most reward added volume.

## The scale

{{< figure src="manufactured.png" alt="measured in-window wash share per flagged pool with the matched-USD floor labelled" caption="The measured self-cancelling share of volume in each flagged pool's snapshot window (36% to 80%), with the matched-USD floor and window length labelled. The hard floor across all five is $467k of directly-observed matched volume; the 24h dollar figure is a wide extrapolation, discussed in the text." loading="lazy" >}}

Two figures bound the size, and they must be read differently. The **hard floor** is what the bots round-tripped inside the measured windows: **$467,000** of matched buy-and-sell that nets to nothing, directly observed, no extrapolation. A **24-hour** figure requires assuming the snapshot behaviour persists, and the two natural ways to extrapolate disagree sharply: scaling each pool's *wash share* by its reported 24h volume gives ~$102M/day, while scaling each pool's *observed bot rate* to 24 hours gives ~$32M/day. The gap is largest where the snapshot window is shortest: on QQQX the share method implies $26M but the pool's own snapshot rate implies under $1M, so the share extrapolation there is unreliable. The honest statement is a range, **$32M to $102M per day across the five pools**, resting on the assumption of persistence; the figures to rely on are the $467k floor and the per-pool shares.

**The snapshot floor is a fraction of the lifetime total.** Following the fourteen named wallets through their entire on-chain history (via Helius, classifying each transaction by the wallet's net token-balance change, which captures DEX-aggregator swaps that a per-pool tape only partly attributes) shows the snapshot caught a sliver of their activity. Across their full histories the fourteen wallets have round-tripped **$5.6M** of matched stablecoin in **2,836** swaps - more than ten times the in-window floor. A single wallet, [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky), cycled **$2.9M** across 912 swaps in QQQX, SPYX and TSLAX alike. The washing is not continuous: every one of the fourteen concentrates its activity into a span of days, not months - the widest is **just over four days** - the same rotating, short-burst behaviour the persistence re-sample caught, where a wallet manufactures volume hard for a few days and the operation then moves on to fresh ones. The same net-balance reconstruction shows a per-pool trade tape is conservative here: across the in-window swaps the true on-chain net runs about **1.3x** what the tape records, because a minority of the bots' swaps route through DEX aggregators that split a single order across pools - so the $467k floor, if anything, understates the wash.

{{< figure src="lifetime.png" alt="per-wallet in-window versus lifetime matched volume on a log scale, lifetime far exceeding the snapshot window" caption="Each named bot's matched (round-tripped) USD: the snapshot window (light) against its full on-chain history (dark), log scale. The fourteen wallets cycled $5.6M lifetime; the snapshot caught a sliver." loading="lazy" >}}

It is also cheap, which is why it runs continuously. The bots buy and sell in matched size, so they capture no spread and their only cost is fees. The on-chain transaction fee on these swaps is the Solana base fee, **5,000 lamports (about $0.001) each, with no priority fee** - even ten thousand bot transactions cost about $10. The pool's swap fee on the round-tripped volume dominates, and at the low fee tiers these pairs use it puts the cost of the entire $467k of observed in-window wash on the order of **tens to a low four figures of dollars**. Manufacturing a dollar of reported volume here costs a small fraction of a cent.

For context, the academic and industry baselines for fabricated volume are large: an oft-cited study estimates that more than 70% of volume on unregulated venues is non-economic ([Cong, Li, Tang, Yang, *Crypto Wash Trading*, Management Science 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30783)), and Chainalysis's 2025 on-chain heuristics, which (like this detector) key on matched buy/sell and multi-address funding clusters, attributed **$2.57B** of suspected wash trading to 2024 alone ([Chainalysis](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/crypto-market-manipulation-wash-trading-pump-and-dump-2025/)). The contribution here is a wallet-level instance of that heuristic family, pointed at a venue and asset class those studies did not reach. The DN market-health wiki has flagged this non-organic-volume marker family on centralized venues before - [Huobi (2023)](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2023-08-14-huobi), [Senso (2021)](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2021-01-05-Senso), and a companion [Bybit study (2026)](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2026-06-13-bybit-wct-lmwr). Prior academic work has named wash wallets on DEXs generally ([Victor and Weintraud](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.07001)); the contribution here carries that wallet-level, funding-chain tracing into the tokenized-equity asset class.

## Why volume would be manufactured here

The detector flags behaviour; motive is a separate question, and the data supports several explanations and proves none. The documented incentives for adding on-chain volume are: climbing the **volume-ranked listings** on DEX aggregators (DEX Screener, GeckoTerminal, Jupiter), where visibility follows reported volume; farming **liquidity-mining or points programs** on Solana DEXs that reward activity (Meteora's points season, Raydium farms); and making a young product **look liquid**, against a backdrop where xStocks' on-chain liquidity has been reported as [thin and weekend-fragile](https://blockeden.xyz/blog/2025/10/30/xstocks/). No xStocks-issuer program that pays a rebate per unit of on-chain volume is documented, and none is assumed here.

## Why it matters

Reported volume is the number people act on. A non-US retail user deciding where to buy QQQX, an aggregator ranking pools by 24h activity, and the product's own "deep, liquid, 24/7" pitch all lean on it. When a pool shows tens of millions in daily volume and most of it, in the windows measured here, is a handful of wallets cycling stablecoins for no position, that signal is corrupted: the market looks more liquid and more in-demand than it is, and a real trader routed into it is acting on a fabricated figure. The point cuts both ways for tokenized equities. Their pitch is transparency: every trade on a public ledger. That same transparency is what lets the manufactured volume be isolated, counted, and named here, where a centralized order book would only show the inflated total. For a young real-world-asset category courting institutions and regulators, the metrics used to judge its legitimacy, volume and liquidity, are precisely the ones being inflated.

## How to read this

This is a flag on a pattern, not an accusation against a person. Solana addresses are pseudonymous, and although the wallets are demonstrably coordinated *with each other*, the analysis stops short of identifying who controls them. US law treats wash trading, transactions with no change in beneficial ownership entered to create a misleading appearance of activity, as manipulation under the Commodity Exchange Act and the securities laws ([CFTC](https://www.cftc.gov/LearnAndProtect/EducationCenter/CFTCGlossary/glossary_wxyz.html)); the matched same-wallet buy and sell shown here is consistent with no change in beneficial ownership, but **whether these wallets acted with the intent to mislead, the element that would make this wash trading in law, is not established here**. The regulatory status of tokenized equities is [actively contested](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/01/29/sec-clarifies-rules-for-tokenized-stocks-tightening-scrutiny-on-synthetic-equity), and xStocks are not offered to US persons. None of that is adjudicated here. What is shown is that five Solana xStock pools carry a large share of volume that, by its own on-chain record, is bought and sold in matched size by a coordinated fleet of automated wallets and nets to no position. Unlike a centralized tape, every statement is independently checkable: paste any wallet below into [solscan.io](https://solscan.io). The named wallets are the active set as of the snapshot; the fleet rotates members over time.

## Methodology and reproducibility

The companion repository, [github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis), reproduces every figure and number from a committed snapshot, with CI that reruns the full pipeline. To regenerate from the raw dumps: clone the repository (tagged at the commit released with this article), `pip install -r requirements.txt`, then run the analysis scripts and `verify.py` (or let CI do it); the committed snapshot makes the run deterministic and key-less.

- **Data, free.** Pool universe and turnover from the **Dexscreener** API; tx-level swaps (wallet, hash, side, USD) and OHLCV from the **GeckoTerminal** API (most-recent ~300 swaps per pool, no deep pagination); wallet funding traces from the public **Solana RPC** (all of the above key-less). The fourteen bots' full lifetime swap histories - used only for the lifetime totals in The Scale - come from the **Helius** enhanced-transactions API on its free tier, classified by each wallet's net token-balance change; that collected history is committed to the repo, so the totals recompute deterministically without a key. The point-in-time snapshot (2026-06-21) is committed so the analysis is deterministic, along with a second sample of the five flagged pools taken six hours later for the persistence check. The funding trace walks each wallet to its earliest retrievable transaction; for the published chains every wallet's full history fits in one page (so the origin is the true first transaction), a caveat noted in code for any higher-activity wallet.
- **Detector and metric mapping** (`metrics_lib.py`). The wash-bot rule extends the DN [market-health metrics](https://dn.institute/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) from the pool level to the wallet level, because the pool-level metrics do not separate wash from organic on these pools: pool `volumedist` (circular volume) and the pool `buysellratio` both run high on the liquid organic controls too (WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular over a short window). The detector is therefore defined per wallet. The matched buy/sell test is the count-and-USD `buysellratio` applied to a single wallet (`min(buys, sells) >= 5` with total buy and sell USD within 10% of each other, the count-based ratio rather than the volume-weighted `buysellratioabs`); the cadence check is `timeoftrade` at the wallet level (near-fixed inter-swap spacing). First-digit (`firstdigitdist`/`benfordlawtest`) is not used here: at the wallet level the signal is the matched two-sided structure, not the trade-size distribution.
- **Pipeline.** Network collectors, run once to build the committed snapshot: `fetch_raw.py` (Dexscreener + GeckoTerminal), `fund_trace.py` + `trace_tree.py` (funding origins via Solana RPC), `wallet_history.py` (each bot's full swap history via the Helius enhanced API), `owner_check.py` (each bot's account owner via Solana RPC, confirming a System-Program keypair and not a router; the result is committed so `verify.py` re-checks it offline). Deterministic analysis, which CI reruns over the committed data on every push: `screen.py` (venue screen + controls + window spans + the sub-window confound check), `cluster.py` (named bots, creation chains, sibling fleets, multi-pool wallets), `temporal.py` (cadence, the floor, and both 24h extrapolations), `persistence.py` (primary vs six-hours-later sample), `lifetime.py` (lifetime washing totals from the committed swap files), `named_wallets.py` (the flagged-wallet table, recomputed from the detector), `figures.py`, `verify.py` (an independent recomputation of every headline claim, including that each flagged pool's named bots match the raw data, the lifetime totals recompute from the committed swaps, and the named-wallet table and owner snapshot are in sync), and a `pytest` suite.
- **Limitations**, stated plainly. The trade snapshot is the most-recent ~300 swaps per pool (a sub-hour window in the busy pools), so wash *shares* are in-window intensities and the daily dollar figure is the labelled extrapolation, not a measurement. A precise *onset* cannot be dated: the free OHLCV history is too sparse and gappy for a per-pool volume-onset curve, and a wallet's age is not its wash onset either (the aged wallets show unrelated activity for months before their first xStock swap). What the wallet dates do establish is that the operation is older than the pools, not when each pool's wash began. The funding trace is depth-capped, so it establishes co-deployment within each pool - a strict serial peel chain for TSLAX/Orca, shared upstream funders elsewhere - not a single named operator across all five. The five flagged pools are also the highest-volume pools in the universe, and only pools with at least 150 snapshot swaps are scored, so "five of sixteen" is five of the sixteen liquid, scored pools, not a census of every xStock pool; the low-volume tail is unscored, not shown clean. Named wallets are the active set as of the snapshot; the fleet rotates members.
