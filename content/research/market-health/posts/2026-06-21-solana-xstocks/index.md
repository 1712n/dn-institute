Five of the most-traded tokenized-stock pools on Solana carry a large share of manufactured volume: fleets of automated wallets that buy and sell the same pool in matched size, never holding a position, several of them funded into existence in coordinated chains. What a centralized tape would report as one inflated number, this post takes apart wallet by wallet - naming them, reconstructing how they were funded, and handing you the transaction hashes.

> **Scope.** The flagged conduct is by pseudonymous, automated wallets. Nothing here indicates that Backed Finance, Kraken, the Solana Foundation, Raydium, or Orca operate these wallets, are aware of them, or benefit from them; xStock pools are permissionless, and a venue or issuer does not control who trades in them. This is a flag on an on-chain trading **pattern**, not a finding that any person acted with the intent to mislead that wash trading requires in law.

## Summary

1. **A wallet-level detector over the liquid xStock pools flags five.** Scoring the 16 pools with enough swaps (14 xStock pools plus two organic-token controls), the flag is the share of dollar volume from *balanced heavy round-trippers*: wallets that buy and sell the same pool at least five times each and end within 10% of flat. The five flagged pools score **0.36 to 0.80**; the a-priori controls (WIF and JUP) score zero, and **every** other pool, including the same xStocks in their other pools, scores **at most 0.06**. A clean gap, not a continuum.
2. **The signature is a wallet that buys and sells in matched size for no net position.** Pool-level "circular volume" does not separate wash from organic (over a short window most active wallets in any pool both buy and sell, so the liquid controls WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular too, as high as some flagged pools). The wallet-level definition separates them completely: the organic controls contain **zero** such wallets, the flagged pools are dominated by a handful. On QQQX the busiest wallet bought **$10,657** and sold **$10,695** across 36 buys and 36 sells.
3. **Both legs are in the same pool, so it is not arbitrage.** These wallets round-trip in and out of a single pool, where there is no second price to trade against, and their buy and sell dollars match to a fraction of a percent. Round-tripping one pool at matched size is volume for its own sake, not a position or a profit.
4. **The bots run on a robot's clock.** The QQQX wallet's 72 swaps **alternate buy, sell with no exception** at a roughly fixed cadence, its cumulative position sawtoothing between zero and about $300 and never leaving flat; three TSLAX wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back. The organic controls show no wallet with this regularity.
5. **Within each pool the wallets are a co-deployed fleet, not independent traders.** In TSLAX/Orca six fresh wallets form a **creation chain**: each one's first on-chain act was paid for by the previous wallet, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT, the seeds falling in near-constant ~4.5 USDT steps. Three SPYX wallets run **identical parameters** (7 buys, 7 sells, ~$12,220 each); two wallets trade SPYX in both its Raydium and Orca pools. (The serial peel chain is shown for TSLAX/Orca specifically; elsewhere the wallets share upstream funders, which a depth-capped trace cannot resolve to a single operator.)
6. **It is the pool, not the token.** QQQX is flagged in one Raydium pool and clean in another; TSLAX is flagged on Orca and clean on Raydium. The token contract and the equity behind it do not change between the two pools, only the venue does, so the behaviour is a property of where it trades.
7. **The wallets predate the pools, and the fleet is mixed.** Some named wallets have been on-chain since **October 2025**, before the flagged pools existed, with no xStock activity until recently; others, like the TSLAX peel chain, were created the **very day of the snapshot**, and the funding wallets reach back to **December 2024**. Aged and same-day alike, with funding older than the pools, this is not a set of accounts spun up for a single burst.
8. **Scale, stated carefully.** In the measured windows the bots round-tripped **$467,000** of buy-and-sell that nets to nothing (a hard, directly-observed floor). A 24-hour figure is an extrapolation, and the two natural methods disagree by about threefold (**$32M to $102M per day** across the five pools, and by an order of magnitude pool by pool); the floor and the per-pool shares are the claims to rely on. Following these same wallets through their full on-chain history lifts the directly-observed matched total to **$5.6M** across 2,836 swaps.
9. **Read it as a flag on behaviour, not a verdict.** Solana addresses are pseudonymous; this characterises a pattern of automated, self-cancelling trading and its on-chain funding structure, not the identity or intent of any person. Every wallet and transaction below is checkable on a public explorer, and the active wallet set rotates over time.

## The market: xStocks, on-chain on Solana

[xStocks](https://xstocks.com) are tokenized US equities and ETFs issued by **Backed Finance AG** under a Liechtenstein prospectus, each backed one-to-one by a custodied share and minted as an **SPL token on Solana** ([Solana Foundation case study](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks)). They launched on **30 June 2025** with Kraken and the Solana Foundation, and trade against USDC, USDT and SOL on **Raydium**, **Orca** and **Meteora**, with Jupiter routing quotes. A companion study found their centralized-exchange markets carry a [pervasive wash-trading pattern on Gate](https://github.com/mkzung/gate-xstocks-wash-analysis); the question here is whether the same *kind* of pattern appears in the tokens' native, fully transparent venue. It does - and on-chain the analysis can do what no centralized tape allows: name the wallets, reconstruct their funding, and hand the reader the hashes.

The macro shape is a hint: several xStock pools turn over their entire liquidity tens to a thousand times a day (QQQX: $33.7M of volume on a $20.7k pool, 1,624x) where an organic token like WIF turns over a fraction of its liquidity. This article resolves that hint pool by pool, then wallet by wallet.

## A screen of the liquid xStock pools

The analysis scores every xStock pool the snapshot covers that has at least 150 swaps (the liquid ones; the universe is built from a Dexscreener search over the main xStock tickers), alongside organic controls run through the identical pipeline. The pool-level score is the **share of dollar volume transacted by wash-bot wallets**, where a wash bot is defined purely at the wallet level:

> a wallet that buys **and** sells the same pool at least five times each (`min(buys, sells) >= 5`), and whose total buy and sell dollars land within 10% of each other (`min/max >= 0.90`).

The definition is deliberately set at the wallet level rather than the pool level. A pool-level "how circular is the volume" measure does **not** separate wash from organic: over a short window most active wallets in any liquid pool both buy and sell, so the liquid controls WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular respectively, as high as the flagged pools. The wallet-level definition separates them completely, because it isolates a specific actor a real market does not contain: a wallet that market-takes both directions in matched size, repeatedly, ending flat.

**Calibration is not circular.** The **0.20** flag is set above the two **a-priori organic controls** (the liquid non-xStock tokens **WIF** and **JUP**, chosen independently of the result; both score zero). It is *not* defined by turnover, which is itself a wash signal, so the clean xStock pools are a **result**: the same QQQX and TSLAX that flag in one pool fall below 0.06 in their other pools under the same detector.

Five pools clear the 0.20 flag; every other pool, including the same xStocks in their other pools, scores at most 0.06." >}}

The five flagged pools are **TSLAX/Orca** (0.80, 5 bots), **QQQX/Raydium** (0.78, 2 bots), **SPYX/Raydium** (0.43, 5 bots), and **two SPYX/Orca pools** (0.41 and 0.36, 3 bots each), which are also the five highest-volume xStock pools in the universe.

Two honesty notes the reader should weigh:

- **The threshold is load-bearing on balance, and holds where it counts.** Across `min_rt` in {3, 5, 8, 10} and balance in {0.80, 0.90, 0.95}, the five-vs-rest separation holds at balance 0.90 for every `min_rt`; at 0.80 it collapses (two-sided organic arbitrageurs light up). 0.90 is defensible because the wallets are bimodal, not continuous: 18 of 33 heavy round-trippers in the flagged pools sit at balance >= 0.90, against just 1 of 22 in the rest. Four of the five flagged pools hold even at 0.95; only the smaller SPYX/Orca is threshold-sensitive.
- **The snapshot is a window, not a day, and the share is window-sensitive.** GeckoTerminal returns the most recent ~300 swaps per pool, spanning **13 to 79 minutes** in the busy pools and many hours in the quiet ones, so wash *share* moves with window length: slice a quiet pool to the shortest flagged window (~14 minutes) and one wallet round-tripping inside it can clear the flag - four of the non-flagged pools do exactly that. The share is an in-window intensity, not a daily rate, and one short window is not by itself proof. What separates the flagged pools is a *fleet sustained across the whole snapshot*: every flagged pool carries two to five balanced bots over its full ~300 swaps, while no non-flagged pool carries more than one - and those fleets recur six hours later (`persistence.py`), trace to shared funding, and cycle millions over their lifetimes. The dollar extrapolation in "The scale" is treated accordingly.

{{< figure src="balance.png" alt="USD balance of heavy round-trippers: flagged-pool wallets cluster near 1.0, other-pool wallets spread below 0.9" caption="USD balance of every heavy round-tripper (at least 5 buys and 5 sells). In the flagged pools (orange) they pile up at near-perfect balance; in every other pool (green) they are directional and spread below the cut. The 0.90 threshold falls in the gap between the two modes." loading="lazy" >}}

The separation is categorical, not a tuned cutoff. Of the **22** heavy round-trippers in the non-flagged pools just **one** reaches the wash-bot balance (a lone borderline 0.91); the **18 of 33** in the flagged pools form a distinct mode pressed against 1.0 (median 0.94 versus 0.66). A one-sided Fisher's exact test (as `verify.py` computes) puts the odds of that split arising from one population at about **1 in 12,000** (p ~ 8e-5). The counts are per-pool wallet appearances spanning the fourteen distinct wallets, so the split does not hinge on treating appearances as independent, nor on the 0.90 cut (the two distributions barely overlap). These are not the tail of the organic distribution; they are a different population. Turnover, a signal the detector does not use, corroborates the **thin** pools strongly (QQQX 1,624x) but only weakly the **deep** ones (SPYX/Raydium, a $1.67M pool at 56x), where the wallet structure carries the flag.

**The pattern recurs, and the wallets do not.** Re-sampled about six hours later, **not one** wash bot reappears: the fleet rotates wholesale. Three of the five pools are again actively washed (TSLAX 75%, QQQX 69%, one SPYX/Orca 41%); the other two sit below the flag at that moment. The activity comes in bursts by a rotating set of wallets, so a single window catches some pools mid-burst and others between - which is why any named wallet is point-in-time. The recurrence and the complete wallet turnover are the constants; `persistence.py` reproduces the comparison.

## The signature: matched buys and sells

Plot every wallet by the dollars it bought against the dollars it sold. A wallet that buys and sells in matched size sits on the diagonal; a directional trader sits off it.

{{< figure src="signature.png" alt="scatter of USD bought versus USD sold per wallet, wash bots on the diagonal in the flagged pools, organic wallets scattered" caption="USD bought versus USD sold per wallet. Left: the flagged xStock pools, where the wash bots (orange) sit on the buy=sell diagonal at $3k to $33k. Right: an organic control (WIF/JUP), where the busiest wallets are directional or one-sided, off the diagonal." loading="lazy" >}}

In the flagged pools the heavy wallets line up on the diagonal at five figures; in the organic control the busiest wallets sit off it, many pure buyers or pure sellers on the axes. On **QQQX/Raydium** the busiest wallet, [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF), bought **$10,657** and sold **$10,695**; on **TSLAX/Orca** four wallets each bought and sold near **$10,100**, their own buy and sell within a fraction of a percent. A wallet that buys and sells the same amount, in the same pool, ends essentially flat and captures no spread.

This is the place to rule out the benign readings, one at a time:

- **Not arbitrage.** Both legs execute in the same pool, and the bots capture no spread - their sells return about **99.3%** of what their buys cost (a sub-1% net loss to fees), not a profit.
- **Not aggregator routing.** Each flagged address is a plain keypair owned by the System Program (not a program-derived router), and each buy and sell is its own transaction signed and paid by that wallet, not a route-leg of one user's swap.
- **Not market-making, and not one lone two-sided wallet.** The claim does not rest on any single balanced wallet, which an active venue can produce; it rests on *coordinated fleets* - a TSLAX/Orca peel chain each funded into existence by the last, three SPYX wallets running identical buy/sell counts to the dollar, all on a near-perfect cadence. A market maker or lone trader is not deployed as an identical-parameter triplet, nor funded into existence by its predecessor.
- **Not a lookalike token.** Each flagged pool's base mint matches Backed's official xStock ([Solana Foundation](https://solana.com/news/case-study-xstocks)), trading at the shares' real prices (TSLAX near $401, SPYX near $750) - what is manufactured is volume, not a price.

What remains is a wallet whose only product is volume: small, similar-sized swaps (around 0.4 QQQX, about $300 each) bought and sold in equal number, so the token position returns to flat and the dollar position nearly so.

## The mechanism: a wallet that never takes a position

{{< figure src="cadence.png" alt="QQQX wallet alternates buy and sell perfectly across 72 swaps while its net position sawtooths and never leaves flat" caption="Wallet C6FyA84D on QQQX/Raydium. Top: 72 swaps in perfect buy/sell alternation. Bottom: cumulative net position, which sawtooths between zero and about $300 and never leaves flat. The wallet's trades net to no position; their economic effect is to add reported volume without the wallet holding the asset." loading="lazy" >}}

The wallet [`C6FyA84D...`](https://solscan.io/account/C6FyA84D6JLtkSSyq45gcLFi67P3q61FhGohEFYd9rvF) makes 72 swaps that **alternate buy, sell, buy, sell with no exception**, a median 17 seconds apart; its running position rises one clip on each buy and falls one on the next sell, a sawtooth that never leaves flat. Across the flagged pools each pool's busiest bot alternates 82% to 100% of the time (the QQQX leader perfectly), and in TSLAX/Orca three wallets run the same 4 to 5 second cadence back-to-back, one finishing as the next starts. Near-perfect alternation at a fixed cadence is a programmatic signature: a loop running `buy; sell; buy; sell`. The organic controls measured here contain no wallet that behaves this way.

## The coordination: a fleet built from one funding chain

Following each flagged wallet back to its first-ever transaction, through the free Solana RPC, shows how the fleet was built.

{{< figure src="funding.png" alt="the TSLAX wash wallets form a chain, each created and seeded by the previous one with a decreasing USDT amount" caption="The TSLAX/Orca fleet as a creation chain. Each wallet's first transaction was paid for by the previous wallet, which created its token account and seeded it with about 500 USDT. The seeds fall in near-constant ~4.5 USDT steps (522.22, 517.67, 513.34, 508.83, 504.48), the signature of automated sequential deployment." loading="lazy" >}}

In the **TSLAX/Orca** pool the five wash bots, plus the fresh wallet that seeded the first of them, form a single six-wallet chain: [`85zuUQ5w`](https://solscan.io/account/85zuUQ5wHER33mpqxezdaUNjnWfL6fhh5Z7wNnaURTjS) seeded [`8gw6JyEW`](https://solscan.io/account/8gw6JyEW1PGLzehpNeNU4jzb2iVtjet1o7YHQPxM1wmH) with **522.22 USDT**, which seeded [`FMMs8SGx`](https://solscan.io/account/FMMs8SGxukt4DRy1wKGyjCcovqj2xci16doZDPqc33eC) with **517.67**, which seeded [`AtzmNv2w`](https://solscan.io/account/AtzmNv2ww5iQRT5XytY43SKvTTeFPL3LdrFPeCiJDFAv) with **513.34**, which seeded [`vwbEYDGU`](https://solscan.io/account/vwbEYDGUamnFvhr9fq6RQnXK8Pn76okVw1kwgFPpt9Z) with **508.83**, which seeded [`2eTkWQyt`](https://solscan.io/account/2eTkWQyt9iGuXP5XUayXRdZuvLoHzDf48ZDjY8EWQUiF) with **504.48**. Each wallet's first transaction was *signed and paid for* by its predecessor, which created its USDT token account and funded it in the same transaction. Fresh wallets created in sequence, each seeded a near-constant step below the last, is the structure of an automated deployment, not of independent people choosing to trade the same pool. Two further patterns confirm coordination within the fleet:

- **Identical parameters.** In **SPYX/Raydium** three distinct wallets ([`78jkE1hP`](https://solscan.io/account/78jkE1hPATuzEnAY6ENpfuLf9DZYtXS5iqnmYAeFpFNY), [`3T1YdUjo`](https://solscan.io/account/3T1YdUjo2edVC1oJ6MK7HvKic2JKGs53GC4HuEV6U7rv), [`B2ES7Uzw`](https://solscan.io/account/B2ES7UzwvJTw2A7dX2WpvqQ7xxB58AkSbSp4WbE2eCqL)) each ran **7 buys and 7 sells of about $12,220**, to the dollar; in **TSLAX/Orca** three wallets each ran **21 buys and 21 sells of about $10,100** and a fourth ran 20 and 20.
- **Shared operation across pools.** Two wallets, [`6G11Z8xR`](https://solscan.io/account/6G11Z8xR1auD4mbgkh9minP9fQycKWdQcAJ7uxH8Smoz) and [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky), trade SPYX in **both** its Raydium and Orca pools.

The funding trace was depth-capped, so this analysis **does not** claim a single operator across all five pools, nor an exchange of origin. The strict serial peel chain is demonstrated for TSLAX/Orca; several other bots - including the largest, [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky) - trace to shared upstream funders, which a depth-capped trace cannot distinguish from a common exchange or bridge. What it does show, transaction by transaction, is that each flagged pool is run by a coordinated fleet - fresh or shared-funded wallets, several running identical parameters to the dollar, all on a robotic cadence - not by independent traders. This wallet-level coordination is the core of the finding and stands on its own, independent of any threshold or control comparison.

## It is the pool, not the token

The same tokens trade cleanly in their other pools. **QQQX** is flagged in its Raydium pool [`EibwWLHy`](https://solscan.io/account/EibwWLHyuFeqgU6fvi6g5FtDLF9kQsQL5HQXvcJNkDN8) (78% wash share) yet **clean** in a second Raydium pool of the identical mint (0 bots); **TSLAX** is flagged on Orca and **clean** on Raydium (score 0.06, below the flag). The token, its custodied share, and its contract are the same; only the pool differs - the activity concentrates plausibly where volume rankings or incentive programs most reward it.

## The scale

{{< figure src="manufactured.png" alt="measured in-window wash share per flagged pool with the matched-USD floor labelled" caption="The measured self-cancelling share of volume in each flagged pool's snapshot window (36% to 80%), with the matched-USD floor and window length labelled. The hard floor across all five is $467k of directly-observed matched volume; the 24h dollar figure is a wide extrapolation, discussed in the text." loading="lazy" >}}

Two figures bound the size, read differently. The **hard floor** is what the bots round-tripped inside the measured windows: **$467,000** of matched buy-and-sell that nets to nothing, directly observed. A **24-hour** figure assumes the snapshot behaviour persists, and the two natural extrapolations disagree sharply: each pool's *wash share* times its reported 24h volume gives ~$102M/day, while each pool's *observed bot rate* over 24 hours gives ~$32M/day. The gap is widest where the window is shortest - on QQQX the share method implies $26M against under $1M from its own snapshot rate, so that extrapolation is unreliable. The honest statement is a range, **$32M to $102M per day**, resting on persistence; rely on the $467k floor and the per-pool shares.

**The snapshot floor is a fraction of the lifetime total.** Following the fourteen wallets through their entire on-chain history (via Helius, classifying each transaction by net token-balance change, which catches DEX-aggregator swaps a per-pool tape only partly attributes) shows the snapshot caught a sliver. Across their full histories the fourteen have round-tripped **$5.6M** of matched stablecoin in **2,836** swaps, more than ten times the in-window floor; one wallet, [`HpcHy6dN`](https://solscan.io/account/HpcHy6dNYRotVim8HPpZmsfnekviyv1sRcX1C533SYky), cycled **$2.9M** across 912 swaps in QQQX, SPYX and TSLAX alike. The washing is not continuous - each wallet concentrates into a span of days, the widest **just over four days** - the same rotating, short-burst behaviour the persistence re-sample caught. That net-balance reconstruction also shows the per-pool tape is conservative: the true on-chain net runs about **1.3x** what the tape records, so the $467k floor, if anything, understates the wash.

It is also cheap, which is why it runs continuously. With no spread captured, the only cost is fees: the Solana base fee of **5,000 lamports (about $0.001) each, no priority fee** - ten thousand bot transactions cost about $10. The pool's swap fee dominates, and at the low fee tiers these pairs use it puts the cost of the entire $467k of in-window wash on the order of **tens to a low four figures of dollars**. Manufacturing a dollar of reported volume costs a small fraction of a cent.

For context, the baselines for fabricated volume are large: an oft-cited study puts more than 70% of volume on unregulated venues as non-economic ([Cong et al., *Crypto Wash Trading*, Management Science 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30783)), and Chainalysis - keying, like this detector, on matched buy/sell and multi-address funding clusters - attributed **$2.57B** of suspected wash trading to 2024 ([Chainalysis 2025](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/crypto-market-manipulation-wash-trading-pump-and-dump-2025/)). The DN wiki has flagged this marker family on centralized venues before ([Huobi 2023](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2023-08-14-huobi), [Senso 2021](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2021-01-05-Senso), a companion [Bybit study](https://github.com/1712n/dn-institute/tree/main/content/research/market-health/posts/2026-06-13-bybit-wct-lmwr)); prior academic work named wash wallets on DEXs generally ([Victor and Weintraud](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.07001)). The contribution here carries that wallet-level, funding-chain tracing into the tokenized-equity asset class.

## Why volume would be manufactured here

The detector flags behaviour; motive is a separate question, and the data supports several explanations and proves none. The documented incentives for adding on-chain volume are: climbing the **volume-ranked listings** on DEX aggregators (DEX Screener, GeckoTerminal, Jupiter), where visibility follows reported volume; farming **liquidity-mining or points programs** on Solana DEXs that reward activity (Meteora's points season, Raydium farms); and making a young product **look liquid**, against a backdrop where xStocks' on-chain liquidity has been reported as [thin and weekend-fragile](https://blockeden.xyz/blog/2025/10/30/xstocks/). No xStocks-issuer program that pays a rebate per unit of on-chain volume is documented, and none is assumed here.

## Why it matters

Reported volume is the number people act on - a retail user choosing where to buy QQQX, an aggregator ranking pools by 24h activity, the product's own "deep, liquid, 24/7" pitch. When a pool shows tens of millions in daily volume and most of it, in the windows measured here, is a handful of wallets cycling stablecoins for no position, that signal is corrupted: the market looks more liquid than it is, and a real trader routed into it acts on a fabricated figure. The point cuts both ways for tokenized equities. Their pitch is transparency - every trade on a public ledger - and that same transparency is what lets the manufactured volume be isolated, counted, and named here, where a centralized order book would show only the inflated total. For a young real-world-asset category courting institutions and regulators, the very metrics used to judge its legitimacy, volume and liquidity, are the ones being inflated.

## How to read this

This is a flag on a pattern, not an accusation against a person. Solana addresses are pseudonymous, and although the wallets are demonstrably coordinated *with each other*, the analysis stops short of identifying who controls them. US law treats wash trading, transactions with no change in beneficial ownership entered to create a misleading appearance of activity, as manipulation under the Commodity Exchange Act and the securities laws ([CFTC](https://www.cftc.gov/LearnAndProtect/EducationCenter/CFTCGlossary/glossary_wxyz.html)); the matched same-wallet buy and sell shown here is consistent with no change in beneficial ownership, but **whether these wallets acted with the intent to mislead, the element that would make this wash trading in law, is not established here**. The regulatory status of tokenized equities is [actively contested](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/01/29/sec-clarifies-rules-for-tokenized-stocks-tightening-scrutiny-on-synthetic-equity), and xStocks are not offered to US persons. None of that is adjudicated here. What is shown is that five Solana xStock pools carry a large share of volume that, by its own on-chain record, is bought and sold in matched size by a coordinated fleet of automated wallets and nets to no position. The named wallets are the active set as of the snapshot and the fleet rotates members over time; but unlike a centralized tape, every statement here is independently checkable - paste any wallet below into [solscan.io](https://solscan.io) and read the manufactured volume off the chain yourself.

## Methodology and reproducibility

The companion repository, [github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis](https://github.com/mkzung/solana-xstocks-wash-analysis), reproduces every figure and number from a committed snapshot, with CI that reruns the full pipeline. To regenerate from the raw dumps: clone the repository (tagged at the commit released with this article), `pip install -r requirements.txt`, then run the analysis scripts and `verify.py` (or let CI do it); the committed snapshot makes the run deterministic and key-less.

- **Data, free.** Pool universe and turnover from **Dexscreener**; tx-level swaps (wallet, hash, side, USD) and OHLCV from **GeckoTerminal** (most-recent ~300 swaps per pool, no deep pagination); funding traces from the public **Solana RPC** - all key-less. The fourteen bots' full lifetime histories (used only for the lifetime totals in The Scale) come from the **Helius** enhanced-transactions API (free tier), classified by each wallet's net token-balance change and committed to the repo so the totals recompute without a key. The snapshot (2026-06-21) and a second sample taken six hours later (for the persistence check) are both committed, so the analysis is deterministic. The funding trace walks each wallet to its earliest retrievable transaction; for the published chains every history fits in one page, so the origin is the true first transaction.
- **Detector and metric mapping** (`metrics_lib.py`). The wash-bot rule extends the DN [market-health metrics](https://dn.institute/market-health/docs/market-health-metrics/) from the pool level to the wallet level, because the pool-level metrics do not separate wash from organic on these pools: pool `volumedist` (circular volume) and the pool `buysellratio` both run high on the liquid organic controls too (WIF and JUP score 74% and 38% circular over a short window). The detector is therefore defined per wallet. The matched buy/sell test is the count-and-USD `buysellratio` applied to a single wallet (`min(buys, sells) >= 5` with total buy and sell USD within 10% of each other, the count-based ratio rather than the volume-weighted `buysellratioabs`); the cadence check is `timeoftrade` at the wallet level (near-fixed inter-swap spacing). First-digit (`firstdigitdist`/`benfordlawtest`) is not used here: at the wallet level the signal is the matched two-sided structure, not the trade-size distribution.
- **Pipeline.** Network collectors run once to build the committed snapshot: `fetch_raw.py` (Dexscreener + GeckoTerminal), `fund_trace.py`/`trace_tree.py` (funding origins via Solana RPC), `wallet_history.py` (each bot's full Helius history), `owner_check.py` (the System-Program-keypair check, committed for offline re-check). The deterministic analysis, which CI reruns over the committed data on every push: `screen.py` (venue screen, controls, sub-window confound check), `cluster.py` (named bots, creation chains, sibling fleets), `temporal.py` (cadence, floor, both 24h extrapolations), `persistence.py`, `lifetime.py`, `named_wallets.py`, `figures.py`, and `verify.py` - an independent recomputation of every headline claim from the committed data - plus a `pytest` suite.
- **Limitations**, stated plainly. The trade snapshot is the most-recent ~300 swaps per pool (a sub-hour window in the busy pools), so wash *shares* are in-window intensities and the daily dollar figure is the labelled extrapolation, not a measurement. A precise *onset* cannot be dated: the free OHLCV history is too sparse for a per-pool onset curve, and a wallet's age is not its wash onset (the aged wallets show unrelated activity for months first); the wallet dates establish only that the operation is older than the pools. The funding trace is depth-capped, establishing co-deployment within each pool - a serial peel chain for TSLAX/Orca, shared upstream funders elsewhere - not a single operator across all five. The five flagged pools are also the highest-volume in the universe, and only pools with at least 150 snapshot swaps are scored, so "five of sixteen" is five of the sixteen scored pools, not a census; the low-volume tail is unscored, not shown clean.
