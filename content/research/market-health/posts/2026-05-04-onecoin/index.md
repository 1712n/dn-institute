## Summary

1. **OneCoin was a fraudulent cryptocurrency scheme** that operated from 2014 to 2017, raising approximately $4 billion from investors in over 175 countries. Unlike most crypto frauds that manipulate a real token on a real blockchain, OneCoin had no blockchain at all — the "cryptocurrency" existed only as numbers in a centralized SQL database controlled by the company.
2. **The scheme was structured as a multi-level marketing (MLM) pyramid** where investors purchased "educational packages" costing between EUR 100 and EUR 118,000. Each package included tokens that could be used to "mine" OneCoin. The mining was entirely simulated — the company simply assigned coin balances to user accounts at predetermined rates.
3. **Price manipulation was absolute** because there was no open market. OneCoin's "price" was set manually by the company and only ever increased, rising from EUR 0.50 at launch in January 2015 to EUR 29.95 by January 2019. Since there was no exchange, no order book, and no blockchain, the price was a fiction maintained in the company's internal system.
4. **Founder Ruja Ignatova disappeared in October 2017** and was placed on the FBI's Ten Most Wanted Fugitives list in June 2022 — the first woman on the list in over a decade. She remains at large as of 2026. Co-founder Karl Sebastian Greenwood pleaded guilty and was sentenced to 20 years in federal prison in September 2023.
5. **The DOJ described OneCoin as one of the largest international fraud schemes ever prosecuted.** Multiple jurisdictions (US, Germany, UK, China, India, Bulgaria) have pursued criminal, civil, or regulatory actions against OneCoin entities and promoters.

## Background

OneCoin was founded in 2014 by Dr. Ruja Ignatova, a Bulgarian-German national with a background in management consulting (McKinsey), and Karl Sebastian Greenwood, a Swedish national. The company was incorporated in multiple jurisdictions, with key entities including OneCoin Ltd (Dubai and Bulgaria), OneLife Network Ltd (Belize), and various national subsidiaries.

The project was marketed through a global MLM network as the "Bitcoin killer" — a cryptocurrency that would replace Bitcoin with superior technology, faster transactions, and mainstream adoption. Ignatova presented at large events in arenas and convention centers, drawing thousands of attendees.

The core product was not the cryptocurrency itself but "educational packages" about cryptocurrency and financial markets. These packages were tiered:

| Package Name | Price (EUR) | OneCoins Included | "Mining" Tokens |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Starter | 100 | 1,000 | 100 |
| Trader | 500 | 5,000 | 500 |
| Pro Trader | 1,000 | 10,000 | 1,000 |
| Executive Trader | 5,000 | 50,000 | 5,000 |
| Tycoon Trader | 25,000 | 250,000 | 25,000 |
| Premium Trader | 118,000 | 1,180,000 | 118,000 |

The tiered structure is a hallmark of MLM pyramid schemes: higher investment levels promised disproportionately higher returns, and participants earned commissions for recruiting new members.

## 🌰 The Fabricated Blockchain

### No Blockchain Existed

The most fundamental fraud in OneCoin was that the cryptocurrency it sold did not exist on any real public blockchain. DOJ filings and trial evidence established:

- **No distributed ledger**: OneCoin balances were stored in a centralized database rather than a verifiable peer-to-peer network. There was no public mining process, no independent block validation, and no cryptographic hash chain comparable to a real cryptocurrency.
- **No consensus mechanism**: Because there was no blockchain, there was no proof-of-work, proof-of-stake, or any other consensus protocol. The company simply updated account balances in its database.
- **No independent verification**: Unlike Bitcoin, where anyone can run a node and verify the chain, OneCoin's "blockchain" was completely opaque. Users could only see their balance through the company's website portal.

Internal communications obtained by prosecutors showed that OneCoin's technical team was aware there was no blockchain. In one email excerpt cited in court filings, a technical staff member wrote about generating "fake blockchain" data to show to auditors.

### Simulated Mining

OneCoin's "mining" process worked as follows:

1. Users purchased educational packages containing "tokens"
2. Tokens were submitted to the OneCoin system to "mine" coins
3. The system assigned coins to user accounts at a fixed, predetermined rate
4. The "difficulty" of mining periodically increased (requiring more tokens per coin), creating artificial scarcity and urgency to buy higher-tier packages

This process had no computational basis. In real cryptocurrency mining, miners expend energy to solve cryptographic puzzles and validate transactions. In OneCoin's system, the company simply ran a database query to credit accounts. The mining "difficulty" was a marketing tool to pressure faster purchases.

## Price Manipulation

### Manually Set Price

OneCoin's "market price" was not determined by supply and demand on any exchange. The company set the price through its internal system:

| Date | OneCoin Price (EUR) | Event |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Jan 2015 | 0.50 | Launch price |
| Jun 2015 | 2.00 | First "appreciation event" |
| Oct 2015 | 4.45 | — |
| Mar 2016 | 5.95 | — |
| Sep 2016 | 6.95 | — |
| Oct 2016 | 7.35 | — |
| Jun 2017 | 14.90 | — |
| Oct 2017 | 25.35 | Ignatova disappears |
| Jan 2019 | 29.95 | Final recorded price |

Key manipulation characteristics:

- **Monotonic increase**: The price only ever went up. In four years, there was not a single recorded price decline. This is impossible in any genuine market with two-sided trading.
- **Round-number pricing**: Prices consistently landed on psychologically appealing round numbers, indicating manual setting rather than market discovery.
- **No correlation with crypto markets**: OneCoin's price showed zero correlation with Bitcoin or the broader cryptocurrency market. During the 2017 crypto bull market and subsequent 2018 crash, OneCoin's stated price continued to climb steadily.

### The xcoinx Exchange Facade

OneCoin operated a nominal exchange called "xcoinx" where users could supposedly trade their coins. In practice:

- **Trading was heavily restricted**: Users could only sell small amounts per day, and the system frequently suspended trading entirely
- **No external access**: The exchange was accessible only to OneCoin members, preventing any external price discovery
- **Withdrawal limitations**: Converting OneCoin to fiat or other cryptocurrencies was extremely difficult; users reported wait times of months for withdrawal requests
- **Matched orders**: The closed venue created the appearance of matched trades without a transparent external counterparty market

The xcoinx exchange served the same function as Bitconnect's internal exchange: it created an illusion of liquidity and tradability while preventing real price discovery that would reveal the token's worthlessness.

## 🌰 MLM Structure and Fund Flow

### Pyramid Economics

OneCoin's revenue came from two sources:

1. **Package sales**: New investors purchasing educational packages (the primary revenue stream)
2. **Recruitment commissions**: Existing members earning commissions for recruiting new purchasers

The commission structure was multi-tiered:

- **Direct referral bonus**: 10% of the recruit's package purchase price
- **Residual commissions**: 5-25% across multiple levels of downstream recruits (depth varied by the promoter's own package level)
- **Leadership bonuses**: Top promoters earned additional bonuses based on their network's total volume

This is the classic Ponzi/pyramid structure: funds from new investors pay returns to existing investors. The "educational packages" were the legal facade — their content was generic, freely available cryptocurrency information repackaged to justify the price tags.

### Fund Disposition

According to DOJ filings and public case reporting, the approximately $4 billion in investor funds was used for:

- **Promoter commissions**: A substantial share of funds was paid as MLM commissions to the recruitment network
- **Operating expenses**: Company operations, events, marketing materials, and staff
- **Personal enrichment**: Ignatova and Greenwood diverted substantial funds to personal use, including luxury real estate, yacht charters, and other assets
- **No genuine crypto investment activity**: There was no real blockchain-based trading operation or product activity capable of generating the marketed returns

## Wash Trading Parallels

While OneCoin did not have a real blockchain or exchange where traditional wash trading could occur, the scheme exhibited analogous manipulation patterns:

### Fabricated Volume

- **Self-reported metrics**: OneCoin claimed millions of daily transactions and billions in market capitalization. These numbers were entirely fabricated since there was no blockchain to verify against.
- **Event-driven volume claims**: During promotional events, OneCoin would announce record "transaction volumes" and "mining output" — all generated by database operations, not market activity.

### Artificial Scarcity

- **Mining difficulty increases**: Periodic increases in "mining difficulty" created urgency without any computational basis, mimicking the scarcity narrative of legitimate cryptocurrencies
- **Supply cap announcements**: OneCoin announced a maximum supply of 120 billion coins, later reduced to 2.1 billion after a "coin split" — arbitrary changes that real blockchain projects cannot make unilaterally

### Price Guarantee

- **No downside risk presentation**: Because the price was manually set to always increase, OneCoin could present a flawless track record to prospective investors. In genuine markets, historical price charts include volatility and drawdowns.
- **Comparison to Bitcoin's early price**: OneCoin's promotional materials compared its price trajectory to Bitcoin's 2010-2013 appreciation, suggesting investors were "getting in early" on an equivalent opportunity.

## Regulatory and Legal Outcomes

### United States

- **Ruja Ignatova**: Indicted March 2019 on wire fraud, securities fraud, and money laundering charges. Added to FBI Ten Most Wanted Fugitives list June 2022. Reward of up to $5 million for information. Remains a fugitive as of 2026.
- **Karl Sebastian Greenwood**: Pleaded guilty to racketeering conspiracy and wire fraud in December 2022. Sentenced to 20 years in federal prison in September 2023. Ordered to forfeit $300 million.
- **Mark Scott**: OneCoin's money launderer, a former Locke Lord LLP attorney. Convicted at trial in November 2019 of conspiracy to commit bank fraud and conspiracy to commit money laundering. Sentenced to 10 years in federal prison in January 2024 after laundering approximately $400 million through Fenero Funds.
- **Irina Dilkinska**: Head of legal and compliance for OneCoin. Pleaded guilty to wire fraud conspiracy and money laundering conspiracy in November 2023. Sentenced to 4 years in federal prison in April 2024 after helping create shell companies and move funds.

### Germany

- BaFin, Germany's financial regulator, issued warnings and orders against OneCoin activity. German prosecutors and courts also pursued OneCoin-linked operators and promoters.

### China

- Chinese authorities shut down OneCoin operations in 2017 and arrested local promoters. Chinese investors accounted for a significant portion of the scheme's total victims.

### India

- Indian law enforcement arrested multiple OneCoin promoters in 2017 and 2018. The Enforcement Directorate (ED) filed money laundering cases against local organizers.

## Lessons for Market Surveillance

OneCoin demonstrates manipulation patterns that should trigger investigation in any purported cryptocurrency project:

1. **Blockchain verification**: Any project claiming to operate a cryptocurrency should have a publicly verifiable blockchain explorer. If the only way to check balances is through the project's own website, the "blockchain" may not exist. Independent third-party audits of blockchain integrity should be a baseline requirement for exchange listings and aggregator inclusion.

2. **Price monotonicity**: A cryptocurrency whose price has never declined over a period exceeding 6 months is exhibiting a manipulation signature. Genuine markets experience corrections. A perfectly ascending price chart — especially one with round-number price points — indicates the price is being set rather than discovered.

3. **Exchange exclusivity**: If a token can only be traded on an exchange operated by the token's creators, and that exchange restricts external access, withdrawals, and trading volume, the exchange is likely functioning as a facade rather than a genuine marketplace.

4. **MLM commission structures**: Cryptocurrency projects that pay multi-tiered recruitment commissions (especially >5% across multiple levels) are structurally dependent on new investor inflows rather than technology value. The commission structure itself reveals that the project's primary "product" is recruitment, not technology.

5. **Educational package bundling**: Packaging a cryptocurrency purchase inside an "educational product" is a common regulatory arbitrage technique. If the education is generic and freely available elsewhere, and the package price far exceeds the education's market value, the package is a securities offering disguised as an information product.

6. **Withdrawal restriction patterns**: Projects that impose escalating withdrawal restrictions — daily limits, "processing delays," identity verification beyond regulatory requirements — are typically managing liquidity for a Ponzi scheme, not implementing compliance controls.
