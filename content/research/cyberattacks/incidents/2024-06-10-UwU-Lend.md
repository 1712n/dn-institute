## Summary

On June 10, 2024, UwU Lend, a DeFi lending protocol launched by Frog Nation's former CFO [Sifu](https://x.com/0xSifu), [was exploited for $19.4 million](https://x.com/CyversAlerts/status/1800139071857316328) through an oracle manipulation attack. The attacker, funded via Tornado Cash, executed three transactions within six minutes to drain the protocol. UwU Lend is a fork of AAVE V2, but its team modified the oracle fallback logic, introducing a critical vulnerability. The fallback oracle calculated prices based on the state of several Curve Finance pools. The attacker utilized flash loans to make large trades that manipulated these pool states, creating a price discrepancy that allowed them to borrow sUSDe at a rate of 0.99 and then liquidate positions at an artificially inflated rate of 1.03. The stolen funds, converted to WBTC, DAI, and ETH, were distributed across two Ethereum addresses. Among the hardest-hit depositors was [Michael Egorov](https://x.com/newmichwill), founder of Curve Finance, who lost over 23.5 million CRV tokens worth approximately $9.85 million. UwU Lend [acknowledged the exploit roughly one hour after detection](https://x.com/UwU_Lend/status/1800159455767843009) and paused the protocol.

## Losses

UwU Lend suffered a total loss of approximately $19.4 million in various assets including WBTC, DAI, and ETH. Among the notable victims:

- **Michael Egorov (Curve Finance founder):** Lost over *23.5 million CRV tokens* (~$9.85M), which the attacker deposited into Curve's Llama Lend to borrow over *8 million crvUSD* (~$8.11M).

In a silver lining, the hacker's CRV position in Llama Lend was [fully hard-liquidated](https://x.com/0xcrv_hub/status/1800270842086986184) thanks to diligent lenders in crvUSD's CRV market who repaid the debt, proving the robustness of the Curve Llama Lend platform.

- **June 10, 2024:** The attacker deposited stolen CRV tokens into [Curve's Llama Lend](https://x.com/CurveFinance/status/1800269737563451705) and borrowed over 8 million crvUSD against them.

## Security Failure Causes

**Flawed Oracle Design:** The core vulnerability was UwU Lend's modified oracle fallback logic. While the protocol was a fork of AAVE V2, the team changed the fallback oracle to calculate prices based on the state of several Curve Finance pools. This design made the price feed susceptible to manipulation through large trades, as [identified by Nick Franklin's analysis](https://x.com/0xNickLFranklin/status/1800184254481277324). The attacker could manipulate the pool states via flash loans, creating a price discrepancy that allowed borrowing at 0.99 and liquidating at 1.03.

**Reliance on DEX-Based Price Feeds:** Using on-chain DEX liquidity pool states as a price oracle is inherently risky, as these can be temporarily manipulated through flash loans. The protocol's choice to use Curve pool states as a fallback oracle, rather than more manipulation-resistant oracle solutions like Chainlink price feeds with time-weighted average prices, created the attack surface that was exploited.

**Audit Limitations:** UwU Lend was [audited by PeckShield](https://github.com/peckshield/publications/blob/master/audit_reports/PeckShield-Audit-Report-UWU-v1.0.pdf), which [characterized the code](https://medium.com/uwu-lend/peckshield-publishes-exemplary-audit-of-uwu-lend-code-7c8ddfe002d7) as "well designed and engineered" with "no high-severity or critical issues" detected. Despite this assessment, the critical oracle vulnerability went unidentified, demonstrating that security audits may not catch all business logic vulnerabilities, particularly when modifications are made to forked codebases.
