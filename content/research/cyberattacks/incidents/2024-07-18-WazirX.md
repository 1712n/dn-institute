## Summary

On July 18, 2024, [WazirX](https://wazirx.com/), one of India's largest cryptocurrency exchanges, [suffered a cyberattack](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/) resulting in the theft of over $235 million in digital assets from a multisig wallet. The compromised wallet was a [Gnosis Safe multisig wallet](https://www.halborn.com/blog/post/explained-the-wazirx-hack-july-2024) using a 4-of-6 signature scheme, with five keys held by WazirX and one key held by Liminal, a digital asset custody provider. The attacker exploited a discrepancy between the Liminal interface display and the actual transaction data to trick the four required signers into approving a malicious payload that [replaced the wallet's implementation contract](https://cointelegraph.com/news/235m-wazirx-crypto-exchange-hack-india) with an attacker-deployed smart contract — effectively bypassing both the multisig requirement and the address whitelist. Blockchain analysts [attributed the attack to North Korean threat actors](https://www.halborn.com/blog/post/explained-the-wazirx-hack-july-2024) based on on-chain laundering patterns. The attack deeply impacted WazirX's ability to maintain 1:1 collateral backing, and the company subsequently entered a restructuring process through a Scheme of Arrangement in Singapore.

## Attackers

The identity of the attacker has not been officially confirmed. Blockchain analytics firm Elliptic and independent on-chain researcher ZachXBT attributed the attack to North Korea-linked threat actors based on laundering patterns consistent with the Lazarus Group. The malicious implementation contract was deployed eight days before the attack:

- Attacker address (Ethereum): [0x04b21735E93Fa3f8df70e2Da89e6922616891a88](https://etherscan.io/address/0x04b21735E93Fa3f8df70e2Da89e6922616891a88)
- Malicious implementation contract: deployed on July 10, 2024
- Compromised WazirX multisig wallet: [0x27fD43BABfbe83a81d14665b1a6fB8030A60C9b4](https://etherscan.io/address/0x27fD43BABfbe83a81d14665b1a6fB8030A60C9b4)

## Losses

The [total stolen assets exceeded $235 million](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/), including:

- ~$96.7 million in SHIB (5.43 trillion tokens)
- ~$52.6 million in ETH
- ~$11 million in MATIC
- ~$7.6 million in PEPE
- ~$5.4 million in USDT
- ~$4.7 million in GALA
- Remaining assets included FLOKI, CHZ, AUDIO, COTI, and other ERC-20 tokens

## Timeline

- **July 10, 2024:** The attacker [deployed the malicious smart contract](https://cointelegraph.com/news/235m-wazirx-crypto-exchange-hack-india) on Ethereum that would later replace the multisig wallet's implementation.
- **July 18, 2024, ~12:30 PM IST:** The attacker submitted a transaction that appeared benign in the [Liminal custody interface](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/) but contained a malicious payload. Four signers (3 WazirX + 1 Liminal) approved the transaction, which replaced the wallet's implementation contract with the attacker's malicious version.
- **July 18, 2024:** Once the implementation was swapped, the attacker drained the multisig wallet of over $235 million in various tokens.
- **July 18, 2024:** WazirX [detected the breach and suspended all withdrawals](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/) and trading activity.
- **July 18, 2024:** On-chain researcher ZachXBT [identified the attack and initial fund movements](https://x.com/zachxbt/status/1813973533250494863), noting laundering patterns consistent with North Korean threat actors.
- **July 25, 2024:** WazirX [published a preliminary post-mortem](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/) acknowledging the breach and outlining initial recovery steps.
- **August 2024:** The stolen funds were laundered through Tornado Cash and other mixing protocols.
- **October 13, 2025:** The Singapore High Court [sanctioned WazirX's Scheme of Arrangement](https://wazirx.com/blog/wazirx-cyber-attack-day-wise-report/) for creditor recovery, allowing the platform to restart operations.

## Security Failure Causes

- **Interface Discrepancy Exploitation:** The attacker exploited a [discrepancy between how transactions appeared in the Liminal custody interface and the actual on-chain transaction data](https://cointelegraph.com/news/wazirx-230m-breach-post-mortem). The signers approved what appeared to be a legitimate transaction, but the payload included a call to replace the wallet's implementation contract — a technique known as a proxy upgrade attack.
- **Insufficient Transaction Verification:** Despite using hardware wallets (Ledger devices) for key storage, the signing process relied on the Liminal interface for transaction preview. The signers did not independently verify the raw transaction data against the on-chain payload, allowing the discrepancy to go undetected.
- **Single Point of Trust in Custody Provider:** The multisig design distributed key custody across WazirX and Liminal, but the transaction construction and display were controlled by Liminal's interface. This created a single point of trust — if the interface could be manipulated (or if its display logic was flawed), the multisig offered limited protection.
