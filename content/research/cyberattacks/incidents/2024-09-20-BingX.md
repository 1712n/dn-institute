## Summary

On September 20, 2024, BingX, a Singapore-based centralized cryptocurrency exchange, [suffered a major security breach](https://x.com/BingXOfficial/status/1836938477570773470) resulting in the theft of approximately $44.7 million from its hot wallets. The attack targeted hot wallets across multiple blockchain networks, including Ethereum, BNB Chain, Polygon, Arbitrum, Avalanche, and others. Blockchain investigator [Tayvano first detected suspicious outflows](https://x.com/tayvano_/status/1836921036367089967) from BingX wallets, followed by [PeckShield confirming a $13.6 million suspicious outflow](https://x.com/peckshield/status/1836927530449916132). The attacker used at least ten addresses to drain funds and rapidly swapped stolen altcoins into ETH and BNB before consolidating them. Security analysts from Cyvers noted that the attacker's behavior was [consistent with tactics used by North Korea's Lazarus Group](https://cryptoslate.com/north-korea-linked-hack-costs-crypto-exchange-bingx-52-million-in-crypto-assets/) in prior exchange hacks. BingX initially described the incident as a "minor asset loss" and claimed the majority of funds were stored safely in cold wallets.

## Losses

BingX suffered a total loss of approximately [$44.7 million](https://x.com/SlowMist_Team/status/1837062653904310344) across multiple blockchain networks. The stolen assets spanned various tokens including ETH, BNB, USDT, and numerous altcoins that were quickly swapped by the attacker. The attack impacted wallets on Ethereum, BNB Chain, Polygon, Arbitrum, Avalanche, and other networks.

## Timeline

- **September 20, 2024, ~04:00 AM SGT:** BingX's team detected ["abnormal network access"](https://x.com/Vivien_BingX/status/1836940851873992845) on their hot wallet, suspecting a hacker attack.
- **September 20, 2024:** [Tayvano first flagged](https://x.com/tayvano_/status/1836921036367089967) suspicious outflows from BingX addresses, listing multiple exploiter addresses.
- **September 20, 2024:** [PeckShield alerted BingX](https://x.com/peckshield/status/1836927530449916132) to a suspicious $13.6 million outflow from their wallets.
- **September 20, 2024:** BingX published a ["Temporary Wallet Maintenance Notice"](https://x.com/BingXOfficial/status/1836938477570773470) roughly an hour after the initial community alerts.
- **September 20, 2024:** BingX Chief Product Officer Vivien Lin [confirmed the attack](https://x.com/Vivien_BingX/status/1836940851873992845), describing it as a "minor asset loss" and stating that the emergency plan had been activated, including asset transfers and withdrawal suspension.
- **September 20, 2024:** [Cyvers reported the total damage at $44.7 million](https://x.com/CyversAlerts/status/1837004902049927248) across multiple chains. [SlowMist confirmed the figure](https://x.com/SlowMist_Team/status/1837062653904310344).
- **September 20, 2024:** BingX promised to restore withdrawals within 24 hours and [hinted at a compensation plan](https://x.com/Vivien_BingX/status/1836940851873992845) for affected users.
- **September 20, 2024:** Blockchain investigator [ZachXBT criticized BingX's security practices](https://x.com/zachxbt/status/1837128094555177306), noting the irony of BingX being unhelpful to victims of pig butchering scams and thefts that utilized the platform.

## Security Failure Causes

**Hot Wallet Compromise:** The exact root cause of the breach was not publicly disclosed by BingX. The attackers gained unauthorized access to BingX's hot wallets across multiple blockchain networks, suggesting either a compromise of private keys, a breach of the exchange's internal infrastructure, or a sophisticated phishing attack targeting key personnel. The multi-chain, multi-address nature of the attack indicated a well-planned and coordinated operation.

**Inadequate Hot Wallet Security:** The sheer volume of assets stored in hot wallets raised questions about BingX's wallet management practices. The use of at least ten exploiter addresses to drain funds across multiple chains simultaneously suggests the attackers had deep access to the exchange's wallet infrastructure.

**Potential Lazarus Group Involvement:** Security analysts from [Cyvers noted that the attacker's behavior](https://cryptoslate.com/north-korea-linked-hack-costs-crypto-exchange-bingx-52-million-in-crypto-assets/) — using multiple wallets to swap altcoins into ETH and BNB before consolidating — was consistent with tactics observed in past Lazarus Group operations, a North Korean state-sponsored hacking group known for targeting cryptocurrency exchanges.
