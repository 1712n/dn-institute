## Summary

On February 21, 2025, Bybit, one of the world's largest centralized cryptocurrency exchanges, was the victim of the [largest crypto hack in history](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), losing approximately $1.46 billion in digital assets from its Ethereum cold wallet. The attackers executed a sophisticated front-end spoofing attack that deceived multisig signers into approving a malicious transaction disguised as a routine cold-to-hot wallet transfer. The transaction replaced the Safe{Wallet} multisig's smart contract implementation with a malicious version containing a hidden `sweepERC20()` function, granting the attackers full control over the wallet. [ZachXBT linked the attack to North Korea's Lazarus Group](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) within hours, and [SEAL (Security Alliance) issued an advisory](https://www.securityalliance.org/news/2025-02-dprk-advisory) attributing the attack to the TraderTraitor threat actor, a DPRK-nexus group tracked by [CISA](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories/aa22-108a). The attack methodology closely mirrored previous incidents against [WazirX and Radiant Capital](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), where multisig wallets were compromised without private key theft. Safe{Wallet}'s front-end infrastructure was compromised via a supply chain attack on a Safe{Wallet} developer's machine, enabling the injection of malicious JavaScript that altered transaction data shown to Bybit's signers. Despite the enormous loss, Bybit CEO Ben Zhou stated the exchange remained solvent and could cover all customer losses.

## Attackers

The attack was attributed to the **Lazarus Group** (also tracked as TraderTraitor, APT38, BlueNoroff), a North Korean state-sponsored hacking organization. [ZachXBT solved Arkham Intelligence's bounty](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) linking the attack to Lazarus Group within four hours of the hack, through analysis of test transactions, connected wallets, and timing analyses. [SEAL confirmed](https://www.securityalliance.org/news/2025-02-dprk-advisory) that TraderTraitor was responsible, noting the group "has compromised countless crypto exchanges in recent years." The FBI later formally identified Lazarus Group cyber actors as responsible for the theft of $1.5 billion from Bybit.

## Losses

Bybit lost approximately **$1.46 billion** from its Ethereum cold wallet. The stolen assets included:

- **401,346 ETH** (~$1.11 billion)
- **90,375 stETH** (~$250.8 million)
- **15,000 cmETH** (~$44 million)
- **8,000 mETH** (~$23.5 million)

According to [BitMEX Research](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), roughly 75% of Bybit's ETH user deposits were stolen in the attack.

## Timeline

- **February 19, 2025:** Attackers [conducted dry-run test transactions](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) two days prior to the attack, as identified by Meir Dolev, CTO of Cyvers.
- **February 21, 2025:** The attackers deployed a malicious smart contract implementation containing a hidden `sweepERC20()` function and presented a masked transaction to Bybit's multisig signers for approval.
- **February 21, 2025:** Bybit's multisig signers approved what appeared to be a legitimate cold-to-hot wallet transfer. The [masked UI showed the correct address and a Safe URL](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), according to Bybit CEO Ben Zhou.
- **February 21, 2025:** Immediately after Ben Zhou signed, the attackers executed the malicious [sweep transaction](https://etherscan.io/tx/0xb61413c495fdad6114a7aa863a00b2e3c28945979a10885b12b30316ea9f072c) themselves, draining 401,346 ETH in a single transaction.
- **February 21, 2025:** [ZachXBT first reported suspicious outflows](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) from Bybit on his Telegram channel. Within minutes, [SlowMist](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) and [PeckShield](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) confirmed the breach.
- **February 21, 2025:** Bybit [officially confirmed the hack](https://announcements.bybit.com/en/article/incident-update---eth-cold-wallet-incident-blt292c0454d26e9140/), stating only the Ethereum cold wallet was affected.
- **February 21, 2025:** Bybit CEO Ben Zhou [announced that Bybit remained solvent](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) and could cover the full loss, with bridge loans from partners 80% committed.
- **February 21, 2025:** [ZachXBT solved Arkham Intelligence's bounty](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) by linking the attack to the Lazarus Group.
- **February 21, 2025:** [SEAL issued a DPRK advisory](https://www.securityalliance.org/news/2025-02-dprk-advisory) confirming TraderTraitor was responsible and providing security recommendations for crypto exchanges.
- **February 21, 2025:** The attackers [began splitting stolen funds](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) across 40+ wallet addresses — 10,000 ETH to 39 addresses, then another 10,000 ETH to 9 more addresses.

## Security Failure Causes

- **Supply Chain Compromise of Safe{Wallet}:** The attackers compromised a Safe{Wallet} developer's machine and injected malicious code into the Safe{Wallet} front-end. This [supply chain attack](https://www.securityalliance.org/news/2025-02-dprk-advisory) allowed the manipulation of transaction data displayed to Bybit's signers. The compromised front-end showed a legitimate-looking interface while masking the true malicious transaction underneath.

- **Blind Signing Vulnerability:** Bybit's multisig signers relied on the UI to verify transaction details rather than independently verifying the on-chain transaction data. As [security researcher Nanak Nihal explained](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), "There is a name for this and it's BLIND SIGNING. Please please please stop using hardware wallets and multisigs and thinking you are safe." Hardware wallets display limited transaction information, making it nearly impossible for signers to detect the discrepancy between the displayed and actual transactions.

- **Lack of Dedicated Signing Devices:** The signers used their daily-use computers to approve transactions, making them vulnerable to malware and compromised browser environments. [Security experts recommended](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) using isolated devices solely for transaction signing, kept offline except when needed, running secure operating systems. As [one commenter noted](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/), "Having a separate laptop will get you 99.99% of the way there."

- **Repeated DPRK Attack Pattern Not Addressed:** This attack used the [same methodology as previous hacks](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/) against WazirX ($235M), Radiant Capital, and DMM Bitcoin — compromising multisig wallets without stealing private keys. Security researcher Tayvano [warned](https://rekt.news/bybit-rekt/): "They've done this 5 times now. Please start taking it seriously." The crypto industry failed to adopt sufficient countermeasures despite known DPRK tactics documented by [CISA since 2022](https://www.cisa.gov/news-events/cybersecurity-advisories/aa22-108a).
