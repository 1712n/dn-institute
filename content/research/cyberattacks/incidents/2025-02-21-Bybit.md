## Summary

On February 21, 2025, Bybit, one of the world's largest cryptocurrency exchanges, [was exploited for approximately $1.5 billion](https://www.elliptic.co/blog/bybit-hacked-1.5-billion-stolen) in what became the largest cryptocurrency hack in history. The attack targeted Bybit's Ethereum cold wallet infrastructure, specifically exploiting the Safe{Wallet} multisig interface used to manage the exchange's cold storage. The attacker, later attributed to North Korea's Lazarus Group, compromised the Safe{Wallet} front-end infrastructure to inject malicious transaction data during a routine transfer from the cold wallet to a warm wallet. The manipulated transaction appeared legitimate to the signers but actually contained a delegatecall to a malicious implementation contract that altered the wallet's logic, granting the attacker full control over approximately 401,347 ETH and various liquid staking tokens. Following the exploit, the stolen funds were rapidly laundered through a network of intermediary addresses and cross-chain bridges.

## Attackers

The attack has been attributed to the **Lazarus Group**, a North Korean state-sponsored hacking organization also known as APT38. The FBI [confirmed the attribution](https://www.fbi.gov/news/press-releases/fbi-identifies-lazarus-group-cyber-actors-as-responsible-for-theft-of-1.5-billion-from-bybit) in a public statement. The group has been responsible for numerous previous cryptocurrency thefts, including the $625 million Ronin Network hack in 2022 and the $100 million Harmony Bridge hack in 2022.

Attacker addresses:
- *0x47666Fab8bd0Ac7003bce3f5C3585383F09486E2* - [Primary collection address](https://etherscan.io/address/0x47666Fab8bd0Ac7003bce3f5C3585383F09486E2)

## Losses

Bybit suffered a total loss of approximately **$1.5 billion** in digital assets:
- *401,347 ETH* (approximately $1.12 billion at time of hack)
- *90,376 stETH* (Lido Staked ETH)
- *15,000 cmETH* (Mantle Staked ETH)
- *8,000 mETH* (Mantle ETH)

Bybit CEO Ben Zhou confirmed the exchange remained solvent and that all client assets were backed 1:1, with the exchange securing bridge loans to cover the shortfall.

## Timeline

- **February 21, 2025, ~02:16 PM UTC:** The [malicious transaction was executed](https://etherscan.io/tx/0x46deef0f52e3a983b67abf4714448a41dd7ffd6d32d32da69d62081c74571b71), transferring approximately 401,347 ETH and staking derivatives from Bybit's cold wallet to an attacker-controlled address.
- **February 21, 2025, ~02:30 PM UTC:** On-chain analyst ZachXBT [alerted the community](https://x.com/zachxbt/status/1892885002767573428) about suspicious outflows from Bybit.
- **February 21, 2025, ~03:00 PM UTC:** Bybit CEO Ben Zhou [confirmed the hack](https://x.com/benaborise/status/1892892454597394929) and stated that only one cold wallet was compromised.
- **February 21, 2025:** The attacker began [rapidly splitting funds](https://etherscan.io/address/0x47666Fab8bd0Ac7003bce3f5C3585383F09486E2) across multiple intermediary wallets.
- **February 22, 2025:** Bybit announced a [recovery bounty program](https://www.bybit.com/en/announcement/bybit-war-room-bounter-program-blt0e28bf3c8a76da9a/) offering 10% of recovered funds to anyone assisting in the recovery.
- **February 26, 2025:** The FBI [officially attributed](https://www.fbi.gov/news/press-releases/fbi-identifies-lazarus-group-cyber-actors-as-responsible-for-theft-of-1.5-billion-from-bybit) the attack to North Korea's Lazarus Group.
- **March 2025:** Bybit confirmed it had [fully replaced](https://www.bybit.com/en/announcement/) the stolen ETH through loans and deposits, restoring full backing of client assets.

## Security Failure Causes

- **Supply Chain Attack on Safe{Wallet}:** The attacker compromised the Safe{Wallet} web application infrastructure rather than Bybit's internal systems directly. The malicious JavaScript code was injected into the Safe UI to display a legitimate-looking transaction while the actual on-chain transaction contained a delegatecall to a malicious contract.
- **Blind Signing Vulnerability:** The multisig signers approved a transaction that appeared legitimate on the Safe{Wallet} UI but contained manipulated calldata. Hardware wallet displays may not have shown the full details of the delegatecall payload.
- **Insufficient Transaction Verification:** The routine nature of the cold-to-warm wallet transfer may have reduced the scrutiny applied to the transaction verification process.
- **Single Point of Failure in UI:** Relying on a single web interface (Safe{Wallet}) for transaction construction created a single point of compromise that bypassed the security benefits of multisig architecture.
