## Summary

On February 11-12, 2025, Starknet lending platform zkLend was exploited for approximately $9.57 million through what the protocol described as an empty-market exploit against its newly launched wstETH market. According to zkLend and BlockSec, the exploit combined an empty-market setup, flash-loan over-repayments that were treated as protocol revenue, and rounding/truncation effects in withdrawal accounting. After the market's `lending_accumulator` had been pushed to an extreme value, the attacker repeatedly used deposit-and-withdraw loops in which raw share balances were reduced too slowly relative to face-value withdrawals, inflating effective collateral value and enabling borrowing of other assets from the platform.

zkLend's post-mortem reported aggregate losses of 2213.638314603870948591 ETH, 1,553,069.487238 USDC, 7,426,029.991485769316032807 STRK, and 518,225.710536 USDT, worth about $9,572,151 at the time of the exploit. The protocol suspended borrowing and then all market operations after the exploit was confirmed.

## Losses

zkLend's official post-mortem reported total losses of approximately $9,572,151, consisting of:

- *2213.638314603870948591 ETH*
- *1,553,069.487238 USDC*
- *7,426,029.991485769316032807 STRK*
- *518,225.710536 USDT*

zkLend said the attacker extracted these assets by inflating collateral balance and then borrowing funds from the system. The stolen assets were subsequently bridged from Starknet through StarkGate, Orbiter, LayerSwap, and rhino.fi.

## Security Failure Causes

**Empty Market Initialization:** zkLend's post-mortem said the exploit targeted a newly launched wstETH market whose first tiny deposit established a baseline that could later be abused.

**Flash-Loan Donation Mechanism:** According to zkLend and BlockSec, the flash-loan logic treated over-repayment as additional revenue. In an empty market, those donations could increase the global `lending_accumulator` far beyond normal assumptions.

**Rounding During Withdrawal Accounting:** Once the accumulator had been artificially inflated, floor-division behavior in withdrawal accounting allowed the attacker to reduce the raw share balance too slowly relative to the face-value amount being withdrawn. zkLend and BlockSec both identified this rounding behavior as the critical second stage of the exploit.
