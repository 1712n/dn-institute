## Summary

On June 18, 2025, Iranian cryptocurrency exchange Nobitex [reported unauthorized access to part of its infrastructure and hot wallet](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/18/hackers-steal-and-destroy-millions-from-irans-largest-crypto-exchange/). Chainalysis said the exploit removed more than $90 million across Bitcoin, Ethereum, Dogecoin, Ripple, Solana, Tron and Ton, and identified Nobitex as the largest digital asset exchange in Iran. The pro-Israel group Gonjeshke Darande, also known as Predatory Sparrow, claimed responsibility and framed the attack as political rather than financially motivated. Chainalysis and Elliptic both reported that the attacker-controlled wallets were burner or inaccessible vanity addresses, which means the stolen funds were effectively destroyed rather than laundered.

## Attackers

Gonjeshke Darande claimed responsibility for the attack, but there was no public official confirmation of the group's operators or nationality. The group had also claimed an attack against Iran's state-owned Bank Sepah one day earlier. Elliptic and several news organizations described the Nobitex incident as aligned with the June 2025 Israel-Iran conflict, while Chainalysis noted that the use of burner addresses without private key access was consistent with political motivation rather than ordinary theft.

## Losses

The confirmed public estimates ranged from more than $80 million in early on-chain reporting to more than $90 million in later blockchain analytics reports. Chainalysis listed the affected assets as Bitcoin, Ethereum, Dogecoin, Ripple, Solana, Tron and Ton. The Block reported that early suspicious outflows were first traced on Tron and EVM-compatible chains before later estimates increased. CNBC, citing Elliptic and Chainalysis, reported that the assets were routed to vanity addresses the attackers likely could not spend from, making recovery through normal seizure or exchange-freeze routes unlikely.

## Timeline

- **June 17, 2025:** Gonjeshke Darande claimed a destructive cyberattack against Bank Sepah, a state-owned Iranian bank, one day before the Nobitex exchange incident was reported.
- **June 18, 2025, early UTC:** On-chain investigator ZachXBT identified suspicious Nobitex outflows, first estimating roughly $48.65 million on Tron and then increasing the estimate above $80 million as additional Tron and EVM-chain activity was found.
- **June 18, 2025:** Nobitex notified users that its technical team found unauthorized access affecting part of its internal infrastructure and hot wallet, suspended access, and began an internal security investigation.
- **June 18, 2025:** Elliptic reported that more than $90 million had moved from Nobitex wallets to hacker-controlled addresses containing anti-IRGC text, and said the addresses appeared to be inaccessible vanity addresses.
- **June 18, 2025:** Chainalysis published its analysis of the exploit, stating that the stolen assets spanned multiple cryptocurrencies and that the attacker-controlled wallets appeared to be burner addresses without private key access.
- **June 19, 2025:** The Block reported that Gonjeshke Darande claimed to have leaked Nobitex source-code material and warned that remaining platform assets were exposed.

## Security Failure Causes

**Hot wallet key compromise:** Halborn assessed that the attack was most likely enabled by compromised private keys stored on systems the attackers accessed. Because hot wallets must remain online enough to process withdrawals, poor key isolation or insufficient signing controls can turn an infrastructure intrusion into direct asset loss.

**Insufficient blast-radius controls:** Nobitex said the incident affected only part of its hot wallet assets and that cold storage remained secure. The size of the observed drain still shows that the online-wallet tier held enough assets, across enough chains, to create a loss above $90 million before access was suspended.

**Off-chain infrastructure exposure:** The attack did not depend on a public smart-contract bug. The reported compromise involved exchange infrastructure, hot wallets, and later alleged source-code leakage. That combination increased the incident from a wallet drain into a broader platform security failure because exposed deployment, privacy, or interface code could aid follow-on attackers.

**Geopolitical targeting risk:** The stolen funds were routed to apparent burn addresses instead of laundering paths. This made the event different from normal profit-seeking exchange thefts and shows that exchanges operating in sanctioned or conflict-linked environments can face destructive attacks where the attacker's goal is disruption, data exposure, or political messaging rather than monetization.
